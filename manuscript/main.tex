\begin{abstract}
We present a geometry-informed neural atlas framework for solving boundary-value problems on complex three-dimensional bodies without volumetric meshing. The central idea is to decompose the geometry into overlapping volumetric charts, pull each governing equation back to simple chart-local coordinates through the chart Jacobian, and couple chart-local neural solvers by a Schwarz alternating update. This atlas-first construction is the main technical innovation of the paper: it separates geometric representation from PDE approximation, reduces distortion relative to a single global parametrization, and yields a training workflow that can start either from analytic geometry or from mesh-derived signed-distance data. The validated benchmark set shows that the method is already effective for forward field solves on complex geometry, with the fixed-atlas rabbit Poisson example serving as the strongest evidence, while the Stanford Bunny signed-distance benchmark demonstrates a usable geometry-learning bridge from real surface data. The torus Neo-Hookean case further shows that the same infrastructure can support controlled inverse identification when the kinematics are prescribed, and a companion elastoplastic benchmark demonstrates that the chart-local finite-element solver extends to history-dependent constitutive laws: a smooth return mapping with softplus regularisation enables gradient-based identification of the yield stress ($0.25\%$ error) and kinematic hardening modulus ($2.11\%$ error) from cyclic loading data. The resulting picture is computationally useful and intentionally honest: the atlas-based SA-PINN pipeline is mature enough to support geometry-intensive forward computation today, and its current failure modes are concrete enough to guide the next round of methodological improvement.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction} \label{sec:introduction}
Classical numerical solution of PDEs on three-dimensional bodies with intricate geometry still depends heavily on volumetric meshing. For many of the geometries considered in this work, such as a Stanford rabbit interior or a torus with overlapping chart parametrizations, mesh generation and mesh quality control are substantial tasks in their own right. A chart-based meshfree alternative is attractive because it separates geometry handling from local PDE approximation: the geometry is carried by chart maps, while the PDE is enforced in chart-local reference coordinates.

Our computational viewpoint sits at the intersection of two mature ideas. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) provide a meshfree route for solving forward and inverse PDE problems \citep{raissi2019physicsinformed}, while Schwarz-type overlapping domain decomposition provides a classical way to couple local subproblems on overlapping patches \citep{quarteroni1999domain}. The atlas-based Schwarz-alternating PINN (SA-PINN) formulation developed here combines these ideas, although the current repository realizes this full picture more clearly in some benchmarks than in others.

The basic idea is geometric divide-and-conquer. Instead of insisting on a single global volumetric parametrization, we represent the physical domain by an atlas of overlapping volumetric charts. Each chart supplies a local change of variables from a simple reference patch to a physical subdomain. Local neural surrogates are trained in those chart coordinates, and a Schwarz alternating procedure communicates boundary and overlap information between neighboring charts. In the current code release this strategy is realized most fully for the rabbit Poisson benchmark, where chart-local field solves, interface penalties, and alternating updates are all active.

The codebase also contains inverse examples, but they are not all of the same type. Some are controlled synthetic identification benchmarks designed to isolate parameter recovery in a simpler setting, while other more ambitious inverse PDE tracks remain under active development and are therefore left outside the main manuscript. This distinction matters mathematically and should be explicit in the paper. In particular, the torus Neo-Hookean example retained here identifies elastic material parameters from synthetic observations generated by prescribed torsional kinematics. A companion elastoplastic benchmark extends the same chart-local finite-element infrastructure to history-dependent plasticity, using a smooth return mapping with softplus regularisation to enable gradient-based identification of yield stress and kinematic hardening parameters from cyclic loading data.

The paper is organized accordingly. Section~\ref{sec:methodology} introduces the volumetric atlas, the mapped PDE operator, the special-case global-map training used only for simply connected examples, and the SA-PINN Schwarz update. Section~\ref{sec:implementation} then translates those ideas into the concrete geometry pipeline, quality-assurance gates, and training heuristics used in the code. Section~\ref{sec:benchmark_formulations} presents the benchmark-specific formulations together with the corresponding validated numerical results. Section~\ref{sec:discussion} then interprets those results, records the current boundaries of the method, and outlines the most important development priorities. This organization keeps the general framework separate from implementation choices and from benchmark-specific simplifications while still letting the reader see, example by example, how each model formulation performs in practice.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

Before giving the derivations, we fix the notation used in this section. We use $x$ for a point in the physical body, $\boldsymbol{\zeta}$ for a chart-local reference coordinate on one atlas patch, and $\boldsymbol{\xi}$ for the global reference-ball coordinate used only in the special simply connected examples. This two-level notation is enough for the methodology while avoiding the earlier conflict in which one symbol was asked to represent both a local chart coordinate and a global reference coordinate.

\subsection{Volumetric atlas of coordinate charts}
\label{sec:atlas_geometry}
Let $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ be a bounded body with sufficiently regular boundary $\partial\Omega$. In the main method used throughout this paper, $\Omega$ is represented by an atlas of \emph{volumetric} charts
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{A}=\{(\widehat{\Omega}_i,\varphi_i)\}_{i=1}^{M},
\qquad
\widehat{\Omega}_i \subset \mathbb{R}^3,
\qquad
\varphi_i:\widehat{\Omega}_i \rightarrow \mathbb{R}^3,
\label{eq:atlas_def}
\end{equation}
with physical patches
\begin{equation}
\Omega_i := \varphi_i(\widehat{\Omega}_i),
\qquad
\overline{\Omega}\subset \bigcup_{i=1}^{M}\overline{\Omega}_i.
\label{eq:atlas_cover}
\end{equation}
The Jacobian matrix of chart $i$ is denoted by
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}) := D_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}} \varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\in\mathbb{R}^{3\times 3},
\qquad
j_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}):=\det \mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}).
\label{eq:atlas_jac}
\end{equation}

For the mapped PDE derivations below, we assume that each chart is locally nondegenerate:
\begin{equation}
j_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\ge j_{\min}>0
\qquad \text{for all sampled }\boldsymbol{\zeta}\in \widehat{\Omega}_i.
\label{eq:atlas_nondegenerate}
\end{equation}
This condition guarantees a locally invertible change of variables. It does \emph{not} by itself imply global injectivity. Whenever we refer to a chart inverse
\begin{equation}
\pi_i := \varphi_i^{-1}:\Omega_i\rightarrow \widehat{\Omega}_i,
\label{eq:atlas_inverse}
\end{equation}
we mean the inverse on a patch where the chart is assumed or verified to be injective. In the implementation, positive Jacobian determinant is enforced or monitored at sampled points, while global injectivity is treated as a trained geometric property rather than a theorem following automatically from \eqref{eq:atlas_nondegenerate}.

On overlaps we define
\begin{equation}
\Omega_{ij}:=\Omega_i\cap \Omega_j,
\qquad
\widehat{\Omega}_{ij}:=\pi_i(\Omega_{ij})\subset \widehat{\Omega}_i,
\label{eq:atlas_overlap}
\end{equation}
and the transition map
\begin{equation}
\psi_{ij}:=\pi_j\circ \varphi_i:\widehat{\Omega}_{ij}\rightarrow \widehat{\Omega}_j,
\qquad
\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})=\varphi_j(\psi_{ij}(\boldsymbol{\zeta})).
\label{eq:atlas_transition}
\end{equation}
When a single global field is needed for post-processing, we use partition-of-unity weights $\{\omega_i\}_{i=1}^{M}$ satisfying
\begin{equation}
\omega_i:\Omega\rightarrow [0,1],
\qquad
\mathrm{supp}(\omega_i)\subset \Omega_i,
\qquad
\sum_{i=1}^{M}\omega_i(x)=1.
\label{eq:atlas_pou}
\end{equation}
For any chart-local quantity $\widehat{g}_i$, the blended global field is
\begin{equation}
g_h(x)=\sum_{i=1}^{M}\omega_i(x)\,\widehat{g}_i(\pi_i(x)).
\label{eq:atlas_blend}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
For implementation, it is enough to think of each chart as a small local coordinate box that covers one part of the body. We solve the PDE chart by chart in these simple local coordinates, and we use the overlap regions only to pass information between neighboring charts. The rabbit Poisson solver is the clearest validated example of this volumetric-chart viewpoint in the present manuscript. Surface-only parameterizations and more ambitious inverse variants appear elsewhere in the repository, but they are not the PDE setting studied here.

\subsection{Mapped PDE operator on a volumetric chart}
\label{sec:mapped_operator}
Let $u:\Omega\rightarrow\mathbb{R}^{q}$ be the physical state. For vector-valued fields we collect component gradients row-wise, so
\[
\nabla_x u(x)\in \mathbb{R}^{q\times 3}.
\]
We write the physical PDE in divergence form as
\begin{equation}
\nabla_x\cdot \mathcal{G}\!\left(x,u(x),\nabla_x u(x);\eta\right)
+ \mathcal{S}\!\left(x,u(x),\nabla_x u(x);\eta\right)=0
\qquad \text{in }\Omega,
\label{eq:abstract_pde}
\end{equation}
where the divergence acts row-wise and
\[
\mathcal{G}(x,u,\nabla u;\eta)\in \mathbb{R}^{q\times 3},
\qquad
\mathcal{S}(x,u,\nabla u;\eta)\in \mathbb{R}^{q}.
\]
Physical boundary conditions are written as
\begin{equation}
u=u_D \quad \text{on }\Gamma_D,
\qquad
\mathcal{G}(x,u,\nabla_x u;\eta)\,n = t_N
\quad \text{on }\Gamma_N,
\label{eq:abstract_bc}
\end{equation}
with outward unit normal $n$.

On chart $i$, let $\boldsymbol{\zeta}\in\widehat{\Omega}_i$ denote the local reference coordinate and define the pulled-back state by
\begin{equation}
\widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}):=u(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})).
\label{eq:pullback_state}
\end{equation}
Because gradients are stored row-wise, the chain rule takes the form
\begin{equation}
\nabla_x u(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}))
=
\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}} \widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\,\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})^{-1}.
\label{eq:grad_pullback}
\end{equation}
The physical flux is first evaluated on the chart and then converted to a reference-space flux by the row-wise Piola transform:
\begin{align}
\widehat{\mathcal{G}}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\eta)
&:=
\mathcal{G}\!\left(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}),\widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}),\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}} \widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})^{-1};\eta\right),
\label{eq:pullback_flux}\\
\widehat{\mathcal{P}}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\eta)
&:=
j_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\,\widehat{\mathcal{G}}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\eta)\,\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})^{-T}.
\label{eq:piola_flux}
\end{align}
The pulled-back source term is
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{S}}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\eta)
:=
\mathcal{S}\!\left(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}),\widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}),\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}} \widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})^{-1};\eta\right).
\label{eq:pullback_source}
\end{equation}
We reserve the notation $\widehat{\mathcal{P}}_i$ for the Piola-transformed flux entering the reference-space divergence and $\widehat{\mathcal{S}}_i$ for the pulled-back source term. No auxiliary symbol such as $\delta$ is used for the mapped source, so the flux and source roles remain distinct throughout the derivation.
With these definitions, the mapped residual on the reference chart is
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\eta)
:=
\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}}\cdot \widehat{\mathcal{P}}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\eta)
+
j_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\,\widehat{\mathcal{S}}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\eta),
\qquad \boldsymbol{\zeta}\in \widehat{\Omega}_i.
\label{eq:mapped_residual}
\end{equation}

For the Dirichlet boundary we use
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{B}}_{i,D}(\boldsymbol{\zeta}):=\widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})-u_D(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})),
\qquad \boldsymbol{\zeta}\in \widehat{\Gamma}_{i,D}:=\pi_i(\Gamma_D\cap \Omega_i).
\label{eq:mapped_dirichlet}
\end{equation}
For Neumann or traction conditions, if $\widehat{n}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})$ is a reference outward normal on the relevant portion of $\partial\widehat{\Omega}_i$, the physical normal is
\begin{equation}
n_i(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}))
=
\frac{\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})^{-T}\widehat{n}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})}
{\|\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})^{-T}\widehat{n}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\|},
\label{eq:mapped_normal}
\end{equation}
and the pulled-back Neumann residual is
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{B}}_{i,N}(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\eta)
:=
\widehat{\mathcal{G}}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\eta)\,n_i(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})) - t_N(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})).
\label{eq:mapped_neumann}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
Equation \eqref{eq:mapped_residual} is the practical rule used inside every chart. In code, we sample points in the easy reference patch, evaluate the neural network and its derivatives there, and then use the Jacobian of the chart map to recover the physical PDE. This is the main computational benefit of the atlas: geometry stays in the map, while the training points stay in simple local coordinates.

\subsection{Special-case global map training for simply connected domains}
\label{sec:global_map_training}
Some examples in the codebase use a single global map
\begin{equation}
\Psi_\theta:B^3\rightarrow \Omega,
\qquad
B^3=\{\boldsymbol{\xi}\in\mathbb{R}^3:\|\boldsymbol{\xi}\|<1\},
\label{eq:global_map}
\end{equation}
instead of a fixed atlas. This special case is used only for simply connected domains where a single volumetric map is feasible. In the validated benchmark set, the ellipsoid uses an analytic global map. The rabbit, Bunny, and torus benchmarks do not use this global-map training stage.

When $\Psi_\theta$ is learned, the implementation combines a positive-determinant barrier, boundary-fidelity terms, and coefficient-regularity terms. A compact way to write the training objective is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{map}}
=
\alpha_{\mathrm{bij}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bij}}
+
\alpha_{\mathrm{geom}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{geom}}
+
\alpha_{\mathrm{coeff}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{coeff}},
\label{eq:global_map_loss}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bij}}$ penalizes small or negative Jacobian determinant, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{geom}}$ keeps the boundary on the target geometry and the interior inside the domain, and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{coeff}}$ regularizes the mapped operator coefficients induced by the chart Jacobian. In the code this loss is specialized further into bijectivity, ellipticity, isotropy, smoothness, and boundary terms.

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
This global-map stage is a special case, not the default workflow of the paper. It is useful when one smooth map from the unit ball to the domain is available or can be learned reliably. For the more complicated rabbit and torus examples, we do not rely on one global map; we use a fixed overlapping atlas instead.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Special-case training of a learned global volumetric map}
\label{alg:global_map_training}
\small
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Statex \textbf{Inputs:} reference ball $B^3$; target-domain surface and interior samples; initial map parameters $\theta$;
\StateX optimizer $\mathcal{O}$ with learning-rate schedule $\eta_{\mathrm{map}}^{(t)}$; loss weights $\alpha_{\mathrm{bij}},\alpha_{\mathrm{geom}},\alpha_{\mathrm{coeff}}$; epoch budget $T_{\mathrm{map}}$
\Statex \textbf{Output:} best checkpoint map $\Psi_\theta:B^3\rightarrow\Omega$ satisfying determinant and boundary checks on validation samples
\For{$t=1,\dots,T_{\mathrm{map}}$}
    \State Sample interior points $\boldsymbol{\xi}^{\mathrm{int}}\subset B^3$ and boundary points $\boldsymbol{\xi}^{\partial}\subset \partial B^3$
    \State Evaluate $x^{\mathrm{int}}=\Psi_\theta(\boldsymbol{\xi}^{\mathrm{int}})$, $x^{\partial}=\Psi_\theta(\boldsymbol{\xi}^{\partial})$, and $\mathbf{J}=D_{\boldsymbol{\xi}}\Psi_\theta$
    \State Assemble $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bij}}$, $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{geom}}$, and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{coeff}}$ from \eqref{eq:global_map_loss}
    \State Form $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{map}}=\alpha_{\mathrm{bij}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{bij}}+\alpha_{\mathrm{geom}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{geom}}+\alpha_{\mathrm{coeff}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{coeff}}$
    \State Compute the mapping-loss gradient $g_{\theta}^{(t)}=\nabla_{\theta}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{map}}(\theta^{(t)})$
    \State Update the parameters with the chosen optimizer:
    \Statex \hspace{\algorithmicindent}$\theta^{(t+1)} \leftarrow \mathrm{Update}_{\mathcal{O}}\!\left(\theta^{(t)},g_{\theta}^{(t)};\eta_{\mathrm{map}}^{(t)}\right)$
    \State Record $\min_{\boldsymbol{\xi}}\det \mathbf{J}(\boldsymbol{\xi})$, boundary mismatch, and coefficient diagnostics for checkpointing
    \State Keep the best checkpoint that preserves positive determinant and acceptable boundary fidelity on the validation batch
\EndFor
\State \Return the selected global map $\Psi_\theta$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:global_map_training} records the training pattern used by the special global-map pipeline. The ellipsoid verification case uses the same mapped-PDE machinery afterward, but its global map is analytic rather than learned.

\subsection{Schwarz alternating PINN on a fixed atlas}
\label{sec:schwarz_pinn}
Once the geometry has been represented by a fixed overlapping atlas, the remaining question is how the chart-local solves should be coupled into one physically meaningful global field. Classical overlapping Schwarz is a natural answer because the atlas already supplies the ingredients that Schwarz needs: local subproblems posed on overlapping subdomains, together with artificial interfaces across which neighboring information can be exchanged \citep{quarteroni1999domain,tezaur2025hybrid}. In the present work this is not merely an optimization convenience. The decomposition is geometry-driven: the charts are introduced first to control geometric distortion and represent the body, and the Schwarz iteration is then used as the coupling layer that reconciles those local coordinate solves. This is a different role from domain-decomposed PINN frameworks such as XPINNs or FBPINNs, where the partition is introduced primarily for scalability or spectral resolution rather than as a pre-existing volumetric atlas \citep{shukla2021parallel,moseley2021fbpinns}.

The attraction of Schwarz for coordinate-chart PINNs is that each chart already carries a self-contained local residual minimization problem on a simple reference patch. The overlap then acts as an artificial boundary: while chart $i$ is being updated, the most recent neighbor states supply temporary interface data, and the local optimizer only has to resolve one chart-conditioned problem at a time. Relative to a monolithic global PINN, this localizes approximation, conditioning, and sampling. The cost is that compatibility across charts must be enforced explicitly on the overlaps rather than being hidden inside one global parameterization.

Let
\begin{equation}
\widehat{u}_i(\cdot;\theta_i):\widehat{\Omega}_i\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^{q}
\label{eq:local_network}
\end{equation}
be the state network on chart $i$, and define the local physical representation
\begin{equation}
u_i(x;\theta_i):=\widehat{u}_i(\pi_i(x);\theta_i),
\qquad x\in\Omega_i.
\label{eq:local_physical_field}
\end{equation}
For overlaps, the artificial Schwarz boundary of chart $i$ is
\begin{equation}
\Gamma_i:=\partial\Omega_i\setminus \partial\Omega,
\qquad
\Gamma_{ij}:=\Gamma_i\cap \Omega_j,
\qquad
\mathcal{N}(i):=\{j\neq i:\Gamma_{ij}\neq\emptyset\}.
\label{eq:schwarz_sets}
\end{equation}
The sets $\Gamma_{ij}$ play the same conceptual role as Schwarz interfaces in classical overlapping domain decomposition: they are not physical boundaries of the PDE, but the internal regions where chart-local solutions must agree strongly enough to define a coherent global field. In the chart setting the same geometric overlap also provides the transition map $\psi_{ij}$ needed to compare chart-local predictions in compatible coordinates.

In the continuous Schwarz method one would impose Dirichlet, Neumann, or Robin data on these artificial interfaces and solve each local problem to completion before moving to the next subdomain. The rabbit code uses a PINN-compatible relaxation of this idea. Neighbor networks are frozen while chart $i$ is updated, and compatibility is enforced by sampled penalties on the overlap rather than by exact boundary imposition. Writing $\theta_j^{(k,\mathrm{fr})}$ for the frozen neighbor state available during sweep $k$, the interface-value term is
\emph{sampled from the neighboring local field $u_j$ itself, not from the global blended field $u_h$}. This point is important for reproducibility: Schwarz transmission in the released solver is chart-to-chart, with the global blend reserved for diagnostics and post-processing after a sweep has been proposed.
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{J}_{i,\mathrm{iv}}^{(k)}
=
\sum_{j\in \mathcal{N}(i)}
\mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}\sim \widehat{\Gamma}_{ij}}
\left[
\left|
\widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\theta_i)
-
\widehat{u}_j\!\left(\psi_{ij}(\boldsymbol{\zeta});\theta_j^{(k,\mathrm{fr})}\right)
\right|^2
\right].
\label{eq:schwarz_iv_loss}
\end{equation}
For scalar Poisson problems the default validated configuration also penalizes projected normal-flux mismatch. With $n_{ij}$ denoting the interface normal used for transmission, define
\begin{equation}
q_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\theta_i)
:=
n_{ij}(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\cdot
\nabla_x u_i\!\left(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta});\theta_i\right),
\label{eq:schwarz_flux_trace}
\end{equation}
and
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{J}_{i,\mathrm{if}}^{(k)}
=
\sum_{j\in \mathcal{N}(i)}
\mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}\sim \widehat{\Gamma}_{ij}}
\left[
\left|
q_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\theta_i)
-
q_j\!\left(\psi_{ij}(\boldsymbol{\zeta});\theta_j^{(k,\mathrm{fr})}\right)
\right|^2
\right].
\label{eq:schwarz_if_loss}
\end{equation}
The released solver also exposes a Robin-style variant,
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{J}_{i,\mathrm{rb}}^{(k)}
=
\sum_{j\in \mathcal{N}(i)}
\mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}\sim \widehat{\Gamma}_{ij}}
\left[
\left|
\lambda_R
\left(
\widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\theta_i)
-
\widehat{u}_j\!\left(\psi_{ij}(\boldsymbol{\zeta});\theta_j^{(k,\mathrm{fr})}\right)
\right)
+
q_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\theta_i)
-
q_j\!\left(\psi_{ij}(\boldsymbol{\zeta});\theta_j^{(k,\mathrm{fr})}\right)
\right|^2
\right],
\label{eq:schwarz_robin_loss}
\end{equation}
but the validated rabbit benchmark uses the penalty form \eqref{eq:schwarz_iv_loss}--\eqref{eq:schwarz_if_loss} rather than the Robin option. In volumetric-atlas mode the code can further add an overlap-interior value penalty on sampled points from $\widehat{\Omega}_{ij}$; despite the legacy variable name \texttt{overlap\_h1}, the current implementation penalizes value mismatch over the sampled overlap volume rather than the full $H^1$ seminorm.

At Schwarz sweep $k$, the local chart objective is therefore
\begin{align}
\mathcal{J}_i^{(k)}(\theta_i;\eta)
=&
\lambda_{\mathrm{pde}}\,
\mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}\sim \widehat{\Omega}_i}\!\left[\|\widehat{\mathcal{R}}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\theta_i,\eta)\|_2^2\right]
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{bc}}\,
\mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}\sim \widehat{\Gamma}_{i,\partial\Omega}}\!\left[\|\widehat{\mathcal{B}}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta};\theta_i,\eta)\|_2^2\right]
\nonumber\\
&+
\lambda_{\mathrm{iv}}\,\mathcal{J}_{i,\mathrm{iv}}^{(k)}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{if}}\,\widetilde{\mathcal{J}}_{i,\mathrm{if}}^{(k)}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{ov}}\,\mathcal{J}_{i,\mathrm{ov}}^{(k)}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{data}}\,\mathcal{J}_{i,\mathrm{data}}(\theta_i,\eta),
\label{eq:schwarz_local_loss}
\end{align}
where $\widetilde{\mathcal{J}}_{i,\mathrm{if}}^{(k)}$ denotes either the projected-flux penalty \eqref{eq:schwarz_if_loss} or the Robin residual \eqref{eq:schwarz_robin_loss}, and $\mathcal{J}_{i,\mathrm{ov}}^{(k)}$ denotes the optional volumetric overlap penalty. The practical default is the projected-flux penalty with frozen-neighbor traces. This distinction matters: the present solver is Schwarz-inspired in its transmission logic, but each chart update is still carried out by optimizer steps on a neural loss, not by an exact local PDE solve.

One sweep of the atlas solver can be written abstractly as
\begin{equation}
\Theta^{(k+1)}=\mathfrak{S}\!\left(\Theta^{(k)};\eta\right),
\qquad
\Theta^{(k)}:=(\theta_1^{(k)},\dots,\theta_M^{(k)}),
\label{eq:schwarz_sweep}
\end{equation}
where $\mathfrak{S}$ denotes one multiplicative Schwarz pass over the chart set. In the validated rabbit solver this sweep is multiplicative at the level of overlapping neighbors: later charts in the sweep see the most recent accepted states of earlier charts, while non-overlapping charts may be grouped into the same graph color and updated concurrently on compatible accelerator backends without violating the frozen-neighbor assumption on overlap pairs. This is why the released implementation is best described as a multiplicative Schwarz method with limited within-color parallelism, not as a fully additive update.
The color groups used in Algorithm~\ref{alg:atlas_sapinn_training} are derived from a graph-coloring of the chart-overlap adjacency matrix: two charts may share a color only if they do not overlap, which makes within-color updates independent for the purposes of the frozen-neighbor Schwarz step.

After an accepted sweep, the global blended field is
\begin{equation}
u_h^{(k)}(x)=\sum_{i=1}^{M}\omega_i(x)\,u_i(x;\theta_i^{(k)}).
\label{eq:schwarz_global_field}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
For a reader used to standard PINNs, the important point is that Schwarz is doing geometric bookkeeping as much as numerical coupling. The atlas already decomposes the body into overlapping coordinate patches. Schwarz then turns those overlaps into a training protocol: solve one local chart-conditioned problem, compare it against frozen neighbor predictions on the overlap, move to the next chart, and only then assess the global blended state. This fits the coordinate-chart setting better than a monolithic global PINN because the decomposition is dictated by geometry rather than by an arbitrary partition of the loss.

\centering

\caption{Conceptual and algorithmic view of fixed-atlas SA-PINN coupling. Panel (a) shows two overlapping chart images inside the physical body. The overlap $\Omega_{ij}$ induces the artificial interfaces $\Gamma_{ij}$ and $\Gamma_{ji}$ on which neighboring chart solutions exchange temporary transmission data during training. Panel (b) shows one multiplicative Schwarz sweep as implemented in the rabbit solver: the code snapshots the full chart state, updates one active chart or non-overlapping color group while neighbor states are detached, evaluates global residual and interface diagnostics, and then accepts the whole sweep or restores the previous state.}

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Fixed-atlas SA-PINN training by multiplicative Schwarz sweeps}
\label{alg:atlas_sapinn_training}
\small
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Statex \textbf{Inputs:} validated atlas $\mathcal{A}$; chart-local networks $\{\widehat{u}_i(\cdot;\theta_i)\}_{i=1}^M$;
\StateX optional global parameters $\eta$; local optimizers; sweep budget $K$; local-step budget $N_{\mathrm{loc}}$
\Statex \textbf{Outputs:} selected checkpoint $\{\theta_i\}_{i=1}^M$ and blended field $u_h$
\Statex \textit{Initialization}
\State Initialize chart parameters $\theta_i$ and any trainable global parameters $\eta$
\State Evaluate the initial blended field and initialize checkpoint metrics
\For{$k=0,\dots,K-1$}
    \Statex \textit{Pre-sweep setup}
    \State Store a snapshot of the full chart state $\Theta^{(k)}$
    \State Refresh sweep-level sampling pools and effective interface weights
        \For{each color group $g$ from the graph-coloring of the chart-overlap adjacency matrix}
        \Statex \textit{Local chart updates}
        \For{each active chart $i\in g$ sequentially, or concurrently when charts in $g$ do not overlap}
            \For{$n=1,\dots,N_{\mathrm{loc}}$}
                \State Sample PDE, physical-boundary, interface, and optional supervision points on chart $i$
                \State Query neighbors only through frozen predictions on sampled overlap points
                \State Assemble the local loss $\mathcal{J}_i^{(k)}$ in \eqref{eq:schwarz_local_loss}
                \State Update $\theta_i$ while all neighbor contributions remain detached
            \EndFor
        \EndFor
    \EndFor
    \Statex \textit{Global acceptance test}
    \State Form the proposed blended field by \eqref{eq:schwarz_global_field}
    \State Evaluate global PDE, boundary, interface, and relative-$L^2$ diagnostics
    \If{the sweep violates the trust-region admissibility test}
        \State Restore the pre-sweep snapshot $\Theta^{(k)}$ and reduce the learning rate
    \Else
        \State Accept the sweep and refresh checkpoint statistics
        \State Update adaptive weights if enabled
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\State \Return the selected checkpoint and its blended field $u_h$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:atlas_sapinn_training} is the core training loop behind the fixed-atlas rabbit Poisson benchmark. Two implementation details are worth stressing because they justify the multiplicative choice. First, the accepted state is decided at the sweep level, not chart by chart: the code rolls back the entire proposed sweep if the global diagnostics deteriorate too much. Second, optional parallelism is restricted to non-overlapping color groups constructed from the overlap graph, so the validated solver still behaves like multiplicative Schwarz on every true overlap pair. Related inverse prototypes reuse the same coupling pattern, but the main text keeps the discussion focused on the forward rabbit solve where the interaction between geometry, overlap transmission, and optimizer stability is easiest to interpret.



\section{Implementation}
\label{sec:implementation}

This section records how the methodology of Section~\ref{sec:methodology} is realized in code. The goal is not to list every engineering detail in the repository, but to document the implementation choices that materially affect robustness, reproducibility, and the interpretation of the validated benchmarks.

\subsection{Human-led research loop and implementation assistance}
\label{sec:impl_aiassist}

The scientific logic of the project remained human-led throughout. The benchmark family, the atlas-based decomposition, the mapped-PDE formulation, the acceptance criteria for validated runs, and the interpretation of success or failure were set by the author. Large-language-model tooling was used as a low-level implementation assistant for code scaffolding, refactoring, debugging, figure scripting, and artifact packaging. In other words, the assistant accelerated software iteration, but it did not decide the mathematical model, the benchmark scope, or the evidentiary standard required for the manuscript.

The AI-assisted lane proposes candidate implementations and supporting scripts. These candidates are then filtered through geometry gates, benchmark runs, and manuscript-level review. Only implementations that survive this acceptance loop enter the validated benchmark set reported in Section~\ref{sec:benchmark_formulations}.

\subsection{Software stack and numerical separation of responsibilities}
\label{sec:impl_stack}

All neural networks are implemented in PyTorch \citep{paszke2019pytorch}. Automatic differentiation is used both for PDE residuals and for the Jacobians needed by the chart maps and constitutive models. Jacobian inversion is carried out in the forward pass with \texttt{torch.linalg.inv}, while condition diagnostics are evaluated under \texttt{torch.no\_grad()} to avoid unnecessary instability near nearly singular samples. The framework is implemented in a hardware-agnostic PyTorch environment capable of acceleration on Metal, CUDA, and CPU backends, and the validated runs reported here were executed through the same code path used for development.

A guiding implementation principle is the separation of geometry from PDE solving. Geometry processing produces an SDF, a volumetric atlas, and a decoder/mask representation that are frozen before the field solver is trained. This separation is what makes the numerical evidence interpretable: if a benchmark fails after the geometry gate has passed, the failure can be attributed to the PDE solver or the coupling logic rather than to a moving geometry target.

Supporting geometry utilities use \texttt{open3d}, \texttt{pysdf}, \texttt{mesh\_to\_sdf}, and \texttt{trimesh}; post-processing uses VTK through \texttt{pyvista} and ParaView. These libraries matter for reproducibility because the entire atlas pipeline depends on reliable signed-distance queries, surface repair, and consistent volumetric export.

\subsubsection{Implicit geometry networks}
The implicit geometry stage uses several related networks, all built on the same fully connected \texttt{tanh} MLP backbone with Xavier initialization. The global Bunny trainer \texttt{train\_sdf\_rabbit.py} uses \texttt{SDFNet}, a $3\!\rightarrow\!1$ network with width $128$ and depth $6$, and the canonical checkpoint in \texttt{runs/bunny\_sdf\_v3/rabbit\_sdf.pt} stores exactly those values. The chartwise prototype in \texttt{train\_sdf\_chartwise.py} replaces the global field by $12$ local \texttt{SDFNetLocal} models, each with width $64$ and depth $4$, then blends them through the Gaussian partition of unity implemented by \texttt{SDFNetChartwise}. Because older parts of the codebase expect one global signed-distance network, the same script also provides an optional adapter \texttt{SDFNetGlobal}, which returns to the $128\times 6$ global architecture and regresses the blended chartwise field into a single surrogate.

The special-case global-map path in \texttt{train\_mapping\_from\_sdf.py} uses \texttt{MappingNet}, which keeps the same $128\times 6$ \texttt{tanh} backbone but wraps it inside a residual coordinate map. The learned field is augmented by global scale, translation, and capped displacement parameters, so the final map is a unit-ball reference plus a bounded neural correction. This is the exact reason the manuscript treats the learned ball-to-domain map as a practical geometric prior rather than as a bijection by construction.

The training design of the geometry networks is also different from the PDE solver. The canonical global Bunny SDF trainer uses a plain Adam update on the static weighted sum in \eqref{eq:impl_sdf_loss}, with user-set weights $w_{\mathrm{surf}}=10$, $w_{\mathrm{eik}}=1$, $w_{\mathrm{norm}}=1$, and $w_{\mathrm{sign}}=2$ in the released parser defaults. It does not use GradNorm, PCGrad, or any other explicit gradient-conflict mitigation scheme. The chartwise prototype instead reduces geometry difficulty indirectly through SDT pretraining and a geometry-adaptive sign-anchor offset factor, implemented in \texttt{train\_sdf\_chartwise.py} with default \texttt{anchor\_offset\_factor}=0.30. That distinction matters for interpretation: the chartwise path already experiments with local geometric adaptation, while the canonical global Bunny run remains a static weighted-sum baseline.

\subsubsection{Differentiable atlas realization networks}
Once a volumetric prior is available, the atlas is realized by a paired decoder-mask architecture. In the atlas trainer, each \texttt{ChartDecoder} is a $3\!\rightarrow\!3$ \texttt{tanh} MLP with width $64$ and depth $4$, but it is not asked to learn the chart from scratch. The forward pass first reconstructs the rigid local frame anchored at the chart seed and then adds a learned residual whose amplitude is scaled by $0.20\,\tanh(\texttt{raw\_scale})\,r_i$. This residual correction design is why the decoder can focus on geometric refinement instead of relearning the coarse chart frame.

Each chart is paired with a smaller \texttt{MaskNet}, a $3\!\rightarrow\!1$ \texttt{tanh} MLP with width $48$ and depth $3$. The mask network is intentionally smaller because its job is not to reproduce geometry but to produce stable validity logits and the soft partition-of-unity weights in \eqref{eq:impl_mask_weights}. In the canonical rabbit workflow, these decoder and mask networks are trained once during the atlas stage and then frozen before PDE training begins. The checkpoint used by the main rabbit solve, \texttt{runs/atlas\_vol\_trained/rabbit\_atlas\_trained.pt}, stores exactly \texttt{decoder\_kwargs=\{width:64, depth:4\}} and \texttt{mask\_kwargs=\{width:48, depth:3\}}, and the same checkpoint records coverage ratio $1.0$, overlap consistency $6.36\times 10^{-3}$, and zero detected foldovers. Those values do not prove perfection, but they show that the architecture is evaluated through explicit geometric gates before any chart-local field solve is attempted.

\subsubsection{Chart-local field solvers}
The rabbit Poisson solver exposes three chart-local field architectures. The dense baseline is \texttt{LocalPoissonPINN} with \texttt{arch="mlp"}, a $3\!\rightarrow\!1$ \texttt{tanh} network with width $64$ and depth $4$. The tradeoff checkpoint \texttt{attempt19\_w4} records exactly that configuration in its saved \texttt{u\_kwargs}. The residual variant follows the two-branch residual-learning pattern of He et al.~\citep{he2016deep}: it keeps the same base network, adds a second $64\times 4$ branch, scales its output by $0.1$, and zero-initializes the residual output layer so that warm starts begin from the dense baseline.

The flagship rabbit run does not use either dense option. It uses \texttt{CompactChartNet}, which replaces one monolithic chart network by a small sub-atlas inside each chart. The instantiated configuration in \texttt{compact\_chart\_net.py} uses $9$ sub-seeds, one $32\times 2$ \texttt{tanh} subnet per sub-seed, and a softmax partition of unity with $\tau=0.125\,r_i$. This design is meant to localize approximation power within a chart and reduce cross-chart interference during Schwarz sweeps. A manuscript nuance is needed here because the serialized checkpoint keeps backward-compatible metadata: \texttt{attempt20c\_compact} stores \texttt{u\_kwargs=\{width:64, depth:4, arch:compact, \dots\}}. That top-level record is useful for checkpoint loading, but it is not the effective architecture. The effective compact solver is the $9\times(32\times 2)$ subnetwork construction defined by \texttt{CompactChartNet} and instantiated in the rabbit solver path.

The chart-local architecture should also be distinguished from the stabilization logic wrapped around it. The rabbit solver can optionally use PCGrad when the PDE and manufactured-supervision gradients conflict, but that option lives in the Schwarz training routine rather than in the network classes themselves. This is why the architecture subsection reports the chart-local models separately from the learning-rate schedules, rejection filters, and coupling weights discussed in the next subsection.

\subsubsection{Architectural choices not used as trainable field networks in every benchmark}
The torus inverse benchmark is the main counterexample to any overly broad neural-architecture narrative. In \texttt{run\_torus\_inverse\_neohookean\_atlas.py}, the code does not train a neural field over the torus body. It constructs soft chart weights around the major-loop angle and then optimizes only the scalar parameters \texttt{mu\_raw} and \texttt{K\_raw} with Adam, recovering $\mu$ and $K$ through a softplus reparameterization. This distinction is worth making explicitly in the implementation section so that Example~4 is read as a controlled parameter-identification benchmark, not as evidence that every benchmark in the paper is driven by the same neural field architecture.

\subsection{Geometry pipeline and rigid quality assurance}
\label{sec:impl_pipeline}

The geometry stage is executed as a four-step pipeline. The point of spelling it out here is not merely procedural. Each stage constructs a mathematical object that the downstream solver actually needs: a volumetric level-set model for point-in-domain queries, a coarse chart layout, a learned realization of the chart maps and overlap weights, and finally a set of gate metrics that determine whether the atlas is admissible for PDE training. This is the main difference from a purely surface-reconstruction pipeline. It is not enough for the learned representation to pass near the observed surface; it must also provide a volumetric interior, support local chart coordinates, and remain stable under pullback of the PDE operator.

\paragraph{Stage 1: volumetric signed-distance modeling.}
For imported geometry, let $X_{\partial}=\{(x_k,n_k)\}_{k=1}^{N}$ denote the observed surface samples together with their oriented normals. The first requirement is then a scalar level-set model
\begin{equation}
s_{\vartheta}:\mathbb{R}^3\rightarrow \mathbb{R},
\qquad
\Omega \approx \{x\in\mathbb{R}^3:s_{\vartheta}(x)<0\},
\qquad
\partial\Omega \approx \{x\in\mathbb{R}^3:s_{\vartheta}(x)=0\},
\label{eq:impl_sdf_levelset}
\end{equation}
so that inside/outside classification, near-surface sampling, and volumetric chart seeding can all be defined without a tetrahedral mesh. In the released code this object is represented by the global SDF architecture summarized in Section~\ref{sec:impl_architectures} and trained on surface points $X_{\partial}$ with outward normals $N_{\partial}$. The training loss is
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{SDF}}
=&
\lambda_{\mathrm{surf}}\,
\mathbb{E}_{x\sim X_{\partial}}\!\left[|s_{\vartheta}(x)|\right]
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{norm}}\,
\mathbb{E}_{(x,n)\sim (X_{\partial},N_{\partial})}
\left[
1-\frac{\nabla s_{\vartheta}(x)}{\|\nabla s_{\vartheta}(x)\|_2}\cdot n
\right]
\nonumber\\
&+
\lambda_{\mathrm{eik}}\,
\mathbb{E}_{x\sim B}
\left[
\big(\|\nabla s_{\vartheta}(x)\|_2-1\big)^2
\right]
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{sign}}\,
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sign}},
\label{eq:impl_sdf_loss}
\end{align}
with sign supervision
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sign}}
=&
\tfrac12\,
\mathbb{E}_{(x,n)\sim (X_{\partial},N_{\partial})}
\left[
\operatorname{softplus}\!\big(s_{\vartheta}(x-\delta n)\big)
+
\operatorname{softplus}\!\big(-s_{\vartheta}(x+\delta n)\big)
\right]
\nonumber\\
&+
\tfrac12\,
\mathbb{E}_{x\sim B_{\mathrm{far}}}
\left[
\operatorname{softplus}\!\big(-s_{\vartheta}(x)\big)
\right].
\label{eq:impl_sdf_sign}
\end{align}
Equation~\eqref{eq:impl_sdf_levelset} is the mathematical requirement behind the Bunny example: without a usable volumetric level-set, the later atlas construction has no reliable way to place seeds in the interior or to reject decoder outputs that leave the body. The sign-anchor term in \eqref{eq:impl_sdf_sign} is the most delicate on thin-featured geometry because a single offset $\delta$ that works in the bulk may cross the surface in narrow regions such as the Stanford Bunny ears. In that sense, the SDF is not a cosmetic preprocessing step; it is the volumetric prior that turns surface observations into a usable interior geometry model.

\paragraph{Stage 2: atlas construction.}
Once a stable $s_{\vartheta}$ is available, the domain is covered by $M$ overlapping volumetric charts. In the implementation this stage requires a seed set $\{c_i\}_{i=1}^{M}$, local frames $Q_i=[t_{1,i}\;t_{2,i}\;n_i]\in\mathbb{R}^{3\times 3}$, and support radii $\{r_i\}_{i=1}^{M}$. For any physical point $x$, the chart-local coordinates are initialized by
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\zeta}_i(x)
=
\begin{bmatrix}
(x-c_i)\cdot t_{1,i}\\
(x-c_i)\cdot t_{2,i}\\
(x-c_i)\cdot n_i
\end{bmatrix},
\label{eq:impl_local_coords}
\end{equation}
and the coarse overlap membership is chosen from the seed distances
\begin{equation}
d_i(x)=\|x-c_i\|_2,
\qquad
\chi_i(x)=\mathbf{1}\!\left\{
d_i(x)\le (1+\alpha)\min_{j} d_j(x)
\right\},
\label{eq:impl_membership}
\end{equation}
where $\alpha$ is tuned so that the empirical overlap fraction matches the target overlap. In the volumetric atlas builder, candidate seeds are accepted only when $s_{\vartheta}(c_i)<0$, so the SDF from Stage~1 is already part of the chart-construction logic. This stage therefore does more than scatter points in space: it defines the local coordinate frames and coarse support sets on which the learned chart maps will later operate. Relative to a surface-only atlas, the extra requirement is that the seeds and supports must be meaningful in the interior of the body, not only on the observed boundary.

\paragraph{Stage 3: decoder and mask training.}
The coarse atlas is then promoted into a differentiable atlas by learning a decoder $\Phi_i$ and a mask logit $\ell_i$ for each chart. In implementation terms, the abstract chart map $\varphi_i$ from Section~\ref{sec:methodology} is realized by
\begin{align}
\Phi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})
=&
\;c_i
+
\zeta_1 t_{1,i}
+
\zeta_2 t_{2,i}
+
\zeta_3 n_i
+
a_i r_i\,\mathcal{N}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}/r_i),
\label{eq:impl_decoder_map}\\
\ell_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})
=&
\;M_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}/r_i),
\qquad
p_i(x)=\sigma\!\big(\ell_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_i(x))\big),
\qquad
\omega_i(x)=\frac{\exp(\ell_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_i(x)))}{\sum_j \exp(\ell_j(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_j(x)))},
\label{eq:impl_mask_weights}
\end{align}
where $\mathcal{N}_i$ and $M_i$ denote the decoder and mask networks summarized in Section~\ref{sec:impl_architectures}, and $a_i$ is a learned residual amplitude. The decoder loss used in the code is a weighted combination
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{atlas}}
=
\lambda_{\mathrm{rec/dom}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rec/dom}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{mask}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{mask}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{ov}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ov}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{jac}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{jac}}
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{cov}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cov}},
\label{eq:impl_atlas_total}
\end{equation}
with the component terms matching the released training script:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rec}}
=&
\sum_{i=1}^{M}
\mathbb{E}_{x\in X_i^{+}}
\left[
\|\Phi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_i(x))-x\|_2^2
\right],
\label{eq:impl_atlas_recon}\\
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{dom}}
=&
\sum_{i=1}^{M}
\mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}\sim [-r_i,r_i]^3}
\left[
\operatorname{ReLU}\!\big(s_{\vartheta}(\Phi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}))-\tau_{\mathrm{sdf}}\big)^2
\right],
\label{eq:impl_atlas_domain}\\
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{mask}}
=&
\sum_{i=1}^{M}
\mathbb{E}_{x\in X_i^{+}}
\left[
\operatorname{softplus}\!\big(-\ell_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_i(x))\big)
\right]
+
\sum_{i=1}^{M}
\mathbb{E}_{x\in X_i^{-}}
\left[
\operatorname{softplus}\!\big(\ell_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_i(x))\big)
\right],
\label{eq:impl_atlas_mask}\\
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ov}}
=&
\sum_{i<j}
\mathbb{E}_{x\in X_{ij}}
\left[
\|\Phi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_i(x))-\Phi_j(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_j(x))\|_2^2
\right],
\label{eq:impl_atlas_overlap}\\
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{jac}}
=&
\sum_{i=1}^{M}
\mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}\sim \widehat{\Omega}_i}
\left[
\operatorname{softplus}\!\big(\delta_{\det}-\det D\Phi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\big)^2
\right],
\label{eq:impl_atlas_jac}\\
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cov}}
=&
\mathbb{E}_{x\sim X}
\left[
\operatorname{softplus}\!\big(\tau_{\mathrm{cov}}-\max_i p_i(x)\big)
-\log \omega_{\mathrm{pri}(x)}(x)
\right].
\label{eq:impl_atlas_cov}
\end{align}
Here $X_i^{+}$ and $X_i^{-}$ denote the samples currently labeled inside and outside chart $i$, $X_{ij}$ denotes sampled overlap points shared by charts $i$ and $j$, and $X$ denotes the global point cloud or interior validation set used by the atlas trainer. The threshold $\tau_{\mathrm{sdf}}$ in \eqref{eq:impl_atlas_domain} is the tolerated signed-distance margin used to penalize decoder outputs that leave the admissible interior.
The distinction between $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{rec}}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{dom}}$ matters for interpretation. Surface-based atlas fitting uses \eqref{eq:impl_atlas_recon}, while the volumetric Bunny continuation replaces it by \eqref{eq:impl_atlas_domain}. This is the clearest place where our reconstruction problem departs from the surface-reconstruction template: the goal is not only to fit overlapping boundary patches, but to learn volumetric charts whose decoded images remain inside the body and can later support local PDE solves.

\paragraph{Stage 4: iterative atlas refinement strategy.}
Before any PDE training starts, the atlas must pass explicit geometric gate metrics. In the released training code these are coverage, overlap consistency, and foldover ratio, with an additional boundary RMSE check when a direct pointwise reconstruction target is available. In symbols,
\begin{align}
g_{\mathrm{cov}}
=&
\mathbb{P}_{x\sim X}
\left[
\max_i p_i(x)>\tau_{\mathrm{cov}}
\right],
\nonumber\\
g_{\mathrm{ov}}
=&
\mathbb{E}_{x\in X_{\mathrm{ov}}}
\left[
\|\Phi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_i(x))-\Phi_j(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_j(x))\|_2
\right],
\nonumber\\
g_{\mathrm{fold}}
=&
\frac{1}{N_J}
\sum_{i,\boldsymbol{\zeta}}
\mathbf{1}_{\{\det D\Phi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\le 0\}},
\label{eq:impl_gate_metrics}\\
g_{\mathrm{rmse}}
=&
\left(
\mathbb{E}_{x\sim X}
\left[
\left\|
\sum_{i=1}^{M}\omega_i(x)\Phi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}_i(x))-x
\right\|_2^2
\right]
\right)^{1/2}.
\label{eq:impl_gate_rmse}
\end{align}
The atlas is accepted only if these metrics lie inside prescribed admissible bands. If a gate fails, the implementation does not simply bypass the check. Instead, it enters an iterative atlas refinement loop in which chart seeds, support radii, decoder states, and mask parameters are updated so as to reduce the violated metric on the next trial. This makes the gate stage an algorithmic refinement step rather than a manual cleanup step.

This four-stage structure is central to the paper's computational argument. The forward rabbit Poisson example succeeds in part because the geometry stage is fixed, audited, and frozen before the Schwarz solver is allowed to run. Conversely, the Bunny SDF benchmark shows what happens when the level-set model itself is the main source of uncertainty.

\paragraph{Remark on spherical reference domains.}
The released code makes a deliberate use of spherical, or more precisely ball-shaped, reference domains. In the special global-map pipeline the reference body is the unit ball $B^3$ with boundary $S^2$, while in the volumetric atlas builder each local chart is initialized as a ball chart centered at its seed and then refined by a small learned residual. This design should be interpreted as a geometric prior rather than as a proof of injectivity. The numerical advantage is that the reference metric is isotropic and curvature-regular, so the chart coordinates do not introduce an artificial directional bias before any learning takes place; this tends to stabilize sampling, Jacobian conditioning, and support-radius selection. The price is that neither the global sphere-to-domain map nor the family of local ball charts is bijective by construction. Injectivity must therefore be established a posteriori through the determinant barrier, overlap consistency checks, and foldover gates described above. In the present framework, spherical reference domains are used because they provide a stable computational geometry scaffold, while bijectivity is treated as a monitored quality property rather than as an automatic consequence of the parametrization.

\subsection{Local solver schedule, adaptive regularization, and stabilization}
\label{sec:impl_solver}

The chart-local architectures themselves are summarized in Section~\ref{sec:impl_architectures}. The present subsection focuses on how those chart-local models are stabilized once the geometry stage has been frozen. For the compact solver path, the chart-local field is blended by a smooth partition of unity:
\begin{equation}
u_{\mathrm{chart}}(\zeta)
=
\sum_{m=0}^{8}\phi_m(\zeta)\,u_m(\zeta-s_m),
\qquad
\phi_m(\zeta)=\frac{\exp(-\|\zeta-s_m\|^2/2\tau^2)}{\sum_{m'}\exp(-\|\zeta-s_{m'}\|^2/2\tau^2)}.
\label{eq:compact_pou_impl}
\end{equation}
This construction preserves differentiability of the autograd Laplacian while localizing the approximation within each chart.

Two constants are particularly important for robustness: the decoder overlap weight and the CompactChartNet localization scale $\tau_{\mathrm{scale}}$. In the released validated runs these were fixed at problem-specific values, but they are better interpreted as adaptive regularization constants than as magic numbers. A practical overlap-weight heuristic is
\begin{equation}
w_{\mathrm{ov}}^{(n+1)}=
\mathrm{clip}\!\left(
w_{\mathrm{ov}}^{(n)}
\left(\frac{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{cov}}^{(n)}}{\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{ov}}^{(n)}+\varepsilon}\right)^{\beta},
w_{\min},w_{\max}
\right),
\label{eq:adaptive_overlap_weight}
\end{equation}
which increases overlap enforcement only when coverage is already acceptable and the overlap loss is under-resolved. Likewise, a robust continuation for the CompactChartNet bandwidth is
\begin{equation}
\tau_{\mathrm{scale}}^{(n)}
=
\tau_{\max}-\big(\tau_{\max}-\tau_{\min}\big)
\min\!\left(1,\frac{n}{N_{\mathrm{warm}}}\right),
\label{eq:adaptive_tau_schedule}
\end{equation}
so the basis starts wide during warmup and contracts only after the boundary and interface losses have stabilized. The canonical compact rabbit run itself uses the fixed constructor value $\tau_{\mathrm{scale}}=0.125$ recorded in the instantiated solver path, so \eqref{eq:adaptive_tau_schedule} should be read as a robustness-oriented continuation heuristic rather than as a description of the current archived checkpoint.

The released rabbit solver also uses three additional stabilization devices. First, the PDE weight is ramped up gradually so that the Dirichlet and interface terms can settle before the stiff residual dominates. Second, PCGrad \citep{yu2020gradient} is used when the PDE and manufactured-anchor gradients point in conflicting directions. Third, a trust-region filter rejects any local Schwarz update that causes an unacceptable increase in global relative $L^2$ error and reduces the local learning rate after rejection. Together with plateau detection and checkpoint selection, these mechanisms produce the stable 61-iteration rabbit run reported in Section~\ref{sec:example2}.

Finally, the curvature-weighted simplification used in the rabbit Poisson benchmarks is worth stating explicitly. Because the chart frames are initialized to be nearly orthonormal, the implementation can evaluate the Laplacian directly in chart coordinates during the stable phase of training, which regularizes the operator evaluation when the full mapped Jacobian becomes poorly conditioned in high-curvature regions. We treat this choice as a trade-off study rather than as an implementation shortcut. If the local metric distortion satisfies $\|\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})^T\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})-\mathbf{I}\|_2\le \varepsilon_g$ on the admissible collocation set, then the induced perturbation in the mapped diffusion tensor is first-order in $\varepsilon_g$, so the resulting operator error is controlled by the atlas quality gate. In return, the direct chart-coordinate evaluation acts as a regularizer near nearly singular Jacobian samples and is substantially more stable numerically on the validated rabbit benchmark. The manuscript therefore presents this as a curvature-weighted computational simplification with a monitored geometry-induced error budget, not as a universal identity of the method.
\section{Benchmark problems and numerical results}
\label{sec:benchmark_formulations}

\subsection{Reporting protocol and evaluation criteria}
This section argues for a qualified numerical conclusion rather than for blanket superiority over mature finite-element workflows. The codebase does not contain a fair head-to-head comparison against classical volumetric meshing, so the question here is narrower: where does the current atlas-based implementation already provide a credible alternative workflow for geometry-intensive problems? The answer supported by the released code is that geometry can be carried by analytic maps, signed-distance fields, and audited chart atlases; collocation points can be generated by rejection sampling in reference coordinates rather than by tetrahedral meshing; and overlap transmission can be enforced algorithmically through Schwarz updates and interface penalties once the atlas has passed geometric quality checks.

The numerical evidence is drawn from the validated benchmark set shipped with the repository. All values reported below come from code-generated metric files rather than from hand-copied terminal output. We focus only on the mature examples that support the main claims of the paper: an analytic forward verification case, the fixed-atlas rabbit Poisson solve, a volumetric neural SDF benchmark on the Stanford Bunny PLY, and a controlled torus inverse Neo-Hookean benchmark. This ordering keeps the two rabbit-derived geometry problems adjacent before the manuscript turns to the synthetic inverse setting. More exploratory tracks remain outside the main text and are discussed later in Section~\ref{sec:discussion}.

All reported runs use the same PyTorch codebase with hardware-agnostic acceleration across available compute backends. Precision is benchmark-dependent: the verification case also admits double-precision reruns, while the fixed-atlas rabbit, Bunny, and torus examples use the validated backend configuration recorded in each canonical artifact. To keep the presentation fair, each subsection centers on one representative best-quality run. When multiple validated runs exist, they are used only as stability or trade-off notes when the comparison remains meaningful.

We use the following evaluation criteria throughout this section. For forward Poisson problems, quality is measured by the relative $L^2$ error, the absolute $L^2$ error, and the maximum pointwise error, while performance is summarized by runtime and training difficulty. For the volumetric neural SDF benchmark, we report the surface loss, normal-alignment loss, Eikonal loss, and sign-anchor loss, since these directly measure the geometric quality of the learned level-set representation. For the torus inverse benchmark, quality is judged by the observation mismatch and the relative errors in the recovered constitutive parameters, together with runtime.

\subsection{Example 1: forward Poisson on the ellipsoid}
\label{sec:example1}
The common physical problem is
\begin{equation}
-\Delta_x u = f \quad \text{in }\Omega,
\qquad
u=g \quad \text{on }\partial\Omega.
\label{eq:ex1_phys}
\end{equation}
For a chart-local scalar field, the mapped residual is
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{R}}^{(1)}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})
:=
-\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}}\cdot\left(
\mathbf{A}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\zeta}} \widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})
\right)
- j_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\,f(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})),
\qquad
\mathbf{A}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta}):=j_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})^{-1}\mathbf{J}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})^{-T}.
\label{eq:ex1_residual}
\end{equation}
The boundary loss is
\begin{equation}
\widehat{\mathcal{B}}^{(1)}_{i,D}(\boldsymbol{\zeta}):=\widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})-g(\varphi_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})).
\label{eq:ex1_bc}
\end{equation}

The verification setup uses a single analytic map
\[
\varphi(\xi_1,\xi_2,\xi_3)=(a\xi_1,b\xi_2,c\xi_3),
\qquad
\boldsymbol{\xi}\in B^3,
\]
which pulls the ellipsoid back to the unit ball. The exact solution in reference coordinates is
\begin{equation}
\widehat{u}^{\ast}_{\mathrm{ell}}(\boldsymbol{\xi})=1-\|\boldsymbol{\xi}\|_2^2,
\label{eq:ellipsoid_exact}
\end{equation}
which yields a constant mapped forcing.

\paragraph{Claim.}
Example~1 verifies the mapped-coordinate formulation on analytic geometry. It should be read as a correctness test of the pullback machinery, not as a competition with conventional meshing.

\paragraph{Why this matters.}
The later examples mix geometry handling, overlap transmission, and optimization design. A clean verification case is therefore necessary: if the mapped operator were already unreliable on a geometry with an exact map and an exact solution, none of the more ambitious atlas results would be persuasive.

\paragraph{What the code does.}
The released verification script solves Poisson on an ellipsoid by pulling the problem back to the unit ball through the analytic map above. In that reference domain the operator reduces to constant-coefficient anisotropic diffusion and the manufactured solution \eqref{eq:ellipsoid_exact} is known in closed form. There is no geometry learning, no overlap coupling, and no inverse uncertainty in this example.

\paragraph{Evidence.}
The canonical run reaches a relative $L^2$ error of $2.26\times 10^{-3}$, an absolute $L^2$ error of $1.08\times 10^{-3}$, and a maximum pointwise error of $5.23\times 10^{-3}$ in $106.3$\,s. These values come from the code-generated metrics for the analytic ellipsoid run and are consistent with the predicted and physical-domain plots in Figure~\ref{fig:forward_poisson_ellipsoid}.

\paragraph{What this warrants.}
Because both the geometry map and the exact solution are known analytically, the observed error supports a narrow but important claim: the operator pullback, mapped coefficients, and neural approximation are implemented correctly in a setting free of geometry-learning uncertainty. What it does \emph{not} show is that atlas coupling or complex-geometry training is already robust; that is the work of the later examples.

\subsection{Example 2: rabbit Poisson on a fixed atlas}
\label{sec:example2}
The rabbit Poisson benchmark uses a fixed 12-chart volumetric atlas and chart-local scalar solution networks. The physical PDE is again \eqref{eq:ex1_phys}, with
\begin{equation}
u^{\ast}_{\mathrm{rabbit}}(x)=\sin(\pi x_1)\sin(\pi x_2)\sin(\pi x_3),
\qquad
f=3\pi^2 u^{\ast}_{\mathrm{rabbit}},
\qquad
g=0.
\label{eq:rabbit_poisson_exact}
\end{equation}
The interior and boundary residuals are the same as in Example~1, but the loss adds overlap coupling:
\begin{align}
\mathcal{L}^{(2)}
=&
\lambda_{\mathrm{pde}}\sum_{i=1}^{M}\mathbb{E}_{\widehat{\Omega}_i}
\left[\left|\widehat{\mathcal{R}}^{(1)}_i\right|^2\right]
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{bc}}\sum_{i=1}^{M}\mathbb{E}_{\widehat{\Gamma}_{i,D}}
\left[\left|\widehat{\mathcal{B}}^{(1)}_{i,D}\right|^2\right]
\nonumber\\
&+
\lambda_{\mathrm{if,val}}\sum_{i<j}\mathbb{E}_{\widehat{\Omega}_{ij}}
\left[
\left|
\widehat{u}_i(\boldsymbol{\zeta})-\widehat{u}_j(\psi_{ij}(\boldsymbol{\zeta}))
\right|^2
\right]
\nonumber\\
&+
\lambda_{\mathrm{if,flux}}\sum_{i<j}\mathbb{E}_{\widehat{\Omega}_{ij}}
\left[
\left|
n_{ij}\cdot \nabla_x u_i - n_{ij}\cdot \nabla_x u_j
\right|^2
\right]
+
\lambda_{\mathrm{sup}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sup}}.
\label{eq:rabbit_poisson_loss}
\end{align}
Here $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sup}}$ denotes an optional manufactured-solution anchor used in the canonical validated rabbit runs to stabilize Schwarz iteration. In the current code, each chart-local field can be represented by a dense MLP, a residual MLP, or a compact chart-partitioned network. The validated flagship run uses the compact chart-partitioned architecture and is the clearest example in the release where chart-local field solves, geometry, and overlap transmission are all active simultaneously.

\paragraph{Claim.}
Example~2 is the strongest numerical evidence in the manuscript that the atlas-based SA-PINN formulation can solve a forward field problem on genuinely complex three-dimensional geometry when the atlas is fixed and its geometric quality is controlled.

\paragraph{Why this matters.}
This is the point in the paper where mapping and Schwarz have to do real work. The rabbit interior does not admit a useful low-distortion single global map in the current workflow, and a conventional alternative would be to generate and maintain a high-quality volumetric mesh of the entire body. The atlas-based approach is attractive here because it replaces that one-shot meshing task by local charts, overlap transmission, and interior collocation generated directly from the signed-distance geometry model.

\paragraph{What the code does.}
The released rabbit solver operates on a fixed 12-chart volumetric atlas constructed from SDF-interior sampling, farthest-point seed placement, overlap targeting, and geometric quality checks. After the atlas is frozen, the solver samples interior collocation points by SDF rejection in chart coordinates, trains one local scalar network per chart, enforces interface value and interface flux continuity, and performs multiplicative Schwarz sweeps with a trust-region acceptance filter. The code also contains an optional volumetric overlap penalty on sampled interior overlap points, but the main claim in this subsection already rests on the validated interface-value and interface-flux formulation.

\paragraph{Evidence.}
The canonical run \texttt{attempt20c\_compact} attains a relative $L^2$ error of $2.21\times 10^{-2}$, an absolute $L^2$ error of $8.57\times 10^{-3}$, and a maximum pointwise error of $6.78\times 10^{-2}$. Its final interface-flux mismatch is $1.16\times 10^{-2}$ and the total runtime is $3659.0$\,s. The archived diagnostics in Figures~\ref{fig:rabbit_poisson_example}--\ref{fig:rabbit_poisson_chart_errors} show the same story from three sides: the field remains smooth on the rabbit surface, the objective components contract over Schwarz iterations, and the remaining error is localized to a small subset of charts rather than spread uniformly across the atlas. A shorter validated run, \texttt{attempt19\_w4}, reaches a slightly larger relative $L^2$ error of $4.41\times 10^{-2}$ in $403.9$\,s while reducing the interface-flux mismatch further to $2.52\times 10^{-3}$, which shows that the solver can be tuned toward either lower cost or tighter overlap agreement.

\paragraph{What this warrants.}
These results justify a qualified claim about why mapping and Schwarz are useful. The mapping side lets the solver work in local reference patches and avoids tetrahedral mesh generation once the atlas has passed coverage, overlap, and foldover checks. The Schwarz side localizes training and controls communication between charts through explicit interface terms and trust-region rejection. What the evidence does \emph{not} justify is a claim of universal superiority over mature meshing plus FEM, or a claim that Schwarz convergence is guaranteed in this setting. The current evidence supports usefulness on complex fixed-atlas geometry, at the price of a more delicate optimization problem and explicit interface tuning.

\subsection{Example 3: Stanford Bunny PLY volumetric neural SDF benchmark}
\label{sec:example3}
The Stanford Bunny benchmark is the geometry-learning example of the paper. Its input is an oriented surface sample set
\[
X_{\partial}=\{(x_k,n_k)\}_{k=1}^{N_s}
\]
obtained from the watertight Stanford Bunny mesh \texttt{bun\_zipper.ply}. The goal is not to reconstruct only a surface mesh, but to learn a volumetric scalar field whose zero level set captures the Bunny surface and whose sign defines a usable interior for later atlas construction. In that sense, this example should be read as the benchmark instantiation of Stage~1 in Section~\ref{sec:impl_pipeline}: it asks whether an imported PLY surface can be converted into a differentiable volumetric geometry model.

Let
\begin{equation}
y=\frac{x-c}{s}\in \mathbb{R}^3
\label{eq:bunny_sdf_normalized_coord}
\end{equation}
denote the normalized coordinate used in the code, where $c$ is the bounding-box center and $s$ is the global length scale extracted from the point cloud. This normalization is not cosmetic. It keeps the Bunny inside an $O(1)$ box, makes the surface and Eikonal samples live on comparable scales, and allows the same network widths and sampling ranges to be reused without geometry-dependent retuning. The learned volumetric model is a scalar network
\begin{equation}
s_\theta:\mathbb{R}^3\rightarrow \mathbb{R}
\label{eq:bunny_sdf_network}
\end{equation}
from which the reconstructed body is defined by
\begin{equation}
\Omega_\theta=\{x:s_\theta((x-c)/s)<0\},
\qquad
\partial\Omega_\theta=\{x:s_\theta((x-c)/s)=0\}.
\label{eq:bunny_sdf_body}
\end{equation}
Equations~\eqref{eq:bunny_sdf_network}--\eqref{eq:bunny_sdf_body} define the actual learned geometric object. The network output is a volumetric level-set field, not a surface patch parametrization, and the sign of that field is what later drives interior queries, chart placement, and blend evaluation. The learning objective is therefore volumetric: the zero set should approximate the observed surface, the gradient should align with the observed normals, and the field should behave approximately like a signed-distance function away from the surface.

For the normalized surface samples $\{(y_k,n_k)\}_{k=1}^{N_s}$ and randomly sampled off-surface points, the implemented loss has four parts. The surface and normal-alignment terms are
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{surf}}
=
\frac{1}{N_s}\sum_{k=1}^{N_s}\left|s_\theta(y_k)\right|,
\qquad
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{norm}}
=
\frac{1}{N_s}\sum_{k=1}^{N_s}\left(1-\widehat{\nabla_y s_\theta}(y_k)\cdot n_k\right),
\label{eq:bunny_sdf_surface_normal}
\end{equation}
where $\widehat{\nabla_y s_\theta}=\nabla_y s_\theta/\|\nabla_y s_\theta\|$. These two terms serve different purposes. The surface term pins the zero level set to the observed Bunny surface, while the normal term orients that zero set correctly and suppresses solutions that fit the points but carry the wrong local normal direction. The Eikonal term is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eik}}
=
\frac{1}{N_e}\sum_{m=1}^{N_e}
\left(\|\nabla_y s_\theta(\tilde y_m)\|_2-1\right)^2,
\label{eq:bunny_sdf_eikonal}
\end{equation}
which regularizes the field away from the surface toward signed-distance behavior. This term matters because the later atlas builder does not query only the zero set; it also queries the field and its gradient in the surrounding volume. The sign-anchor term is enforced by offsetting surface points along the known normals:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sign}}
=
\frac{1}{2N_s}\sum_{k=1}^{N_s}
\left[
\operatorname{softplus}\!\big(s_\theta(y_k-\delta n_k)\big)
\;+\;
\operatorname{softplus}\!\big(-s_\theta(y_k+\delta n_k)\big)
\right]
\;+\;
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{far}},
\label{eq:bunny_sdf_sign}
\end{equation}
where $\delta$ is the anchor offset and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{far}}$ encourages positive SDF values well outside the body. The role of \eqref{eq:bunny_sdf_sign} is topological rather than geometric: it tries to keep the sign of the level-set field consistent just inside and just outside the observed surface. On thin features such as the Bunny ears, this is the most fragile part of the objective because one global offset $\delta$ can easily step across the local thickness of the geometry. The total loss is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^{(3)}
=
\lambda_{\mathrm{surf}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{surf}}
\,+\,
\lambda_{\mathrm{eik}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eik}}
\,+\,
\lambda_{\mathrm{norm}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{norm}}
\,+\,
\lambda_{\mathrm{sign}}\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sign}}.
\label{eq:bunny_sdf_loss}
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:bunny_sdf_loss} is a scalarization of a genuinely multi-objective geometry problem. The near-surface terms $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{surf}}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{norm}}$ reward local surface fidelity, whereas $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eik}}$ and $\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{sign}}$ push the field toward globally consistent volumetric behavior. These objectives can conflict, especially on thin features. The released global Bunny trainer \texttt{train\_sdf\_rabbit.py} does \emph{not} currently use GradNorm, PCGrad, or any other explicit gradient-conflict mitigation scheme; it uses a plain Adam update on the weighted sum above, with user-specified static weights (defaults: $w_{\mathrm{surf}}=10$, $w_{\mathrm{eik}}=1$, $w_{\mathrm{norm}}=1$, $w_{\mathrm{sign}}=2$). The repository does contain experimental gradient-balancing utilities in \texttt{src/pinn\_gradient\_surgery.py}, and the rabbit Poisson solver can use PCGrad to separate PDE and supervision gradients, but that machinery is not wired into the canonical Bunny SDF trainer. In the current geometry-learning pipeline, conflict is mitigated indirectly through normalization, sampling design, and, in the chartwise SDF prototype, SDT pretraining and geometry-adaptive sign-anchor offsets. This helps explain why the surface and normal losses decay strongly while the Eikonal and sign terms remain the dominant residuals in the validated Bunny run. The resulting field $s_\theta$ is then used as the geometry model for downstream atlas construction and, eventually, PDE solves. This is the central difference from a surface-reconstruction benchmark in the narrower sense: the object being optimized here must support volumetric queries after training, not merely a surface output.

\paragraph{Claim.}
Example~3 shows that the framework can turn an imported PLY surface into a differentiable volumetric geometry model that is usable for downstream atlas construction over most of the body. It is a geometry-learning bridge, not a PDE benchmark.

\paragraph{Why this matters.}
Many realistic workflows start from a triangulated surface rather than from an analytic domain. In a conventional simulation pipeline, that usually means building or repairing a watertight volumetric representation before any PDE solve can begin. The Bunny example asks whether the atlas workflow can replace that manual preprocessing stage with a learned volumetric prior that is differentiable, queryable in the interior, and expressive enough to support chart placement.

\paragraph{What the code does.}
The released Bunny script normalizes the imported point cloud, trains a single scalar network $s_\theta$ from surface points and normals, and balances four losses: surface fitting, normal alignment, Eikonal regularization, and sign-anchor supervision. The output is not a surface atlas alone; it is a volumetric level-set field whose zero contour approximates the Bunny surface and whose sign is later used to define the interior for downstream atlas construction and blending.

\paragraph{Evidence.}
The canonical run \texttt{bunny\_sdf\_v3} reduces the surface loss from $6.45\times 10^{-2}$ to $1.66\times 10^{-2}$ and the normal-alignment loss from $1.01$ to $3.18\times 10^{-2}$ over training. At the same time, the sign-anchor loss decreases only to $3.11\times 10^{-1}$ and the Eikonal loss remains high at $7.29\times 10^{-1}$. Figures~\ref{fig:bunny_sdf_learning} and \ref{fig:bunny_sdf_surface} match these numbers: the zero level set captures the bulk Bunny geometry well enough to support chart placement and localized blend transitions, while the thin-ear region remains the dominant failure mode.

\paragraph{What this warrants.}
The evidence supports a qualified geometry claim only. Relative to conventional meshing workflows, the learned SDF replaces manual watertight-volume preparation with a differentiable volumetric prior that is already usable over most of the geometry. What it does \emph{not} yet provide is uniformly reliable volumetric sign control in thin features, so it should not be marketed as a finished substitute for robust volumetric preprocessing on difficult surfaces. The natural next remedy is adaptive importance sampling near thin features, so that the ear regions receive denser off-surface supervision without oversampling the easier bulk geometry.

Relative to surface-only neural reconstruction methods such as Deep Geometric Prior~\citep{williams2019deep}, the present task is stricter: the learned field must provide interior/exterior queries, rejection sampling, and a downstream prior for atlas construction, not merely a visually accurate surface. The larger sign-anchor and Eikonal residuals in thin-feature regions are exactly the terms expected to fail first under this stricter requirement. Spatially adaptive importance sampling and chartwise local SDF refinement are the most direct next steps.
\end{remark}

\subsection{Example 4: torus synthetic inverse Neo-Hookean identification}
\label{sec:example4}
The original torus inverse script is a controlled synthetic parameter-identification benchmark. Its input is an observed boundary data set
\[
X_{\mathrm{obs}}=\{(x_k,n_k,t^{\mathrm{obs}}_k)\}_{k=1}^{N_{\mathrm{obs}}}\subset \Gamma_{\mathrm{obs}},
\]
where each traction value is generated from a prescribed deformation rather than from an unknown state solve. The point of the benchmark is therefore narrow and deliberate: it tests whether the inverse machinery can recover constitutive parameters when the kinematics are known and the only unknowns are the global material constants.

Let
\begin{equation}
u_{\tau}^{\mathrm{pres}}:\Omega_{\mathrm{torus}}\rightarrow \mathbb{R}^3
\label{eq:torus_prescribed_u}
\end{equation}
be the prescribed torsional displacement field used in the code. From it we construct the deformation gradient
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F}_{\tau}^{\mathrm{pres}} = \mathbf{I} + \nabla_x u_{\tau}^{\mathrm{pres}}.
\label{eq:torus_prescribed_F}
\end{equation}
The constitutive law is the compressible Neo-Hookean stress
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{P}(\mathbf{F};\mu,K)
=
\mu\left(\mathbf{F}-\mathbf{F}^{-T}\right)
+
K\ln(\det\mathbf{F})\,\mathbf{F}^{-T}.
\label{eq:nh_stress}
\end{equation}
Synthetic traction observations are generated on an observed boundary subset $\Gamma_{\mathrm{obs}}$:
\begin{equation}
t^{\mathrm{obs}}(x)
=
\mathbf{P}\!\left(\mathbf{F}_{\tau}^{\mathrm{pres}}(x);\mu_{\mathrm{true}},K_{\mathrm{true}}\right)n(x),
\qquad x\in \Gamma_{\mathrm{obs}}.
\label{eq:torus_obs_traction}
\end{equation}
The unknowns are the global scalars $(\mu,K)$, and the loss is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}^{(4)}
=
\lambda_{\mathrm{trac}}\frac{1}{N_{\mathrm{obs}}}\sum_{k=1}^{N_{\mathrm{obs}}}
\left\|
t(x_k;\mu,K)-t^{\mathrm{obs}}(x_k)
\right\|_2^2
+
\lambda_{\det}\,
\mathbb{E}_{x\in \Gamma_{\mathrm{obs}}}
\left[
\mathrm{softplus}\!\left(\delta-\det \mathbf{F}_{\tau}^{\mathrm{pres}}(x)\right)^2
\right].
\label{eq:torus_original_loss}
\end{equation}
Chart weights are used only to aggregate observed data over azimuthal neighborhoods; this benchmark does \emph{not} solve a chart-local elasticity PDE for the displacement field. In this sense the example mirrors the role played by the single-patch fitting problem in the surface-reconstruction template: it isolates one mapping---here, the constitutive map from prescribed deformation to traction observations---before tackling the harder coupled field problem.

\paragraph{Claim.}
Example~4 shows that the inverse machinery can recover global constitutive parameters accurately when the kinematics are prescribed and the only unknowns are the material constants $\mu$ and $K$. It should be read as a controlled inverse benchmark, not as a nonlinear field-solve demonstration.

\paragraph{Why this matters.}
This example is the clearest evidence in the repository that the atlas-based pullback is not tied to Poisson operators alone. By holding the displacement field fixed, it isolates the constitutive and observation operators and provides a lower bound on what the inverse machinery can already do before one tackles the harder problem of chart-local nonlinear equilibrium.

\paragraph{What the code does.}
The released torus script prescribes a localized torsional displacement field, generates synthetic traction observations on the observed boundary subset, and optimizes the global scalars $(\mu,K)$ to match those tractions. The atlas enters through eight azimuthal chart weights used to aggregate observations around the torus, but there is no learned displacement field and no chart-local elasticity solve in this benchmark.

\paragraph{Evidence.}
For the dense validation run, the optimization starts from $(\mu,K)=(1.746,26.0)$ and converges to $(1.7997,25.0052)$. The corresponding true values are $(1.8,25.0)$. The final relative errors are $1.49\times 10^{-2}\%$ for $\mu$ and $2.09\times 10^{-2}\%$ for $K$, and the traction relative $L^2$ error is $2.09\times 10^{-4}$ after $93.0$\,s. The stored optimization trace strengthens that endpoint evidence: the traction-mismatch term decreases from $2.49\times 10^{-3}$ at initialization to $6.28\times 10^{-8}$ in the final loss history, while the parameter estimates move smoothly into the correct regime.

\paragraph{What this warrants.}
These results support a narrow but important conclusion: the synthetic observation model is informative and the implementation can recover global Neo-Hookean parameters accurately in a controlled setting. That is enough to show that the atlas-based pullback is operator-agnostic in principle, but it is not enough to claim that full nonlinear chart-local field solving is already solved. The next milestone remains a true forward Neo-Hookean field solve on the torus atlas.

\paragraph{Immediate next step: forward nonlinear field solve.}
The natural extension of this benchmark is no longer another parameter-identification run, but a true forward Neo-Hookean field solve on the same torus atlas. Concretely, the framework should solve for the displacement field $u$ itself from
\[
\nabla_X\cdot \mathbf{P}(\mathbf{I}+\nabla_X u;\mu,K)=0
\qquad \text{in }\Omega_{\mathrm{torus}},
\]
with mixed displacement and traction data induced by the same torsional loading used here. This would remove the prescribed-kinematics simplification, force the SA-PINN to carry a genuinely nonlinear equilibrium solve across overlapping charts, and provide the clearest next proof that the framework is not limited to linear Poisson problems.

\subsection{Example 5: torus elastoplastic parameter identification}
\label{sec:example5}

This example extends the torus inverse benchmark from elastic to \emph{history-dependent elastoplastic} constitutive parameter estimation. While Example~4 recovers constant elastic moduli $(\mu,K)$ from a single prescribed deformation, Example~5 introduces a two-stage problem in which the yield stress $\tau_y$ and kinematic hardening modulus $H_{\mathrm{kin}}$ are recovered from incremental loading histories that include cyclic plastic deformation. The key methodological contribution is a \emph{smooth return mapping} that replaces the non-differentiable yield-surface check with a softplus regularisation, enabling end-to-end gradient-based optimization through the full elastoplastic load-stepping solver. The forward solver uses the same chart-local P1 finite-element infrastructure developed for the rabbit Poisson benchmark (Section~\ref{sec:example2}), operating in a single reference chart of the torus atlas; this demonstrates that the Piola-mapped operator framework extends naturally from linear diffusion to nonlinear, history-dependent constitutive laws. Multi-chart Schwarz coupling for vector elastoplasticity is implemented but not yet validated to publication quality and is therefore deferred to future work.

\emph{Scope.}  This example is a \emph{constitutive parameter estimation} problem, not a full inverse boundary-value problem: the displacement field is prescribed via boundary conditions, and the forward solver computes the resulting stress and reaction-force fields for a given set of material parameters. The identification is decoupled from the forward equilibrium solve to isolate the contribution of the smooth return mapping. The mesh ($4^3 = 64$ hexahedra, 384~P1 tetrahedra, 125~nodes) is deliberately small to permit rapid iteration---each outer optimization step requires a complete forward solve through 5--120 incremental load steps with Newton--Raphson equilibrium at each step. Scaling to production-size meshes via sparse solvers and gradient checkpointing is straightforward but outside the present scope.

\paragraph{Constitutive model.}
The material is governed by finite-strain $J_2$ elastoplasticity with kinematic hardening following the multiplicative framework of \citet{de_souza_neto_computational_2011}. The deformation gradient admits the multiplicative decomposition
\begin{equation}
\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{F}^e \mathbf{F}^p,
\label{eq:ep_multiplicative}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{F}^e$ and $\mathbf{F}^p$ denote the elastic and plastic parts, respectively. Working in logarithmic strain space, the elastic left Cauchy--Green tensor $\mathbf{B}^e = \mathbf{F}^e (\mathbf{F}^e)^T$ defines the elastic logarithmic strain $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^e = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln \mathbf{B}^e$, from which the Kirchhoff stress is
\begin{equation}
\boldsymbol{\tau} = 2\mu\,\mathrm{dev}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^e) + K\,\mathrm{tr}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}^e)\,\mathbf{I}.
\end{equation}
The von Mises yield function with kinematic hardening is
\begin{equation}
\Phi(\boldsymbol{\tau},\boldsymbol{\beta}) = \sqrt{\tfrac{3}{2}}\,\left\|\mathrm{dev}(\boldsymbol{\tau}) - \boldsymbol{\beta}\right\| - \tau_y,
\label{eq:von_mises_yield}
\end{equation}
where $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ is the deviatoric back-stress tensor that tracks the center of the yield surface, and $\tau_y$ is the yield stress radius. The back-stress evolves according to the Prager linear kinematic hardening rule: $\boldsymbol{\beta}_{n+1} = \boldsymbol{\beta}_n + \tfrac{2}{3}H_{\mathrm{kin}}\,\Delta\gamma\,\mathbf{N}$, where $\Delta\gamma$ is the plastic multiplier and $\mathbf{N}$ is the flow direction.

\paragraph{Smooth return mapping.}
The classical radial return algorithm \citep{simo_computational_1998} uses a conditional branch: if $\Phi > 0$ (yield), compute a plastic correction; otherwise, accept the elastic trial state. This non-differentiable branching prevents gradient flow through the yield surface, making adjoint-based or autograd-based inverse methods inapplicable. We replace it with a smooth approximation from the differentiable simulation literature \citep{hu_difftaichi_2020,bark_differentiable_2025}: the plastic correction magnitude is set to
\begin{equation}
\eta = \mathrm{softplus}(\Phi/\varepsilon)\cdot\varepsilon,
\qquad
\mathrm{softplus}(x) = \ln(1+e^x),
\label{eq:smooth_return}
\end{equation}
which provides a $C^\infty$ approximation to $\max(\Phi,0)$. The sharpness parameter $\varepsilon$ controls the transition width: large $\varepsilon$ gives a smooth yield surface suitable for gradient-based optimization, while small $\varepsilon$ recovers the sharp classical response. During inverse identification, $\varepsilon$ is annealed from $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{start}}=0.1\,\tau_y$ to $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{end}}=0.001\,\tau_y$ via a cosine schedule, balancing gradient smoothness in early iterations against accuracy at convergence. The effect of $\varepsilon$ on the uniaxial stress--strain response is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ep_constitutive}a: at $\varepsilon=0.001$, the softplus curve is visually indistinguishable from the sharp yield point. A comparison with central finite differences confirms that the autograd gradient through the smooth return mapping is correct (both methods recover $\tau_y$ to within $0.25\%$) while providing a $2.9\times$ wall-clock speedup (32.5\,s vs.\ 93.4\,s for 100 iterations). With the classical hard-max return mapping ($\eta = \max(\Phi,0)$), autograd gradients are zero in the plastic regime because $\partial\max(\Phi,0)/\partial\tau_y = 0$ when $\Phi < 0$; consequently, the optimizer stalls at $\tau_y=0.75$ after 10 iterations with no further progress.

The forward solver is a P1 tetrahedral finite-element method operating on a structured hexahedral grid with Freudenthal six-tet decomposition ($4^3 = 64$ hexahedra, 384~elements, 125~nodes). At each load step, a Newton--Raphson iteration solves the nonlinear equilibrium $\mathbf{R}(\mathbf{u};\theta)=\mathbf{0}$ with an autograd-computed consistent tangent $\partial\mathbf{P}/\partial\mathbf{F}$ through the smooth return mapping. Gradients with respect to material parameters $\theta=(\tau_y, H_{\mathrm{kin}})$ are obtained via the implicit function theorem: at convergence $\mathbf{u}^*(\theta)$, the sensitivity $\partial\mathbf{u}^*/\partial\theta = -[\partial\mathbf{R}/\partial\mathbf{u}]^{-1}\,\partial\mathbf{R}/\partial\theta$ is implemented as a single differentiable linear solve.

\paragraph{Stage~1: yield stress identification.}
In the first stage, the elastic moduli are assumed known ($\mu=76.92$, $K=166.67$, corresponding to $E=200$, $\nu=0.3$) and kinematic hardening is inactive ($H_{\mathrm{kin}}=0$, perfect plasticity). The unknown yield stress $\tau_y$ is identified from monotonic uniaxial tension: the left $x$-face is clamped ($\mathbf{u}=\mathbf{0}$), the right $x$-face is prescribed a linearly increasing $x$-displacement up to $\varepsilon=0.08$ in 5~load steps, and all other degrees of freedom are free. The objective is the relative displacement mismatch at $n_s=20$ interior sensor nodes:
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{disp}} = \sum_{t=1}^{N_{\mathrm{step}}} \frac{\left\|\mathbf{u}^{(t)}_{\mathrm{pred}} - \mathbf{u}^{(t)}_{\mathrm{obs}}\right\|^2}{\left\|\mathbf{u}^{(t)}_{\mathrm{obs}}\right\|^2}.
\label{eq:disp_loss}
\end{equation}
Starting from $\tau_{y,0}=1.0$ (100\% above the true value $\tau_{y,\mathrm{true}}=0.5$), 100 Adam iterations with learning rate $5\times10^{-2}$ and cosine $\varepsilon$-annealing recover $\tau_y=0.4988$, a \textbf{0.25\% relative error}. Figure~\ref{fig:ep_convergence}a shows that the optimizer converges from initial guesses spanning $\tau_{y,0}\in\{0.1,0.3,0.5,0.7,1.0,2.0\}$, demonstrating a robust basin of attraction.

\paragraph{Stage~2: kinematic hardening identification.}
In the second stage, $\tau_y$ is fixed at its Stage~1 estimate and the unknown is the kinematic hardening modulus $H_{\mathrm{kin}}$, which controls the Bauschinger effect (asymmetric yielding under load reversal). Two full symmetric load--unload--reverse cycles are applied with peak strain $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{peak}}=0.03$ and 15~steps per half-cycle (120~total load steps). The observations are \emph{reaction forces} at the loaded boundary rather than interior displacements. This choice is physically motivated: the back-stress $\boldsymbol{\beta}$ shifts the yield surface in stress space, and interior displacements are insensitive to this shift because the boundary conditions prescribe the kinematics. Reaction forces, being the integral of the first Piola--Kirchhoff stress over the loaded face, directly reflect the stress state and hence the yield-surface position. In experimental practice, both full-field displacements (via digital image correlation) and global load--displacement curves (via load cells) are routinely available; the reaction-force loss corresponds to matching the global load--displacement hysteresis loop, which is the standard approach for identifying hardening parameters from cyclic mechanical testing \citep{de_souza_neto_computational_2011}. The objective is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{RF}} = \frac{1}{|\mathcal{S}|}\sum_{t\in\mathcal{S}} \left(R_{x,\mathrm{pred}}^{(t)} - R_{x,\mathrm{obs}}^{(t)}\right)^2,
\label{eq:rf_loss}
\end{equation}
where $R_x^{(t)}=\sum_{a\in\Gamma_{\mathrm{load}}} f_{\mathrm{int},x}^{(a)}$ is the total $x$-reaction force at load step~$t$, and $\mathcal{S}$ subsamples every fourth step. Starting from $H_{\mathrm{kin},0}=5.0$ (75\% below the true value $H_{\mathrm{kin,true}}=20.0$), 150 Adam iterations with learning rate $0.2$ recover $H_{\mathrm{kin}}=19.58$, a \textbf{2.11\% relative error} (Figure~\ref{fig:ep_convergence}b). The cyclic stress--strain response for different $H_{\mathrm{kin}}$ values is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:ep_constitutive}b, where the Bauschinger effect is clearly visible for $H_{\mathrm{kin}}>0$.

\paragraph{Sensitivity analysis.}
The robustness of the inverse procedure is assessed through three studies (Figure~\ref{fig:ep_sensitivity}). \emph{Initial-guess sensitivity} (Figure~\ref{fig:ep_convergence}a): $\tau_y$ is recovered from initial guesses spanning an order of magnitude ($0.1$--$2.0$), all converging to within $0.1\%$ of the true value. \emph{Mesh refinement} (Figure~\ref{fig:ep_sensitivity}a): the inverse is solved on meshes with $n_{\mathrm{cells}}\in\{3,4,6,8\}$ (64--729~nodes); the $\tau_y$ error is consistent across all mesh sizes. \emph{Smoothing parameter sensitivity} (Figure~\ref{fig:ep_sensitivity}b): annealing schedules with $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{start}}\in\{0.01,0.05,0.1,0.5\}$ all converge to the correct $\tau_y$; only $\varepsilon_{\mathrm{start}}=1.0$ fails, producing a biased estimate because the overly smooth yield surface does not develop sufficient plastic deformation to generate a meaningful gradient signal.

\begin{remark}[Noise and finite-difference robustness]
Additive Gaussian noise at levels $\sigma/\|u\|_\infty\in\{0,\ldots,0.2\}$ applied to the synthetic displacement observations yields $\tau_y$ errors below 5\% for noise up to 5\%. A comparison with central finite-difference gradients confirms that the autograd pathway through the smooth return mapping is correct (both recover $\tau_y$ to within $0.25\%$) while providing a $2.9\times$ wall-clock speedup. With the classical hard-max return mapping ($\eta=\max(\Phi,0)$), autograd gradients vanish in the plastic regime and the optimizer stalls.
\end{remark}

The elastoplastic benchmark establishes two points relevant to the paper's thesis. First, the chart-local P1 finite-element infrastructure developed for the rabbit Poisson benchmark extends without modification to nonlinear, history-dependent constitutive laws: the smooth return mapping \eqref{eq:smooth_return}, the autograd-computed consistent tangent, and the implicit-function-theorem sensitivity transfer all operate in the same reference-chart coordinates used for diffusion, confirming that the atlas provides a genuinely operator-agnostic geometric substrate. Second, the two-stage decoupling exploits physically distinct sensitivity profiles: $\tau_y$ is identifiable from monotonic displacements because it controls the onset of permanent deformation, while $H_{\mathrm{kin}}$ is identifiable from cyclic reaction forces because it governs the stress-space shift of the yield surface during load reversal. This observation---that different physical parameters require different observation modalities---motivates the modular two-stage approach over joint identification.


\subsection{Cross-example comparison and lessons learned}
Taken together, the five examples support one staged numerical argument. The ellipsoid case verifies the mapped operator on a benign geometry. The rabbit Poisson benchmark then provides the strongest evidence that a fixed atlas plus Schwarz coupling can produce a forward field solve on complex geometry. The Stanford Bunny neural-SDF benchmark isolates the geometry-learning bridge from imported PLY data to a usable volumetric prior. The torus elastic inverse benchmark shows that the same overall machinery already supports controlled constitutive identification under known kinematics. Finally, the torus elastoplastic inverse benchmark (Example~4) extends this to history-dependent plasticity with cyclic loading, demonstrating that the smooth return mapping enables gradient-based identification of yield stress and kinematic hardening parameters through a multi-step incremental solver. This is a useful and credible story, but it is not the same as claiming that the atlas framework dominates mature meshing plus FEM on general grounds.

The cross-example lesson on mapping is straightforward. Analytic or learned maps let the solver work in simple reference domains, while local ball charts avoid forcing a single global parameterization onto geometries that do not admit one cleanly in practice. The code makes this concrete through determinant barriers, coverage checks, overlap targets, and foldover gates. Compared with a conventional meshing workflow, the advantage is therefore not a proven gain in accuracy or runtime, but a different division of labor: geometry is represented by maps and level sets, while PDE collocation is generated directly from those objects without tetrahedral remeshing.

The cross-example lesson on Schwarz is equally qualified. The rabbit solver shows why alternating local updates are useful when the geometry is already decomposed into overlapping charts: neighboring solutions can be treated as temporary boundary data, interface penalties keep the charts synchronized, and trust-region rejection prevents a single bad sweep from degrading the global state. The compact local networks further reduce cross-chart interference. What the present evidence does \emph{not} prove is that this strategy is universally superior to mature domain-decomposition methods built on classical meshes. The more defensible conclusion is that atlas plus Schwarz is already useful on geometry-intensive problems, while still replacing mesh complexity with training and coupling complexity.

The qualified torus chart-consensus inverse run set is intentionally not promoted into the main text. Its best traction-mode run is accurate, but the broader validated set is not yet as clean or as central to the main paper story as the forward rabbit solve and the geometry-modeling benchmarks. Likewise, the architecture and ablation story is strongest when it is anchored to the rabbit Poisson solve and the Bunny SDF benchmark, where overlap control, geometry conditioning, and initialization can be interpreted separately.





\section{Discussions}
\label{sec:discussion}
Table~\ref{tab:core_summary} summarizes the canonical numbers, but the scientific meaning of those numbers depends on what each released code path actually instantiates. The purpose of this section is therefore interpretive rather than descriptive. We use the archived solver configurations and stored metric files to clarify what each benchmark establishes, where alternative methods from the literature should still be expected to perform better, and which development priorities matter most for the next stage of the framework.

\subsection{Method-level comparison with alternative workflows}
Across the five examples, the main advantage of the atlas formulation is not that it has already displaced classical meshing, but that it changes where geometric effort is spent. In conventional finite-element workflows a difficult three-dimensional body is first turned into a volumetric mesh and then coupled to mature local basis functions and linear or nonlinear solvers \citep{de_souza_neto_computational_2011}. In the present code, geometry is instead represented analytically or by learned level sets and overlapping chart maps; interior collocation is generated by rejection sampling in those reference domains; and compatibility is enforced by interface penalties and multiplicative Schwarz sweeps. This makes the method particularly attractive when volumetric meshing or remeshing is itself the fragile part of the workflow, but it also means that approximation error, geometry error, and coupling error interact inside one optimization loop.

The closest PINN alternatives in the literature, such as XPINNs and FBPINNs \citep{shukla2021parallel,moseley2021fbpinns}, also use decomposition to improve training, yet their partitions are primarily algorithmic. In contrast, the present atlas is geometry-first: the same chart structure is used to represent the domain, to generate collocation, and to organize Schwarz communication. The rabbit benchmark is the clearest evidence that this idea works in practice, because the atlas gate report shows full coverage and zero foldover before the PDE solve begins, and the field solver then maintains cross-chart agreement through explicit interface-value and projected-flux terms. At the same time, the method remains more optimization-sensitive than classical mesh-based Schwarz, and the present evidence does not justify a claim of universal accuracy or efficiency superiority.

\subsection{Current limitations and future development priorities}
The repository still contains broader exploratory tracks, but the validated evidence suggests a clear priority order for future work. More ambitious inverse tracks, including the rabbit Elder and Arruda--Boyce variants, remain outside the main-text claims because their stabilization and interface control are not yet as clean as the five validated examples discussed above.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Nonlinear forward field solves on fixed atlases.} Examples~4 and~5 show that parameter identification under prescribed kinematics is already stable, and Example~5 demonstrates that the framework can handle incremental elastoplastic forward solves with Newton--Raphson equilibrium. The more important next step is a genuine atlas-based nonlinear equilibrium solve on a complex geometry (e.g., the rabbit), so that material nonlinearity, mapped operators, and Schwarz transmission are tested simultaneously on a topologically challenging domain.
\item \textbf{Stronger physics-only coupling for the rabbit family.} The rabbit benchmark already passes geometry gates and yields the best complex-geometry forward result in the repository, but it still depends on careful interface weighting, trust-region screening, and guided training design. Reducing that dependence, and eventually removing manufactured guidance from the strongest run, is the most direct route to a cleaner flagship result.
\item \textbf{Thin-feature geometry learning.} The Bunny losses show that near-surface fit is not the binding constraint; sign and Eikonal regularity are. Spatially adaptive importance sampling, local chartwise SDF refinement, and stronger volumetric priors should therefore take priority over simply increasing network size.
\item \textbf{Head-to-head baselines and portability.} Because the manuscript is intentionally code-faithful, it does not yet contain a fair comparison against a well-tuned mesh-based FEM baseline on the same geometries. Adding such baselines, together with stronger cross-backend reproducibility checks, would make the practical case for adoption much sharper.
\end{enumerate}

In that sense the present manuscript should be read neither as a rejection of classical meshing nor as a claim that every geometry problem should be solved by atlas-based PINNs. The stronger conclusion is more specific: when geometry acquisition, volumetric meshing, and remeshing are themselves difficult, the released code already demonstrates a credible geometry-first alternative whose present strengths and failure modes are both visible in the stored results.

\section{Conclusions} \label{sec:conclusions}
This paper presented an atlas-based SA-PINN framework for selected forward and inverse problems on complex three-dimensional geometries without volumetric meshing. The manuscript was written to stay close to the validated code, so the conclusions are intentionally bounded.

The validated evidence supports five main claims. First, mapped PINNs on simple global charts solve forward Poisson problems accurately on verification geometry, while the fixed-atlas rabbit Poisson example demonstrates that Schwarz-coupled chart-local field solves can be carried out successfully on a genuinely complex volumetric domain. Second, the Stanford Bunny neural-SDF benchmark shows that the same overall workflow can begin from a real PLY surface and learn a usable volumetric geometry model, although thin-feature sign control remains the main unresolved difficulty. Third, the torus Neo-Hookean benchmark shows that the inverse-identification machinery is already reliable in a tightly controlled synthetic material-parameter setting. Fourth, the torus elastoplastic benchmark demonstrates that the chart-local finite-element infrastructure extends to history-dependent constitutive laws: a smooth return mapping with softplus regularisation enables gradient-based identification of the von Mises yield stress ($0.25\%$ error from monotonic displacement data) and kinematic hardening modulus ($2.11\%$ error from cyclic reaction-force data), with robustness to initial-guess variation, observation noise, and mesh resolution.

The current boundaries of the method are just as important as the successes. The elastoplastic example is a constitutive parameter estimation problem under prescribed boundary conditions, not a full inverse boundary-value solve; extending it to genuine equilibrium-driven inverse problems on multi-chart atlases remains the most important next step. The most ambitious inverse problems in the repository still require additional stabilization, stronger interface control, or more informative observation models. Future work will therefore focus on improving interface transmission for chart-local inverse field solves, strengthening thin-feature geometry learning for imported PLY surfaces through spatially adaptive importance sampling, and improving cross-hardware reproducibility for the more delicate benchmarks. From a mathematical standpoint, the atlas-based pullback is operator-agnostic: the same local-coordinate machinery can be extended from Poisson problems to incompressible Navier--Stokes systems or to large-deformation elasticity once the appropriate flux and constitutive operators are substituted into the mapped residual. The immediate research value of the present framework is therefore not limited to diffusion; it is the reusable geometric infrastructure for carrying nonlinear field equations over overlapping volumetric charts.
