\begin{abstract}
Contact between geometrically complex bodies is governed, we argue, by how the boundary is represented
rather than by the scheme that enforces the contact constraint. On rough and non-convex geometry the gap,
the surface normal, and the active set are read from a model of the boundary, and a fixed-capacity ambient
level set smooths the asperities, cusps, and fine relief that carry the contact response; no downstream
penalty, augmented-Lagrangian, or mortar scheme can recover what the representation has discarded. We pose
contact detection as a boundary-to-boundary transition map, $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$, on a learned
coordinate atlas, from which the gap, a conservative surface normal, and every disjoint contact arc follow
without an ambient field. The charts are Fourier-feature coordinate networks whose random-feature embedding
defeats the spectral bias that low-passes a neural signed-distance function; the level set is retained
throughout as a matched baseline on identical meshes and tolerances. We cast the formulation as a
variational inequality with a frictional dissipation principle, and prove that the chart returns a normal
equal to the gradient of the gap, hence energy-conservative contact, together with a sign-exact active set.
The method is verified against six closed-form benchmarks (CV-1 Hertz, CV-2 Cattaneo--Mindlin, CV-3
Brazilian, CV-4 nine-disc, CV-5 superformula, CV-6 Koch) to a few percent, and validated on a real
Inada-granite tensile fracture, where dilation and apparent friction emerge from the resolved asperity
geometry under plain Coulomb friction with no imposed dilatancy law. The level set smooths the mean
asperity slope from $19.4^\circ$ to $12.5^\circ$ and under-predicts peak shear strength by $61\%$ and
emergent dilatancy by $98\%$, while the chart reconstructs the surface to $2.3~\mu$m against $107~\mu$m.
\keywords{computational contact mechanics \and coordinate atlas \and transition map \and
neural signed-distance function \and Fourier-feature charts \and rock-joint dilatancy}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

\subsection{Geometry, not the force law, controls contact fidelity}\label{sec:intro-motivation}

The shear strength of a rough rock joint is stored in its geometry. When the two faces of a tensile
fracture are pressed together and sheared, the load is carried on the interlocking asperities, and the
joint cannot slide without riding up their flanks; the climb dilates the interface and raises its
resistance above that of a flat sawcut. The strength an engineer designs with is therefore set by the
distribution of asperity slopes, a property of the boundary, and not by any material constant. This is the
setting in which the present paper makes a single claim: \emph{on rough and non-convex geometry the
representation of the boundary, not the constraint-enforcement scheme, controls contact fidelity.} We will
show that a fixed-capacity ambient signed-distance field smooths the mean asperity slope of a real
Inada-granite joint from $19.4^\circ$ to $12.5^\circ$ and, through the consequence that this entails for
the shear-strength law, under-predicts the peak strength by $61\%$ and the emergent dilatancy by $98\%$,
whereas a boundary chart of the same surface resolves the slopes and recovers the response.

A computational contact algorithm reduces, at each query point $\xbf$ near a body $X$, to two local
questions. Is $\xbf$ inside $X$, and by how much? This is the gap $\gn$, with the sign convention
$\gn<0\Leftrightarrow$ penetration used throughout. Along which direction is the surface? This is the
outward normal $\nbf$. Given the pair $(\gn,\nbf)$, a mature library of enforcement schemes---penalty,
augmented Lagrangian, mortar, Nitsche---converts it into nodal forces \citep{wriggers2006computational,
laursen2002computational,kikuchi1988contact}. On smooth, convex bodies this division of labour is benign:
the gap and the normal are smooth functions of position, every scheme converges to the same answer, and the
discussion centres on enforcement. On geometrically complex bodies it breaks down. The gap and the normal
cease to be smooth, or even single-valued, and the geometric representation from which they are read becomes
the dominant control on accuracy. A rock joint, a cusped cam lobe, a faceted aggregate, or a self-similar
fractal boundary carries contact information at length scales that an ambient field of fixed capacity cannot
store. We make the claim precise and give it a method: contact read from a chart \emph{transition map} on a
learned coordinate atlas, measured against the incumbent representation on a verification ladder that runs
from closed-form benchmarks to a real rock joint.

\subsection{The level set and its two intrinsic weaknesses}\label{sec:intro-levelset}

The incumbent representation is the level set. For a body $A$ one stores a signed-distance function
$\phi_A:\R^d\to\R$, negative inside the body, increasingly represented by a neural network, and reads the
gap and the normal directly from the field and its gradient,
\begin{equation}
  \gn(\xbf)=\phi_A(\xbf),\qquad
  \nbf(\xbf)=\frac{\grad\phi_A(\xbf)}{\norm{\grad\phi_A(\xbf)}},
  \label{eq:sdf-gap-intro}
\end{equation}
so a single automatic-differentiation call delivers both quantities at once \citep{liu2020implicit,
kolev2003level}. To render $\phi_A$ a true signed \emph{distance} one trains it with the eikonal penalty,
which drives the gradient toward unit norm,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eik}}
  =\mathbb{E}_{\xbf\sim\Omega}\!\left[\big(\norm{\grad\phi_A(\xbf)}-1\big)^2\right],
  \label{eq:eikonal}
\end{equation}
combined with surface and normal-alignment losses on a boundary point cloud. The economy of
\eqref{eq:sdf-gap-intro} is the appeal of the representation and also the source of its weaknesses. We
retain the level set throughout the paper as the comparison baseline. It is a fair and capable baseline,
not a strawman: on the geometry it can represent it is exactly as accurate as the chart
(Section~\ref{sec:verif}).

Two weaknesses are intrinsic to the level set rather than artefacts of training, and both bear directly on
contact. The first is \emph{medial-axis non-smoothness}. The exact Euclidean signed distance is only $C^0$
on the medial axis, the locus equidistant from two or more boundary points, where the closest-point map is
multivalued and $\grad\phi$ jumps. In a concavity the medial set reaches close to the surface, so the
gradient normal of \eqref{eq:sdf-gap-intro} degrades precisely where a non-convex contact forms. The second
is \emph{spectral bias}. A coordinate multilayer perceptron learns low frequencies first and high
frequencies slowly or never; its neural-tangent kernel is Laplace-like, with high-frequency power decaying
with frequency \citep{rahaman2019spectral}. A fixed-capacity neural $\phi_A$ therefore low-passes cusps,
asperities, and fine detail: it cannot represent a boundary sharper than its capacity ceiling, and it
smooths the geometry that, on a rough joint, carries the strength. We measure both weaknesses, rather than
assert them, in Sections~\ref{sec:verif} and~\ref{sec:cv7}. The chart introduced next is built to avoid
both: it stores one fewer dimension, and it supplies the network the high frequencies it would otherwise
fail to learn.

\subsection{Related work}\label{sec:intro-related}

\paragraph{Signed-distance and closest-point contact.}
Reading a gap and a normal from an implicit field is established practice. \citet{kolev2003level} treat the
contact interface as the zero level set of one body's field restricted to the other; the
implicit-level-set material point method of \citet{liu2020implicit} reads the normal gap and contact normal
from a per-body signed-distance representation, and our force machinery reuses its normal-gap form. The
node-to-surface projection of \citet{zavarise1998segment} and \citet{fischer2005frictionless} obtains a
smooth, differentiable correspondence by closest-point projection without explicit facet intersection, the
same smoothness the implicit field provides. It returns only a \emph{single} foot, however, which is the
kinematic limitation the transition map removes (Section~\ref{sec:tmap}).

\paragraph{Neural implicit geometry, spectral bias, and Fourier features.}
Neural signed-distance and occupancy networks \citep{park2019deepsdf} and periodic-activation networks
\citep{sitzmann2020implicit} represent geometry implicitly with a fixed-capacity network and inherit the
spectral bias characterised by \citet{rahaman2019spectral}: high-frequency detail is learned slowly or not
at all. The remedy we adopt is Fourier-feature input encoding \citep{tancik2020fourier,rahimi2007random},
which lifts the input through a fixed bank of sinusoids and flattens the neural-tangent kernel to a tunable
cutoff; the same encoding underlies the positional encoding of neural radiance fields
\citep{mildenhall2020nerf}. We use Fourier features not to sharpen an ambient field but to sharpen a
\emph{boundary} chart, which is one dimension lower and therefore far easier to fit to a prescribed cutoff
(Section~\ref{sec:fourier}).

\paragraph{Constraint enforcement.}
The forces that consume the gap and the normal are standard. We use penalty and augmented-Lagrangian normal
contact with regularised Coulomb friction \citep{wriggers2006computational,alart1991mixed,simo1992augmented},
set against the mortar \citep{puso2004mortar} and Nitsche \citep{chouly2013nitsche} families and the
variational-inequality foundations of \citet{kikuchi1988contact} and \citet{laursen2002computational}. These
schemes are orthogonal to our contribution: because each consumes only the gap, the normal, and the
normal-force magnitude, the chart and the level set are interchangeable at the force layer, and the geometry
is the only variable we change.

\paragraph{Rock-joint constitutive laws.}
The validation problem is the direct shear of a rough rock joint, for which a mature constitutive literature
supplies the closed-form anchor and the physical interpretation of the measured numbers. The bilinear law of
\citet{patton1966multiple} gives the apparent friction angle of a regularly serrated joint as $\phi_b+i$,
with $\phi_b$ the basic friction angle and $i$ the asperity inclination; the empirical model of
\citet{barton1977shear} and the review of \citet{barton1990review} extend this to natural roughness; the
asperity-degradation model of \citet{plesha1987constitutive} and the joint element of
\citet{goodman1976methods} supply the interface kinematics for cyclic shear. In the present framework the
dilatancy and the apparent friction are not imposed through such a law. They emerge from the resolved
asperity geometry, and the Patton law is the reference against which the emergent quantities are checked.

\subsection{Contributions and organisation}\label{sec:intro-contributions}

The contributions of this work are the following. We pose contact detection as a boundary-to-boundary
transition map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$ on a learned coordinate atlas, and place it within a
variational-inequality formulation of frictional contact whose dissipation principle the chart-driven solver
inherits unchanged (Sections~\ref{sec:govern}--\ref{sec:tmap}). We establish two analytical properties of the
chart detector: the normal it returns is the gradient of the gap, so the penalty traction is the exact
gradient of a potential and contact is energy-conservative; and the radial inverse-chart gap, though enlarged
in magnitude, is sign-exact, so the chart and the level set share an active set on star-shaped bodies
(Section~\ref{sec:tmap}). We instantiate the atlas through Fourier-feature coordinate charts and show, by a
kernel argument and a controlled ablation, that their random-feature embedding defeats the spectral bias that
ceilings a neural signed-distance function, so a $(d{-}1)$-dimensional boundary function resolves a prescribed
cutoff where a $d$-dimensional ambient field at fixed capacity cannot (Section~\ref{sec:fourier}). We verify
the method against a six-problem closed-form ladder, CV-1 through CV-6, on which the chart and the level set
are equivalent for the convex and superposable cases and measurably separate on the cusped superformula and
the self-similar Koch boundary (Section~\ref{sec:verif}). Finally, we validate it on the direct shear of a
real Inada-granite tensile fracture, where the apparent friction $\tan(\phi_b+i)$ and the normal dilation
emerge from the resolved asperity geometry under plain Coulomb friction, and where the level set, by smoothing
the asperity slopes, under-predicts strength and dilatancy on the quantity an engineer would design with
(Section~\ref{sec:cv7}). The detector composes with chart-based finite-element and material-point solvers
without modifying the force laws, so the measured accuracy gap is charged to the geometric representation
alone (Section~\ref{sec:discr}).

\paragraph{Notation.}
Bodies are labelled $A,B,\dots$; the interior of body $X$ is $\Omega_X\subset\R^d$ ($d=2,3$) and its boundary
$\partial\Omega_X$. A \emph{chart} is a parametrisation of a body's boundary, $\map{X}:\Theta_X\to
\partial\Omega_X$; a \emph{level set} is an ambient scalar field $\phi_X:\R^d\to\R$ whose zero set is
$\partial\Omega_X$. The transition map between two charted bodies is $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$. We write
the Macaulay bracket $\pos{z}=\max(0,z)$, bold symbols for vectors and tensors, and keep the sign convention
$\gn<0\Leftrightarrow$ penetration. The apparent friction coefficient measured from a contact run is $\muapp$,
compared against the Patton value $\tan(\phi_b+i)$.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:govern} states the strong and weak forms
of two-body frictional contact, casts equilibrium as a variational inequality, derives the frictional
dissipation principle, and adopts the regularised enforcement we take from the established literature.
Section~\ref{sec:tmap} develops the transition map on a coordinate atlas and proves the conservative matched
normal, the sign-exact active set, and the multi-arc enumeration. Section~\ref{sec:fourier} constructs the
Fourier-feature charts and bounds the resolvable asperity slope through the network kernel.
Section~\ref{sec:discr} discretises the regularised problem in chart-based finite-element and material-point
settings. Section~\ref{sec:verif} verifies the method against the closed-form ladder CV-1 through CV-6, and
Section~\ref{sec:cv7} validates it on the real Inada-granite joint. Section~\ref{sec:disc} states the
preconditions, limitations, and conclusions.

\section{Governing equations and the variational inequality of frictional contact}\label{sec:govern}

We pose frictional contact as a constrained minimisation of the total energy, derive the Signorini
conditions as its optimality conditions, and state the dissipation principle that the tangential law obeys.
The constraint enforcement that follows---penalty and augmented Lagrangian for the normal pressure,
regularised Coulomb for friction---is classical and is recalled here only to fix notation. The single point
this section establishes for the remainder of the paper is that the enforcement consumes the geometry solely
through the pair $(\gn,\nbf)$. Force assembly, the contact tangent, the broad phase, and the well-posedness
estimate are identical whether $(\gn,\nbf)$ comes from a learned atlas or from a level set, so the accuracy
gap reported in Sections~\ref{sec:verif}--\ref{sec:cv7} is attributable to the geometric representation
alone.

We consider two deformable bodies $\Omega^A,\Omega^B\subset\R^d$ undergoing displacements
$\ubf^A,\ubf^B$ in the space $V$ of admissible fields, meeting along a candidate contact surface $\Gamma_c$
in the deformed configuration. The normal gap $\gn(\xbf)$ is the signed distance from a candidate contact
point to the obstacle surface, with $\gn<0$ in penetration; the outward unit normal $\nbf$ points away from
the obstacle, so the contact traction $-t_N\nbf$ with pressure $t_N\ge0$ restores a penetrating point.

\subsection{Constrained minimisation, the variational inequality, and the Signorini conditions}
\label{sec:govern-vi}

Let each body carry a polyconvex stored-energy density $W(\bm F)$ with the usual coercivity and growth
bounds; the Neo-Hookean and small-strain models used in this paper qualify \citep{ball1977convexity,
ciarlet1988mathematical}. The total energy is the stored elastic energy less the work of the external loads,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:energy}
E(\vbf)=\sum_{\alpha\in\{A,B\}}\int_{\Omega^\alpha}W\!\big(\bm I+\grad\vbf^\alpha\big)\,dV
\;-\;\sum_{\alpha\in\{A,B\}}L^\alpha(\vbf^\alpha),
\qquad \vbf=(\vbf^A,\vbf^B),
\end{equation}
and non-penetration confines the admissible displacements to the closed convex cone
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cone}
\mathcal K=\big\{\,\vbf\in V:\ \gn(\vbf)\ge 0\ \text{on }\Gamma_c\,\big\}.
\end{equation}
Equilibrium is the constrained minimisation $\ubf=\arg\min_{\vbf\in\mathcal K}E(\vbf)$. Because $E$ is convex
and coercive on $\mathcal K$ and $\mathcal K$ is a closed convex cone, the minimiser is characterised by a
variational inequality: the directional derivative of $E$ is non-negative into every admissible direction,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:vi}
\ubf\in\mathcal K:\qquad
\big\langle E'(\ubf),\,\vbf-\ubf\big\rangle\;\ge\;0\qquad\forall\,\vbf\in\mathcal K .
\end{equation}
The unilateral constraint makes \eqref{eq:vi} an inequality rather than the equation $E'(\ubf)=0$, and the
active contact set is itself an unknown of the problem \citep{kikuchi1988contact,laursen2002computational}.
Introducing the Lagrange multiplier $t_N\ge0$ conjugate to the gap recovers the pointwise contact
conditions.

\begin{proposition}[Variational inequality and the Signorini conditions]\label{prop:vi-kkt}
The variational inequality \eqref{eq:vi} is equivalent to the existence of a pressure multiplier $t_N\ge0$,
supported on $\Gamma_c$, such that the stationarity and the Signorini--Hertz--Moreau (Karush--Kuhn--Tucker)
conditions hold,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weak-contact}
\sum_{\alpha\in\{A,B\}}\!\Big[a^\alpha(\ubf^\alpha,\delta\ubf^\alpha)-L^\alpha(\delta\ubf^\alpha)\Big]
+\int_{\Gamma_c} t_N\,\delta\gn\,dA=0\quad\forall\,\delta\ubf\in V,
\end{equation}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:signorini}
\gn\ \ge\ 0,\qquad t_N\ \ge\ 0,\qquad \gn\,t_N\ =\ 0\qquad\text{on }\Gamma_c,
\end{equation}
where $a^\alpha(\ubf^\alpha,\delta\ubf^\alpha)=\int_{\Omega^\alpha}\bm P(\bm F^\alpha):\grad\delta\ubf^\alpha\,dV$
is the internal virtual work, $\bm P$ the first Piola--Kirchhoff stress, and the first variation of the gap is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gap-variation}
\delta\gn=\nbf\cdot(\delta\ubf^B-\delta\ubf^A).
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Convexity of $E$ on the convex set $\mathcal K$ makes $\ubf$ a minimiser if and only if the variational
inequality \eqref{eq:vi} holds. The constraint $\gn\ge0$ is a cone constraint; Lagrangian duality on
$\Gamma_c$ produces a multiplier $t_N\ge0$ with stationarity of
$\mathcal L(\vbf,t_N)=E(\vbf)-\int_{\Gamma_c}t_N\,\gn(\vbf)\,dA$, which is \eqref{eq:weak-contact} once
$\delta\gn$ is taken from \eqref{eq:gap-variation}, together with primal feasibility $\gn\ge0$, dual
feasibility $t_N\ge0$, and complementary slackness $\gn t_N=0$, that is \eqref{eq:signorini}. The gap
variation \eqref{eq:gap-variation} follows because the gap is measured along $\nbf$, so to first order it
responds only to the relative motion projected onto the normal, irrespective of how the surface is
represented.
\end{proof}

Equation~\eqref{eq:gap-variation} is the hinge of the entire comparison: the contact virtual work depends on
the geometry through the normal $\nbf$ alone, so once the representation supplies $(\gn,\nbf)$, nothing in
the variational statement distinguishes a chart from a level set.

\subsection{Frictional dissipation and the principle of maximum dissipation}\label{sec:govern-friction}

Friction resists tangential sliding and is the only dissipative mechanism in the interface. Writing
$\vbf_T=(\bm I-\nbf\otimes\nbf)\vbf$ for the tangential slip velocity and $\tbf_T$ for the tangential
traction, the mechanical dissipation rate is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dissipation}
\mathcal D=-\,\tbf_T\cdot\vbf_T\ \ge\ 0,
\end{equation}
and thermodynamic admissibility requires $\mathcal D\ge0$ pointwise. The Coulomb law confines $\tbf_T$ to the
friction disk $\mathcal C(t_N)=\{\bm\sigma_T:\norm{\bm\sigma_T}\le\mu\,t_N\}$, with $\mu$ the friction
coefficient, and selects the traction by a maximum-dissipation principle analogous to that of
rate-independent plasticity \citep{simo1998computational}.

\begin{proposition}[Maximum frictional dissipation and a thermodynamically consistent regularisation]
\label{prop:maxdiss}
Among all admissible tractions $\bm\sigma_T\in\mathcal C(t_N)$, the one that maximises the dissipation
$-\bm\sigma_T\cdot\vbf_T$ is the Coulomb slip traction,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:maxdiss}
\tbf_T=\arg\max_{\bm\sigma_T\in\mathcal C(t_N)}\big(-\bm\sigma_T\cdot\vbf_T\big)
=-\mu\,t_N\,\frac{\vbf_T}{\norm{\vbf_T}}\quad(\vbf_T\neq\bm 0),
\end{equation}
and it dissipates $\mathcal D=\mu\,t_N\norm{\vbf_T}\ge0$. The smooth regularisation derived from the convex
dissipation pseudo-potential
\begin{equation}\label{eq:friction-potential}
\Psi_T(\vbf_T)=\mu\,t_N\sqrt{\norm{\vbf_T}^2+\varepsilon_T^2},\qquad
\tbf_T=-\frac{\partial\Psi_T}{\partial\vbf_T}
=-\mu\,t_N\,\frac{\vbf_T}{\sqrt{\norm{\vbf_T}^2+\varepsilon_T^2}},
\end{equation}
is itself dissipative,
$\mathcal D=\mu\,t_N\,\norm{\vbf_T}^2/\sqrt{\norm{\vbf_T}^2+\varepsilon_T^2}\ge0$, with equality only at
$\vbf_T=\bm 0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The objective $-\bm\sigma_T\cdot\vbf_T$ is linear in $\bm\sigma_T$ over the disk $\mathcal C(t_N)$, so its
maximum is attained on the boundary, anti-parallel to $\vbf_T$; the Karush--Kuhn--Tucker conditions of the
constrained maximisation give \eqref{eq:maxdiss} and the associated normality $\vbf_T\in
N_{\mathcal C}(\tbf_T)$. For the regularisation, $\Psi_T$ is convex in $\vbf_T$, so
$\tbf_T=-\partial\Psi_T/\partial\vbf_T$ defines a generalised standard material; contracting $\tbf_T$ with
$\vbf_T$ yields the stated non-negative dissipation.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Second-law consistency of the interface]\label{cor:second-law}
The total interfacial dissipation is non-negative,
$\dot{\mathcal D}_{\mathrm{tot}}=-\int_{\Gamma_c}\tbf_T\cdot\vbf_T\,dA\ge0$, whichever representation supplies
the normal $\nbf$. Consequently a chart-driven contact solve is thermodynamically consistent, a property
confirmed numerically by the closure of the cyclic energy ledger in Section~\ref{sec:cv7}.
\end{corollary}

The parameter $\varepsilon_T$ is a small slip-velocity scale that rounds the corner of the Coulomb law at
$\vbf_T=\bm 0$: for $\norm{\vbf_T}\gg\varepsilon_T$ the traction approaches the cone bound $\mu t_N$ and the
law behaves as sharp slip, while for $\norm{\vbf_T}\ll\varepsilon_T$ it grows linearly through zero, so stick
is approximated by a stiff tangential spring. Because the law of \eqref{eq:friction-potential} reads only the
scalar normal pressure $t_N$, it composes unchanged with either normal enforcement below.

\subsection{Regularised enforcement of the variational inequality}\label{sec:govern-enforce}

The pressure multiplier $t_N$ of Proposition~\ref{prop:vi-kkt} is supplied by a regularisation of the
non-penetration constraint. The penalty method replaces the indicator of $\mathcal K$ by a one-sided
quadratic potential active only on penetration,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:penalty-potential}
\Pi_c(\gn)=\tfrac12\,\varepsilon_n\,\pos{-\gn}^2,\qquad
t_N=\frac{\partial\Pi_c}{\partial(-\gn)}=\varepsilon_n\,\pos{-\gn},
\end{equation}
with penalty stiffness $\varepsilon_n>0$. The augmented-Lagrangian method of \citet{alart1991mixed} and
\citet{simo1992augmented} adds a persistent multiplier $\lambda\ge0$ updated by an Uzawa iteration,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:al-pressure}
t_N=\pos{\lambda-\varepsilon_n\,\gn},\qquad
\lambda^{k+1}=\pos{\lambda^{k}-\varepsilon_n\,\gn(\ubf^{k+1})},
\end{equation}
so that at convergence the multiplier recovers the exact pressure, $\lambda\to t_N^\star$, and the penetration
vanishes without $\varepsilon_n\to\infty$. The plain penalty \eqref{eq:penalty-potential} is the special case
$\lambda\equiv0$. Both regularisations are instances of the same variational relaxation.

\begin{proposition}[Penalty and augmented Lagrangian as relaxations of the variational inequality]
\label{prop:moreau}
The penalty potential \eqref{eq:penalty-potential} is the Moreau--Yosida regularisation, with parameter
$\varepsilon_n^{-1}$, of the indicator of the admissible half-line $\{\gn\ge0\}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:moreau}
\Pi_c(\gn)=\min_{s\ge0}\Big\{\tfrac{\varepsilon_n}{2}(\gn-s)^2\Big\}=\tfrac12\,\varepsilon_n\,\pos{-\gn}^2 ,
\end{equation}
so the penalised energy $E+\int_{\Gamma_c}\Pi_c\,dA$ is the Moreau envelope of $E+\chi_{\mathcal K}$, whose
minimiser $\ubf_\varepsilon$ converges to the variational-inequality solution $\ubf^\star$ as
$\varepsilon_n\to\infty$, with the rate of \eqref{eq:penalty-estimate} below. The augmented-Lagrangian update
\eqref{eq:al-pressure} is the proximal-point (Uzawa) iteration on the dual of the same Lagrangian and
contracts, $\norm{\lambda^{k+1}-\lambda^\star}\le\rho\,\norm{\lambda^{k}-\lambda^\star}$ with $\rho<1$, for
every fixed $\varepsilon_n>0$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The minimisation in \eqref{eq:moreau} over $s\ge0$ is attained at $s=\max(0,\gn)$, giving $\tfrac{\varepsilon_n}{2}\pos{-\gn}^2$, which is by definition the Moreau--Yosida envelope of the indicator
$\chi_{[0,\infty)}$; epi-convergence of the Moreau envelope as $\varepsilon_n\to\infty$ delivers convergence
of minimisers. The Uzawa update is the proximal-point map of the dual functional; firm non-expansiveness of
the projection $\pos{\cdot}$ yields the contraction with $\rho<1$ \citep{laursen2002computational}.
\end{proof}

We close with the standard stability statement, which carries over unchanged because the contact treatment is
classical and the chart alters only how $\gn$ is evaluated. Under the polyconvexity and coercivity hypotheses
on $W$, the energy $E$ attains a minimiser on $\mathcal K$ by the direct method, and adding the non-negative
convex penalty $\Pi_c$ preserves both weak lower semicontinuity and coercivity, so the penalised problem is
well posed for every $\varepsilon_n>0$. The penalty solution $\ubf_\varepsilon$ approaches the multiplier
solution $\ubf^\star$ at the rate of \citet{kikuchi1988contact},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:penalty-estimate}
\norm{\ubf_\varepsilon-\ubf^\star}_{H^1}\le\frac{C}{\sqrt{\varepsilon_n}},\qquad
\norm{t_{N,\varepsilon}-t_N^\star}_{H^{-1/2}(\Gamma_c)}\le C\sqrt{\varepsilon_n},
\end{equation}
so balancing the penalty error against the $O(h)$ discretisation error fixes the stiffness at
$\varepsilon_n\sim E/h$, while the augmented Lagrangian removes the trade-off through Proposition~\ref{prop:moreau}. Collecting the contributions, the contact-pressure error splits as
$\norm{t_{N,h}-t_N^\star}\le C_1 h + C_2\,\varepsilon_n^{-1/2}+C_3\,\epsilon_{\mathrm{geom}}$, in which the
first two terms are common to both representations and the third, the geometric approximation error
$\epsilon_{\mathrm{geom}}$ of the oracle, is the term that Sections~\ref{sec:verif} and~\ref{sec:cv7} measure
and on which the atlas and the level set part company.

\section{Contact as a transition map on a coordinate atlas}\label{sec:tmap}

The pair $(\gn,\nbf)$ that drives the formulation of Section~\ref{sec:govern} is read from a model of the
boundary, and two such models are available. A \emph{level set} stores an ambient scalar field
$\phi_X:\R^d\to\R$ whose zero set is $\partial\Omega_X$, with the interior $\Omega_X\subset\R^d$ open and
$\gn<0$ in penetration. A \emph{chart} parametrises the boundary directly, $\map{X}:\Theta_X\to
\partial\Omega_X$. This section develops the chart description and the boundary-to-boundary transition map
that reads contact from it, and establishes the two analytical properties on which the chart's advantage
rests: the normal it returns is the gradient of the gap, so the contact force is conservative
(Proposition~\ref{prop:matched-normal}); and the radial gap it returns is sign-exact, so its active set
coincides with the level set's on star-shaped bodies (Proposition~\ref{prop:active-set}). The level set
enters only as the comparison baseline.

\subsection{Boundary-chart and ambient level-set representations}\label{sec:tmap:two}

\subsubsection{The ambient level set}

The level set stores a signed-distance function, a scalar field over all of space whose value is the distance
to the surface, negative inside and positive outside. For body $A$ a neural network $\phi_A:\R^d\to\R$
approximates this field, and the gap and the outward normal follow from the field and its normalised gradient,
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sdf-gap}
\gn^A(\xbf)=\phi_A(\xbf),\qquad
\nbf_A(\xbf)=\frac{\grad\phi_A(\xbf)}{\norm{\grad\phi_A(\xbf)}}.
\end{equation}
%
so both quantities follow from a single automatic-differentiation call \citep{liu2020implicit,
kolev2003level}. As a fair baseline the network is trained as a genuine signed distance, minimising the
eikonal residual together with surface, normal-alignment, and sign-anchor terms on a boundary point cloud,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sdf-objective}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{SDF}}
=\underbrace{\mathbb E_{\Omega}\big[(\norm{\grad\phi}-1)^2\big]}_{\text{eikonal}}
+\lambda_s\underbrace{\mathbb E_{\partial\Omega}\,\abs{\phi}}_{\text{surface}}
+\lambda_n\underbrace{\mathbb E_{\partial\Omega}\big[1-\widehat{\grad\phi}\cdot\nbf_{\mathrm{gt}}\big]}_{\text{normal alignment}}
+\lambda_e\underbrace{\mathbb E_{\Omega\setminus\partial\Omega}\,e^{-\beta\abs{\phi}}}_{\text{sign / far field}},
\end{equation}
with $\nbf_{\mathrm{gt}}$ the sampled surface normal and a $\tanh$ multilayer-perceptron architecture
\citep{park2019deepsdf}. The two intrinsic weaknesses of this representation---medial-axis non-smoothness in
concavities and the spectral-bias ceiling of a fixed-capacity coordinate network
\citep{rahaman2019spectral}---are those identified in Section~\ref{sec:intro-levelset}; both are properties of
the description rather than of the training, and Section~\ref{sec:fourier} makes the second quantitative.

\subsubsection{The boundary chart}

The chart stores only the boundary, as a function of the boundary's own parameter. Three chart families
appear in this work, in order of increasing generality. The \emph{radial chart}
$\rho_X:\Sph^{d-1}\to\R^+$ is a height field on the sphere of directions, recording for each unit direction
the distance from the centre to the surface; it represents star-shaped bodies, those visible in their entirety
from one interior point. With centre $c_X$ and orientation $Q_X$ (the body axes as columns), the boundary
point in direction $\hat\ubf$ is $\map{X}(\hat\ubf)=c_X+Q_X\,\rho_X(\hat\ubf)\,\hat\ubf$. The \emph{height
chart} $h_X:\mathcal D\to\R$ over an open parameter domain $\mathcal D$ is the graph $z=h(x)$ of a
single-valued rough surface, such as one face of a rock joint. The \emph{decoder chart}
$D_X:\bm\xi\mapsto\xbf$ is a boundary-fitted volumetric map for general, possibly non-star-shaped geometry,
inverted by Newton iteration.

A chart is cheaper to fit than a level set because its radius or height function lives one dimension below the
ambient field, models no interior, and carries no medial axis. The cost is a precondition on the body
geometry---star-shapedness for radial charts, single-valuedness for height charts---and a gap measured along a
ray rather than perpendicular to the surface, examined in Section~\ref{sec:tmap:bias}. The benefit is
fidelity: a $(d{-}1)$-dimensional boundary function is far easier to resolve to a prescribed cutoff than a
$d$-dimensional ambient field at fixed capacity, as Section~\ref{sec:fourier} quantifies.
Figure~\ref{fig:tm-levelset-vs-chart} contrasts the two descriptions.

\subsection{The transition map}\label{sec:tmap:def}

Let $\map{A}:\Theta_A\to\partial\Omega_A$ and $\map{B}:\Theta_B\to\partial\Omega_B$ be boundary charts of two
bodies. The \emph{transition map} carries a boundary point of $A$ to the boundary parameter of $B$ with which
it coincides in physical space,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tmap-def}
\tmap:\ \theta_A\ \longmapsto\ \psi_B=\big(\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}\big)(\theta_A).
\end{equation}
In \eqref{eq:tmap-def}, $\map{A}$ maps the parameter $\theta_A$ to the physical point $\xbf=\map{A}(\theta_A)$
on $\partial\Omega_A$, and $\map{B}^{-1}$ returns the boundary parameter $\psi_B$ on $\partial\Omega_B$ that
the same point projects to. The construction is the transition map of differential geometry, the change of
coordinates $\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$ between two charts of an atlas, applied here across two bodies to read
their contact correspondence (Figure~\ref{fig:tm-concept}).

The contact normal follows from the chart geometry rather than from a signed-distance gradient. In three
dimensions two surface tangents span the contact point, and their normalised cross product is the outward unit
normal,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:jacobian-normal}
\nbf=\frac{\tbf_1\times\tbf_2}{\norm{\tbf_1\times\tbf_2}},\qquad
\tbf_\alpha=\frac{\partial\map{A}}{\partial\xi^\alpha}.
\end{equation}
The normal is objective: under a rigid-body motion $\xbf\mapsto Q\xbf+\bm c$ the chart and its tangents rotate
with $Q$, so $\nbf(Q\xbf+\bm c)=Q\,\nbf(\xbf)$, as a contact normal must transform. The undeformed normal gap
is then the chart form of the implicit level-set normal gap \citep{liu2020implicit},
$g_{n0}=(\map{}^{(j)}-\map{}^{(i)})\cdot\nbf$, the surface-to-surface offset projected onto $\nbf$.

\subsubsection{The inverse-chart gap}\label{sec:tmap:invgap}

Detection requires only a signed gap and an outward normal at a query point, and on a star-shaped body the
transition map supplies both in closed form. Expressing the query point in the body frame of $B$,
$\bm d=Q_B^\top(\xbf-c_B)$, and splitting it into a direction and a length gives the inverse-chart gap as a
single radius subtraction,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:radial-gap}
\dhat=\frac{Q_B^\top(\xbf-c_B)}{\norm{Q_B^\top(\xbf-c_B)}},\qquad
\gn^B(\xbf)=\underbrace{\norm{Q_B^\top(\xbf-c_B)}}_{r}-\rho_B(\dhat),
\end{equation}
where $\dhat$ is the unit direction from the centre of $B$ to the query point, $r$ the radial distance, and
$\rho_B(\dhat)$ the chart radius in that direction; the gap is negative exactly when the point lies inside,
and it is globally single-valued and $C^1$ wherever $\rho_B$ is. Equation~\eqref{eq:radial-gap} inverts the
roles of field and boundary that the level set assumes: where the signed-distance function stores a field
over $\R^d$ and reports the distance to the nearest surface in any direction, the transition map stores only
$\partial\Omega_B$ and reports, by inversion, which boundary point the query corresponds to and whether it
lies inside. On star-shaped and single-valued geometry the latter is both cheaper to store and sharper to
fit.

In two dimensions the radial chart can carry an analytic radius, the Gielis superformula, which generates a
wide family of closed boundaries from a few parameters,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:superformula-chart}
\rho(\theta)=\Big(\big\lvert\tfrac1a\cos\tfrac{m\theta}{4}\big\rvert^{n_2}
+\big\lvert\tfrac1b\sin\tfrac{m\theta}{4}\big\rvert^{n_3}\Big)^{-1/n_1},
\end{equation}
in which $\theta$ is the polar angle, $m$ sets the number of lobes, and $a,b,n_1,n_2,n_3$ shape them; the
inverse chart is the angle $\theta=\angle\,Q_B^\top(\xbf-c_B)$ of the body-frame offset
\citep{gielis2003generic}. The analytic radius and a trained neural radius feed the identical gap
\eqref{eq:radial-gap}: the evaluator reproduces the closed-form gap to $10^{-10}$, and the trained neural
radius reproduces the analytic gap to $3.8\times10^{-3}L$ on the cusped superformula
(Section~\ref{sec:verif}), so the neural chart is a drop-in replacement for the analytic one.

\subsection{The matched conservative normal}\label{sec:tmap:matched}

The normal returned with the gap must equal the gradient of the gap, or the penalty force fails to be the
gradient of a potential and contact dissipates energy spuriously, contradicting
Corollary~\ref{cor:second-law}. The radial gap $F(\xbf)=r-\rho(\dhat)$ depends on $\xbf$ through a unit
direction, so the raw direction gradient $\grad_{\hat d}\rho$ carries a spurious radial component to which
$\rho$ is insensitive; projecting it onto the tangent plane of the direction sphere removes that component,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tangential-proj}
\grad_S\rho=(I-\dhat\dhat^\top)\,\grad_{\hat d}\rho.
\end{equation}

\begin{proposition}[Conservative matched normal]\label{prop:matched-normal}
With the tangential projection \eqref{eq:tangential-proj}, the body-frame gap gradient and the world-frame
normal are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:matched-normal}
\grad_d F=\dhat-\frac{\grad_S\rho}{r},\qquad
\nbf=\widehat{\,Q\,\grad_d F\,},
\end{equation}
and the returned normal is the unit gap gradient, $\grad_{\xbf}F=\norm{\grad_d F}\,\nbf$. Consequently the
penalty traction over a tributary volume $V$ is the exact gradient of the penalty potential of
\eqref{eq:penalty-potential},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:conservative-force}
\fbf=-\grad_{\xbf}\!\Big(\tfrac12\varepsilon_n\pos{-F}^2 V\Big)
=\varepsilon_n\pos{-F}\,V\,\norm{\grad_d F}\,\nbf,
\end{equation}
so chart-driven normal contact is energy-conservative. Using the un-projected gradient $\grad_{\hat d}\rho$
in place of \eqref{eq:tangential-proj} gives $\nbf\neq\widehat{\grad_{\xbf}F}$, and the resulting force field
has non-zero curl, so its circulation around a closed path does not vanish.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Differentiate $F=r-\rho(\dhat)$ with $r=\norm{Q^\top(\xbf-c)}$. The radial term gives $\grad r=Q\dhat$, and
since $\partial\dhat/\partial(Q^\top\xbf)=r^{-1}(I-\dhat\dhat^\top)$ the chain rule gives
$\grad[\rho(\dhat)]=r^{-1}Q(I-\dhat\dhat^\top)\grad_{\hat d}\rho=r^{-1}Q\,\grad_S\rho$. Collecting terms,
$\grad_{\xbf}F=Q(\dhat-\grad_S\rho/r)=Q\,\grad_d F$, hence $\nbf=\widehat{\grad_{\xbf}F}$. Because the penalty
potential depends on $\xbf$ only through $F$, the chain rule yields \eqref{eq:conservative-force}. Replacing
$\grad_S\rho$ by the un-projected $\grad_{\hat d}\rho$ breaks the identity $\nbf=\widehat{\grad F}$, so the
force is not a gradient and its circulation is generically non-zero.
\end{proof}

The projection \eqref{eq:tangential-proj} is load-bearing for every non-spherical body, where
$\grad_S\rho\neq\bm 0$. The forces that consume the matched triple $(\gn,\nbf,\norm{\fbf_N})$ are exactly
those of Section~\ref{sec:govern}, so the chart and the level set are interchangeable at the force layer and
only the geometry changes. On geometry both representations capture, this interchangeability is exact: on a
sphere the radial chart reproduces the signed distance, gap and normal, to machine precision at every depth
and orientation, and substituting the chart detector into a live two-sphere material-point collision is
bit-identical in velocity, impulse, and penetration. The chart never does worse than the level set where both
apply; it separates only where the level set degrades.

\subsection{Multi-arc enumeration}\label{sec:tmap:multiarc}

A boundary scan over $\map{A}$ enumerates every contact patch, a kinematic property the single foot of a
closest-point projection cannot reproduce.

\begin{lemma}[Completeness of the boundary scan]\label{lem:multiarc}
Let $A$ carry a boundary chart $\map{A}:\Theta_A\to\partial\Omega_A$ and let $B$ be star-shaped with radial
gap $\gn^B$. The chart active set $\mathcal A=\{\theta_A\in\Theta_A:\gn^B(\map{A}(\theta_A))<0\}$ is open, and
the boundary scan recovers every connected component of $\mathcal A$: if $\mathcal A=\bigsqcup_{k=1}^{m}
\mathcal A_k$ is a union of $m$ disjoint arcs, the scan returns all $m$. A single closest-point projection
returns the global minimiser $\arg\min_{\theta_A}\gn^B$ and hence at most one component.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
The composition $\gn^B\circ\map{A}$ is continuous, being the $C^1$ chart composed with the continuous radial
gap \eqref{eq:radial-gap}; $\mathcal A$ is its open sublevel set $\{\,\cdot<0\}$, and an open subset of the
parameter circle (in $d=2$) is a countable disjoint union of open arcs, each a contact patch. Evaluating
$\gn^B$ on a dense sweep marks at least one sample in every arc, so all $m$ components are detected.
Closest-point projection solves a single global minimisation and returns one point, in the lowest-valued
component only.
\end{proof}

The scan evaluates the inverse-chart gap \eqref{eq:radial-gap} at each $\theta_A$ and marks the parameters with
$\gn^B<0$, a one-dimensional sweep over $\theta$ in two dimensions or a geodesic cap scan in three.
Figure~\ref{fig:tm-multiarc} shows the consequence on a non-convex pair: the scan reports two disjoint contact
arcs where a single closest-point projection returns only the globally nearest foot and misses the second
contact, which matters for cams, gears, and any geometry that touches in more than one place. On the cusped
superformula of Section~\ref{sec:verif} the chart scan reports $\ge2$ arcs against the projection's one.
Conventional node-to-surface and mortar schemes recover disjoint patches by segment search instead
\citep{zavarise1998segment,puso2004mortar,fischer2005frictionless}.

\begin{figure}

\subsection{Inverting the chart}\label{sec:tmap:invert}

The cost of the transition map is dominated by the inverse chart $\map{B}^{-1}$, which takes three forms of
increasing work. For a \emph{radial chart} the inverse is closed form: the boundary parameter is the
direction $\psi=\angle\,Q_B^\top(\xbf-c_B)$ in two dimensions, or the normalised body-frame offset in three,
at $O(1)$ cost per query. For a \emph{height chart} there is nothing to invert. The boundary is the graph
$\map{}(x)=(x,h(x))$, so the domain coordinate $x$ is already the inverse-chart coordinate and the gap is a
single height comparison,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:height-gap}
g_{\mathrm{vert}}(x,z)=z-h_\theta(x),\qquad
\nbf=\frac{(-h_\theta'(x),\,1)}{\sqrt{1+h_\theta'^2(x)}},
\end{equation}
with the normal tilting from vertical in proportion to the surface slope $h_\theta'(x)$; the
three-dimensional analogue is $\nbf=(-h_x,-h_y,1)/\sqrt{1+\norm{\grad h}^2}$. This is the form used by the
rough rock-joint detector of Section~\ref{sec:cv7}, which therefore costs $O(1)$ per query with no Newton
solve. For a general \emph{decoder chart} $D_B(\bm\xi)$ no closed form exists, and the inverse is the
parameter whose image equals the query point, found by Newton iteration on the residual $D_B(\bm\xi)-\xbf$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:newton-invert}
\bm\xi^{(k+1)}=\bm\xi^{(k)}-J^{-1}\big(D_B(\bm\xi^{(k)})-\xbf\big),\qquad
J=\frac{\partial D_B}{\partial\bm\xi},
\end{equation}
with $J$ the decoder Jacobian assembled by automatic differentiation. The solve is stabilised by flooring the
singular values of $J$ and keeping its determinant consistent with the clamped spectrum, which guards against
a near-folded chart; the iteration starts from the linear local-coordinate projection and terminates at
$\norm{D_B(\bm\xi)-\xbf}<10^{-8}$.

\subsection{A conservative-large but sign-exact gap}\label{sec:tmap:bias}

The radial gap is cheaper than a Euclidean distance because it is measured along the ray from the centre
rather than perpendicular to the surface, and that choice enlarges its magnitude on tilted flanks. The
following proposition makes the enlargement precise and shows that, despite it, the chart and the level set
share an active set on star-shaped bodies.

\begin{proposition}[Sign-exact radial gap and active-set equivalence]\label{prop:active-set}
Let $\Omega_B$ be star-shaped about $c_B$ with $C^1$ radial chart $\rho_B$. For a query point $\xbf$ let
$g_{\mathrm{rad}}=r-\rho_B(\dhat)$ be the radial gap \eqref{eq:radial-gap} and $g_\perp$ the signed
perpendicular (Euclidean) gap, and let $\alpha$ be the angle between the ray $\dhat$ and the outward normal
$\nbf$ at the radial foot. Then $\cos\alpha=\dhat\cdot\nbf>0$ and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:radial-bias}
g_{\mathrm{rad}}=\frac{g_\perp}{\cos\alpha}+O\!\big(g_\perp^2\big)
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad
\abs{g_{\mathrm{rad}}}\ge\abs{g_\perp},
\end{equation}
and, because the multiplier $1/\cos\alpha$ is positive, $\operatorname{sign}g_{\mathrm{rad}}=
\operatorname{sign}g_\perp$. Hence the chart and the level-set active sets coincide,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:active-set}
\{\xbf:g_{\mathrm{rad}}(\xbf)<0\}=\{\xbf:g_\perp(\xbf)<0\}=\Omega_B .
\end{equation}
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
Star-shapedness about $c_B$ means every ray from $c_B$ meets $\partial\Omega_B$ exactly once, so the outward
normal at the foot cannot be orthogonal to the inward ray and $\cos\alpha=\dhat\cdot\nbf>0$, i.e.\
$\alpha\in[0,\pi/2)$. Dropping a perpendicular from $\xbf$ to the tangent plane at the radial foot forms a
right triangle whose hypotenuse is the along-ray segment of length $\abs{g_{\mathrm{rad}}}$ and whose leg
along $\nbf$ is $\abs{g_\perp}$, with included angle $\alpha$; to first order $g_\perp=g_{\mathrm{rad}}
\cos\alpha$, the surface curvature entering at $O(g_\perp^2)$. Since $\cos\alpha\in(0,1]$, multiplication by
$1/\cos\alpha$ enlarges the magnitude and preserves the sign, and sign preservation gives \eqref{eq:active-set}.
\end{proof}

The enlargement is benign. The sign is exact, so the penalty activates over precisely the true penetrating set
(Figure~\ref{fig:tm-radial-bias}); the magnitude is conservative-large, so an exterior point reads a
marginally larger gap and a penetrating point a marginally deeper penetration, and the penalty is at worst
slightly stiffer, never softer. The value equals the Euclidean distance only on the sphere and in the limit
$g\to0$. The radial field is $C^1$ but not eikonal: $\norm{\grad g}$ can reach $\sim10^4$ in deep concave
valleys, where the radial normal rotates rapidly. A perpendicular-foot refinement recovers the normal-projected
distance but is used only for verification, since that foot jumps across the medial axis and would violate
Corollary~\ref{cor:second-law} inside the integrator. The chart detector is thus an opt-in geometric input that
leaves the force laws, the broad phase, and the discretisations of Section~\ref{sec:discr} untouched; floors
and half-spaces, not star-shaped about any finite centre, remain on the signed-distance path. For height charts
the analogous statement is that \eqref{eq:height-gap} is the small-slope-exact normal-projected gap rather than
the closest-point distance, so the fidelity there lives in the asperity slopes, not the gap magnitude, as
Section~\ref{sec:cv7} measures.

\section{Fourier-feature charts and the resolution of asperity geometry}\label{sec:fourier}

The transition map of Section~\ref{sec:tmap} reads contact off the boundary charts, so its accuracy is the
accuracy of those charts as representations of the geometry. A chart that smooths the asperity slopes returns
a smoothed gap and a smoothed normal, and by the variational structure of Section~\ref{sec:govern} no
downstream enforcement scheme can recover the lost detail. This section constructs the charts and isolates the
single ingredient that lets a small network resolve sharp, rough, cusped boundaries that an ambient level set
and a plain coordinate network alike smooth away: a fixed Fourier-feature encoding of the input. The level set
is retained as a fair baseline, since it too is a coordinate network and inherits the same spectral bias as a
plain chart.

\subsection{Spectral bias as a kernel statement}\label{sec:spectral}

A coordinate network is a function $f_\theta:\R^d\to\R$ of affine maps and $\tanh$ nonlinearities applied to
the raw coordinate. Both objects this paper compares are of this form: an ambient neural signed-distance
function $\phi_\theta(\xbf)$ of the spatial point, and a boundary chart $h_\theta(x)$ of the surface
parameter. Both regress onto sampled data, and both inherit the spectral bias of \citet{rahaman2019spectral}.

\begin{proposition}[Spectral bias of a coordinate network]\label{prop:ntk}
Under gradient flow on the mean-squared loss, a wide coordinate multilayer perceptron has a
translation-invariant, Laplace-like neural-tangent kernel whose spectrum decays as
$\lambda(k)\sim\norm{k}^{-(d+1)}$. Diagonalising training in the Fourier basis, each mode of the residual
relaxes independently,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ntk-decay}
\hat f_\theta(k,t)-\hat z(k)\ \approx\ \big(\hat f_\theta(k,0)-\hat z(k)\big)\,e^{-\eta\,\lambda(k)\,t},
\qquad \lambda(k)\sim \norm{k}^{-(d+1)},
\end{equation}
with $\eta$ the learning rate. Hence at any finite training budget $t$ the recovered field is low-pass: modes
with $\norm{k}\gtrsim(\eta t)^{1/(d+1)}$ are essentially unlearned, and a fixed-capacity network cannot
represent a boundary sharper than that cutoff.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
In the neural-tangent-kernel regime, training is kernel regression with the network kernel. A stationary
kernel is diagonalised by the Fourier modes $e^{2\pi\mathrm{i}k\cdot x}$ with eigenvalues $\lambda(k)$, so the
modes decouple and each relaxes as $e^{-\eta\lambda(k)t}$. The arccosine kernel of a $\tanh$ or ReLU network
has the power-law spectrum $\lambda(k)\sim\norm{k}^{-(d+1)}$, so high $\norm{k}$ relaxes arbitrarily slowly.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:ntk} is the failure mode of the ambient level set, and it carries a mechanical
consequence. A neural signed-distance function flattens the asperity flanks of a rock joint; through the
strength law $\muapp=\tan(\phi_b+i)$ of \citet{patton1966multiple} a flattened mean asperity angle $i$
under-predicts the apparent friction, so the kernel cutoff of \eqref{eq:ntk-decay} imposes a ceiling on the
representable joint strength, derived in Section~\ref{sec:cv7}. A boundary chart of the \emph{raw} coordinate
inherits the identical pathology: changing the domain of $f_\theta$ from the ambient field to the surface
parameter does not change its spectral bias. The chart parametrisation alone does not beat the level set; the
Fourier features do.

\subsection{The Fourier-feature encoding and its kernel}\label{sec:banks}

The remedy of \citet{tancik2020fourier} and \citet{rahimi2007random} replaces the raw coordinate by a fixed,
non-trainable bank of sinusoids applied before the network. With the input normalised to the unit box,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:normalize}
\tilde x = \frac{2(x-x_{\mathrm{lo}})}{x_{\mathrm{hi}}-x_{\mathrm{lo}}}-1\in[-1,1]^d,
\end{equation}
and a bank $B\in\R^{K\times d}$ of $K$ frequency vectors, the encoding and the chart are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma}
\gamma(x)=\big[\cos(2\pi B\tilde x),\ \sin(2\pi B\tilde x)\big]\in\R^{2K},
\qquad
h_\theta(x)=\mathrm{base}+\mathrm{MLP}_\theta\!\big(\gamma(x)\big).
\end{equation}
The bank $B$ is fixed for the life of the chart; only the network weights $\theta$ and the scalar offset
$\mathrm{base}$, which carries the mean height so the network regresses only the deviation, are trained.
Prepending $\gamma$ replaces the power-law kernel of \eqref{eq:ntk-decay} by a stationary, band-limited one.

\begin{proposition}[Stationary Fourier-feature kernel and band limit]\label{prop:rff}
The feature map \eqref{eq:gamma} induces the stationary kernel
\begin{equation}\label{eq:kernel}
k(\tilde x,\tilde x')=\gamma(\tilde x)\cdot\gamma(\tilde x')
=\sum_{j=1}^{K}\cos\!\big(2\pi\,b_j\cdot(\tilde x-\tilde x')\big),
\end{equation}
depending only on the separation $\tilde x-\tilde x'$. For the deterministic geometric bank $\{f_k\}$ it is
the band-limited sum $\tfrac1K\sum_k\cos(2\pi f_k(\tilde x-\tilde x'))$, flat to the cutoff $f_{\max}$. For the
Gaussian bank $b_j\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2 I_d)$ the expected kernel is, by Bochner's theorem, the isotropic
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gauss-kernel}
\mathbb E_B\big[k(\tilde{\bm u},\tilde{\bm v})\big]
=\exp\!\big(-2\pi^2\sigma^2\norm{\tilde{\bm u}-\tilde{\bm v}}^2\big)
\;\xrightarrow{\;d=2,\ \text{radial}\;}\;
J_0\!\big(2\pi\sigma\norm{\tilde{\bm u}-\tilde{\bm v}}\big),
\end{equation}
flat to the cutoff $\sim 2\pi\sigma$. The induced kernel thus has a flat passband below an explicit, tunable
cutoff, so every mode below cutoff is learned at a comparable rate.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The identity $\cos a\cos a'+\sin a\sin a'=\cos(a-a')$ gives \eqref{eq:kernel} term by term. For the Gaussian
bank, Bochner's theorem identifies the expected kernel with the Fourier transform of the spectral measure; for
$b\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2 I)$ the Gaussian characteristic function gives
$\mathbb E[\cos(2\pi b\cdot\bm\Delta)]=e^{-2\pi^2\sigma^2\norm{\bm\Delta}^2}$, whose two-dimensional radial
profile is $J_0(2\pi\sigma\norm{\bm\Delta})$.
\end{proof}

\begin{corollary}[Dimensional capacity advantage]\label{cor:capacity}
To resolve a finest wavelength $\ell$ over a domain of size $L$ with a flat passband, a $(d{-}1)$-dimensional
boundary chart tiles a $(d{-}1)$-cube of frequency space, needing $K\sim(L/\ell)^{d-1}$ features, whereas a
$d$-dimensional ambient field tiles a $d$-cube and needs $K\sim(L/\ell)^{d}$. The boundary function meets a
prescribed cutoff with a factor $L/\ell$ fewer features, and at fixed capacity resolves geometry the ambient
field cannot.
\end{corollary}

Two properties make the encoding the right tool for geometry. The cutoff is explicit and tunable through
$f_{\max}$, $\sigma$, or the harmonic order $K$, where the level set's smoothing is an intrinsic property of
its architecture; and the bank is deterministic and frozen, so the encoding is a reproducible preprocessing
of the input rather than a learned, run-dependent representation. Corollary~\ref{cor:capacity} is borne out
by the measured Inada reconstruction---$2.3~\mu$m for the chart against $107~\mu$m for the ambient
signed-distance function at more parameters (Section~\ref{sec:cv7}).

This paper uses three charts, differing only in the domain each occupies and the bank it uses to tile
frequency space; the training loop is identical across all three. The geometric bank gives equal coverage on
a logarithmic frequency axis, suited to a profile with asperities spread across many wavelengths; the
Gaussian bank gives isotropic two-dimensional coverage, suited to a height field over a patch; and the
harmonic bank is periodic by construction, so a chart wrapped around the circle closes on itself.

\subsection{Training and the verify-first protocol}\label{sec:train}

Every chart is trained by the same procedure: normalize the input by \eqref{eq:normalize}, encode with the
fixed bank \eqref{eq:gamma}, then regress the network onto sampled surface points by minibatch
mean-squared error,
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mse}
\mathcal L(\theta)=\frac{1}{\abs{\mathcal B}}\sum_{i\in\mathcal B}\big(h_\theta(x_i)-z_i\big)^2,
\end{equation}
%
with Adam at learning rate $2\times10^{-3}$ and a cosine-annealing schedule. The networks are small and are
trained in double precision, which conditions the geometry and the Jacobians used downstream, with a fixed
random seed for reproducibility. Training uses only mean-squared-error regression, without curriculum or loss
weighting, because the Fourier encoding rather than the optimiser carries the high frequencies.

A trained chart \emph{is} geometry, and a corrupted chart silently corrupts every downstream solve, so a
chart is admitted to a contact computation only after three checks. (i) \emph{Reconstruction fidelity}: the
RMSE on the sampled surface points, reported as a fraction of the surface RMS, must reach a few percent.
(ii) \emph{No element foldover}: for the volumetric decoder the chart-Jacobian determinant must satisfy
$\det J>0$ on every element, since a folded chart has no valid pushforward. (iii) \emph{Discretization
convergence}: a manufactured-solution study on the curved chart geometry must recover the $O(h^2)$ rate of
the underlying P1 chart finite-element method, confirming that the trained geometry did not spoil the
solver. A chart that fails, with relief amplitude too high so $\det J$ folds, or cutoff too low so the
surface is under-resolved, is rejected, not patched.

The Fourier contribution is isolated by a controlled ablation on the real Inada-granite tensile-fracture
profile \citep{inada2020digitalrocks}, a self-affine surface with RMS $\approx 1.7$~mm. The same network, of
identical width, depth, optimiser, and schedule, is trained twice on the measured points: once with the
Fourier features active and once on the raw coordinate, the latter serving as the spectral-bias control.
Because the profile is a deterministic surface, the metric is the reconstruction RMSE on the measured points,
and the bank is band-limited below the sampling Nyquist so the fit is well posed.
Figure~\ref{fig:curves} reports the two training curves and the recovered surfaces. With the Fourier features
the chart descends through the asperity scales to a reconstruction RMSE of $0.003\%$ of the RMS and recovers
the ablation window's mean slope angle $\bar i = 19.9^\circ$ (the full-profile capstone of
Section~\ref{sec:cv7} measures the surface mean as $19.4^\circ$). The identical network on the raw coordinate
plateaus at a spectral-bias floor of $7.6\%$ of RMS ($136~\mu$m) and low-passes the slopes to
$\bar i = 11.5^\circ$. The two networks differ only in the input encoding, yet one converges to the surface
while the other stalls at a smoothed approximation: the representation thesis reduced to its mechanism, on a
real surface.

The same separation holds for the boundary-fitted volumetric decoder used by the chart finite-element method
of Section~\ref{sec:discr}, where the relief is carried by the Gaussian bank. The Fourier decoder reconstructs
the rough face to $2.2\%$ of RMS, a vanilla $\tanh$ decoder of equal capacity reaches only $48\%$, and an
ambient three-dimensional neural signed-distance function of the same surface reaches $15.7\%$; the decoder
additionally passes $\det J>0$ on every element and the $O(h^2)$ manufactured-solution check (measured rates
$2.07$ and $1.83$). On the one-dimensional Inada profile the production height chart ($K=192$, $f_{\max}=1500$)
reconstructs the surface to $2.3~\mu$m, about $0.1\%$ of the $1.7$~mm RMS, while the ambient neural
signed-distance function reaches only $107~\mu$m---$47\times$ worse with \emph{more} parameters---and smooths
the mean asperity angle from $19.4^\circ$ to $12.5^\circ$. Through the Patton law (Section~\ref{sec:cv7}) this
smoothing under-predicts the peak shear strength by $61\%$: the asperity slopes the chart preserves are
precisely the geometry that drives the contact mechanics.

\subsection{Why charts resolve what the level set smooths}\label{sec:why}

The advantage is a property of the kernel, not of the parametrisation. The level set is a coordinate network
of the ambient point and carries the Laplace-like kernel of Proposition~\ref{prop:ntk}, whose spectral ceiling
is an intrinsic, non-tunable consequence of the architecture; on rough or cusped geometry it filters away
exactly the high frequencies that contact requires. The Fourier-feature chart carries the stationary,
band-limited kernel of Proposition~\ref{prop:rff}, with a cutoff set explicitly to resolve the finest
wavelength of interest and no spectral ceiling below it, and by Corollary~\ref{cor:capacity} it does so with a
factor $L/\ell$ fewer features than the ambient field. The chart also stores geometry as a surface function,
so its cost scales with the surface rather than the ambient volume, but the fidelity argument rests on the
kernel.

The ablation above is the load-bearing evidence that the Fourier features, not the chart parametrisation
alone, do the work: a chart of the raw coordinate smooths the Inada profile to the same $7.6\%$ floor and the
same $11.5^\circ$ angle as the level set, because it inherits the same spectral bias, and only the prepended
bank brings the reconstruction to $0.003\%$ and the slope angle to its true value. The claim is therefore
narrow and exactly what the measurements support. We do not assert that a boundary chart has a lower height
RMSE than a level set on a well-sampled, single-valued surface; dense interpolation is easy for both. We
assert that the level set smooths the asperity \emph{slopes} through an intrinsic spectral ceiling and thereby
under-predicts strength and dilatancy, while a Fourier-feature chart with a tunable cutoff resolves those
slopes. The scope is that height and surface charts represent single-valued geometry, the harmonic radial
chart requires star-shaped boundaries, and the band limit is a chosen resolution. Within that scope the
encoding converts a smoothing approximator into a faithful one, and every downstream result in this paper
rests upon it.

\section{Discretisation in chart-based finite-element and material-point settings}\label{sec:discr}

The regularised problem of Section~\ref{sec:govern} is discretised on two settings that share the same force
expressions and the same $(\gn,\nbf)$ oracle: a chart-based finite-element method and a chart-based material
point method. They differ only in how they carry the geometry of the chart, and the contrast isolates a
bookkeeping subtlety in each. Throughout, the geometric input enters at a single place, which is what makes the
atlas-versus-level-set comparison controlled.

\subsection{Chart-finite-element pushforward and the consistent contact tangent}\label{sec:discr-fem}

The chart finite-element method solves the elasticity problem on a reference parametric cube $U_i=[-r,r]^d$ and
maps it to the curved physical body through the chart decoder $\map{i}$, whose Jacobian
$\bm J_i=\partial\map{i}/\partial\bm\xi$ relates reference and physical gradients. Gradients on the cube push
forward to physical gradients by the chain rule,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fem-pushforward}
\grad_{\xbf}\ubf=\grad_{\bm\xi}\ubf\,\bm J_i^{-1},\qquad
\bm F=\bm I+\grad_{\bm\xi}\ubf\,\bm J_i^{-1},
\end{equation}
and the change-of-variables factor $\abs{\det\bm J_i}$ turns the cube integral into the physical stiffness,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fem-assembly}
\bm K_{ab}=\int_{U_i}\big(\grad_{\bm\xi}N_a\,\bm J_i^{-1}\big)^{\!\top}
:\mathbb C:\big(\grad_{\bm\xi}N_b\,\bm J_i^{-1}\big)\,\abs{\det\bm J_i}\,d\bm\xi,
\end{equation}
with $N_a$ the shape functions and $\mathbb C$ the elasticity tensor. A steep neural chart can render $\bm J_i$
nearly singular; the inverse and determinant are therefore formed through a singular-value-clamped operator that
floors the singular values, while $\det\bm J_i>0$ (no fold-over) is monitored on every element. On the
boundary-fitted rough geometry the pushforward passes a patch test and recovers the manufactured-solution rate
$O(h^2)$ (measured exponents $2.07$ and $1.83$), so the discretisation error is controlled independently of the
contact treatment. The penalty force $\fbf^{\,\mathrm N}=\varepsilon_n\pos{-\gn}A\,\nbf$ of
Section~\ref{sec:govern} enters the chart residual work-conjugately, with $A$ the physical tributary area.

A Newton solve requires the derivative of the contact force with respect to the displacement. At an active node
the contact tangent gathers a normal stiffness, a friction--normal coupling, and a curvature term,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:contact-tangent}
\bm K_c=\frac{\partial\fbf_{\mathrm{contact}}}{\partial\ubf}
=\underbrace{\varepsilon_n A\,(\nbf\otimes\nbf)}_{\text{normal stiffness}}
+\underbrace{\varepsilon_n A\,\mu\,(\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}\otimes\nbf)}_{\text{friction--normal coupling}}
+\underbrace{t_N A\,\frac{\partial\nbf}{\partial\ubf}}_{\text{curvature}} .
\end{equation}
Because $\nbf$ tilts with the asperity slope, the rank-one block $\nbf\otimes\nbf$ couples the normal and
tangential directions on a tilted asperity, where a flat-interface model would couple none; the
friction--normal block is non-symmetric, the structural signature of a non-associative friction law. The
curvature term carries the rotation of $\nbf$ as the surface deforms, the shape operator (Weingarten map) of
the contact surface, supplied by the surface description through its second derivative. For the level set it is
the SDF Hessian, and for the height chart it is the graph curvature,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shape-operator}
\frac{\partial n_i}{\partial x_j}\bigg|_{\mathrm{SDF}}
=\frac{1}{\norm{\grad\phi}}\big(\delta_{ik}-n_i n_k\big)\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial x_k\partial x_j},
\qquad
\frac{\partial\nbf}{\partial\ubf}\bigg|_{\mathrm{chart}}
=-\frac{h_\theta''(x)}{(1+h_\theta'^2)^{3/2}}\,(\tbf\otimes\tbf).
\end{equation}
On the smooth single-asperity benchmarks the curvature term is small, and the rank-one normal and
friction--normal blocks with an Armijo line search drive the frictional Newton residual to $\sim10^{-9}$. The
exception is the two-deformable, moving-master node-to-surface problem, where the contact point itself moves and
the active set is non-smooth; there the residual settles at $1.5$--$6\%$ and does not shrink under refinement,
a non-smoothness to be cured by a semismooth-Newton, augmented, or mortar treatment
\citep{alart1991mixed,puso2004mortar,chouly2013nitsche} rather than a finer mesh.

\subsection{Material-point coupling in physical space}\label{sec:discr-mpm}

The chart material point method stores particle velocity, gravity, and stress directly in physical space. The
\emph{contact} force therefore needs no Jacobian pull-back: it scatters to the grid through the same
particle-to-grid channel as gravity,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mpm-p2g}
\fbf_I^{\mathrm{ext}}\ \mathrel{+}=\ \sum_p \fbf_p^{\,\mathrm{contact}}\,N_I(\bm\xi_p),
\qquad
\fbf_p^{\,\mathrm{contact}}=\varepsilon_n\,\pos{-\gn(\xbf_p)}\,V_p\,\nbf_p+\tbf_T\,V_p,
\end{equation}
with $N_I$ the grid shape function, $V_p$ the particle volume, and $\xbf_p=\map{i}(\bm\xi_p)+\ubf_p$ the
particle position; forces and grid velocities already live in the same physical space, so the contact force is
added verbatim. The \emph{constitutive} update on a curved chart, by contrast, does require the chart Jacobian.
The reference velocity gradient must be pulled back to physical space, and the internal force scattered through
the physical shape-function gradient,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mpm-pullback}
\widetilde{\bm L}_p=\sum_I \vbf_I^{\mathrm{phys}}\,(\grad_{\bm\xi}N_I)(\bm\xi_p),\qquad
\bm L_p=\widetilde{\bm L}_p\,\bm J_p^{-1},\qquad
\fbf_I^{\mathrm{int}}=-\sum_p V_p\,\bm\sigma_p\,\big(\bm J_p^{-\top}\grad_{\bm\xi}N_I\big),
\end{equation}
with $\bm J_p=\partial\map{i}/\partial\bm\xi$. The contact channel of \eqref{eq:mpm-p2g} is exempt precisely
because $\fbf_p^{\,\mathrm{contact}}$ is already a physical vector scattered by $N_I$, not by its gradient. The
penalty spring adds a stability bound to the explicit step, $\Delta t_{\mathrm{contact}}\le0.5\sqrt{m_{\min}/
\varepsilon_n}$, so the effective step is $\min(\Delta t_{\mathrm{CFL}},\Delta t_{\mathrm{contact}})$. The
explicit path reproduces the Coulomb floor, returning $\muapp\approx0.34$ against an imposed $\mu=0.4$, but a
mated rough-on-rough block is unstable in explicit penalty dynamics, so the material point method serves as a
dynamic cross-check rather than the primary rough-joint solver (Section~\ref{sec:cv7}).

\subsection{The geometry as the single controlled variable}\label{sec:discr-dispatch}

The geometry enters the solver at exactly one place. A query of a batch of physical points returns the pair
$(\gn,\nbf)$ from one of two evaluators,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:detector}
(\gn,\nbf)=
\begin{cases}
\big(\phi_A(\xbf),\ \grad\phi_A/\norm{\grad\phi_A}\big), & \text{level set},\\[4pt]
\big(\gn^{\mathrm{chart}}(\xbf),\ \nbf^{\mathrm{chart}}(\xbf)\big), & \text{transition map},
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
the second being the radial detector of Section~\ref{sec:tmap} for star-shaped bodies, or the height-chart gap
$\gn=z-h_\theta(x)$ with normal $(-h_\theta',1)/\sqrt{1+h_\theta'^2}$ for single-valued joints. Both honour the
same contract: a signed gap with $\gn<0$ in penetration and a unit outward normal. Everything downstream---the
broad-phase cull, the active-set selection $\{\gn<0\}$, the penalty and augmented-Lagrangian forces, the
friction law, the consistent tangent \eqref{eq:contact-tangent}, and both discretisations---reads only
$(\gn,\nbf)$ and cannot tell which branch produced it. Substituting the atlas for the level set therefore
changes one branch of \eqref{eq:detector} and nothing else, so the same solver, loading, and tolerances are
applied to two geometries and the measured accuracy gap is charged to the geometry alone. On the real
Inada-granite joint the chart returns a contact gap with $4.2\%$ RMS error against the analytic surface where
the ambient signed-distance function returns $44.7\%$, a factor of $10.5$ on the quantity that drives contact,
at $1.47\times$ the per-query cost and with no ambient field to store; Section~\ref{sec:cv7} traces this
difference into the predicted strength and dilatancy.

\section{Verification: closed-form hierarchy (CV-1--CV-6)}\label{sec:verif}

We verify the method against six closed-form benchmarks, ordered as an ablation of geometric
difficulty. The first four problems, CV-1 through CV-4, are convex, collinear, or superposable: their
closed forms are linear combinations of known elastic kernels, and on them the radial chart of
\S\ref{sec:tmap} reduces to the ambient level set. The last two, CV-5 and CV-6, break that
equivalence. A cusped non-convex superformula and a self-similar fractal force the transition map and
the neural signed-distance function (SDF) apart, and we measure the separation. We report both regimes
with equal weight. The cases in which the chart buys nothing are not a weakness to hide; they fix the
baseline, so that the CV-5 and CV-6 separation cannot be read as a tuned advantage.

Two conventions hold throughout. We write $\gn<0$ for penetration, where $\gn$ is the normal gap of
\S\ref{sec:tmap}. Each benchmark is solved on a learned geometry---a trained neural signed-distance function,
a trained radial chart, or, for the elastostatic problems, a learned chart-decoder domain map---and compared
against the analytical reference of Appendix~A. Because a neural object can be no more accurate than the
geometry on which it was trained, the targets below are matched, not exceeded, and we neither expect nor claim
machine precision.

Each subsection states the physical problem, names the detector property it tests, gives the closed-form
acceptance target, describes the solve, and reports the measured result with its consequence for the
chart-versus-level-set comparison.

\subsection{The equivalence regime: CV-1--CV-4 (convex / superposable)}\label{sec:verif_equiv}

The first four benchmarks are the elastic and packing baselines, and they share one feature: each
reduces to the superposition of known closed forms \citep{johnson1985contact,timoshenko1970elasticity}.
None presents a non-convex, non-collinear, or curved-surface correspondence to compute, so none
exercises the transition-map machinery. On a convex indenter the radial inverse-chart gap reduces to
the SDF gap, and the two representations are interchangeable at the detector. We state this plainly
because it bounds the claim of the paper: the chart's advantage is specific to hard geometry, and these
four problems are not hard geometry. Their role is to certify the chart-FEM solver and to pin down the
floor that the transition map must clear before any claim of advantage.

\subsubsection{CV-1 (Hertz line contact)} Two elastic cylinders pressed together along a line form a
contact patch whose half-width grows with the load. This is the canonical convex contact, and it tests
whether the chart-FEM solver and a neural-SDF indenter reproduce the elastic kernel where chart and
level set agree. The closed form for the half-width under plane strain is
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hertz_a}
a = 2\sqrt{\frac{P'R}{\pi E^{*}}},\qquad
\frac{1}{E^{*}} = \frac{1-\nu_1^2}{E_1}+\frac{1-\nu_2^2}{E_2},
\end{equation}
%
where $a$ is the contact half-width, $P'$ the load per unit thickness, $R$ the relative radius of the
two surfaces, $E^{*}$ the reduced modulus, and $E_i,\nu_i$ the Young's moduli and Poisson ratios of the
two bodies. Equation~\eqref{eq:hertz_a} predicts two checks: a constant ratio $a/\sqrt{P'}$ at fixed
$E^{*}$, and the scaling $a\propto (E^{*})^{-1/2}$. To exercise both, we drive a chart-FEM block against
a neural-SDF disc indenter under penalty contact and sweep $E^{*}$ over a factor of eight. The measured
ratio holds the closed form to $\sim 1.6\%$ across the sweep. The contact pressure $p_0 = 2P'/\pi a$ is
the half-ellipse identity, not an independent check, so we do not count it. As expected on a convex body, the
neural signed-distance indenter matches the exact-SDF baseline rather than improving on it, and chart and level
set coincide. The agreement holds at the field level, not only in the
half-width: Figure~\ref{fig:cv1-hertz} shows the chart-FEM subsurface vertical stress, whose stress bulb
and off-surface maximum-shear depth $z/a\approx0.48$ reproduce the closed-form Hertz field.

\subsubsection{CV-2 (Cattaneo--Mindlin partial slip)} A tangential load below the friction limit, applied
to a Hertz patch, splits the contact into a central zone that sticks and an outer annulus that slips.
This is the simplest frictional contact, and it tests whether the friction layer reproduces the
stick/slip partition on a geometry where chart and level set still agree. With a tangential load
$Q<\mu P$ (where $\mu$ is the friction coefficient and $P$ the normal load) on the CV-1 contact, the
two-dimensional line-contact partition obeys
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cattaneo}
\frac{c}{a} = \sqrt{\,1-\frac{Q}{\mu P}\,},
\end{equation}
%
the planar reduction of the Mindlin compliance law
\citep{mindlin1949compliance,johnson1985contact}, where $c$ is the radius of the stick zone and $a$ the
contact half-width of CV-1. We measure $c$ against \eqref{eq:cattaneo} on a deep half-plane, since block
shear does not reproduce the edge-singular tangential traction. The stick radius matches to $\sim
5$--$7\%$ at low $Q$, and the error rises to a mean of $\sim 11\%$ as $Q\to\mu P$. Near the friction
limit the slip boundary sweeps across the contact faster than the node spacing can resolve, so the
discrete stick radius coarsens. CV-2 inherits the CV-1 contact and adds only a friction layer, so the
transition map stays dormant and the two representations remain equivalent. Figure~\ref{fig:cv2-traction}
shows the recovered contact tractions: the elliptical Hertz normal pressure and the Cattaneo--Mindlin
tangential traction, whose stick/slip transition at $r=c$ marks the boundary between the locked core and
the slipping annulus.

\subsubsection{CV-3 (Brazilian disc)} A disc compressed across a diameter develops a tensile stress
transverse to the load and a compressive stress along it. This is a pure stress boundary-value problem
with no contact detector engaged, and it tests the chart-FEM stress recovery on a learned disc
geometry. For a disc of diameter $D$ and thickness $t$ under pole load $P$, the center stress state is
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:brazilian}
\sigma_{xx} = +\frac{2P}{\pi D t}\quad\text{(tensile)},\qquad
\sigma_{yy} = -\frac{6P}{\pi D t}\quad\text{(compressive)},
\end{equation}
%
with $\sigma_{xx}$ the stress transverse to the load and $\sigma_{yy}$ the stress along it. This state
follows from superposing two Flamant load-point fields and a uniform biaxial tension that restores a
traction-free rim \citep{hondros1959evaluation,timoshenko1970elasticity}. The chart-FEM solve
reproduces the center stresses of \eqref{eq:brazilian} to $1.62\%$ in $\sigma_{xx}$ and $0.58\%$ in
$\sigma_{yy}$ against the exact closed form. The constant-strain triangulation leaves a $\sim 1.6\%$
center floor that plateaus under mesh refinement, a discretization error rather than a modeling error,
and we exclude the pole singularity from the comparison. With no detector engaged, the chart contributes only
the domain map, and chart and level set are again interchangeable. Figure~\ref{fig:cv3-brazilian}
shows the recovered $\sigma_{yy}$ field over the disc, with the compressive column under the loaded
diameter and the transverse tensile band that splits the specimen, both following the Flamant
superposition.

\subsubsection{CV-4 (nine-disc unit cell)} A $3\times3$ array of discs under isotropic confinement loads
each interior disc through four equal contacts at its poles. This is a packing problem whose symmetry
collapses it to a superposition of Brazilian solutions, and it tests whether the charts add by
superposition across multiple contacts. No diagonal contacts form, because $2\sqrt2\,R>2R$; by $D_4$
symmetry every disc sees four equal inward loads $N$ at its north, south, east, and west poles, and
reflection parity makes friction inactive. The unit cell is then the superposition of two perpendicular
Brazilian solutions, with the equibiaxial center stress
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:nine_disc}
\sigma_{xx} = \sigma_{yy} = -\frac{2N}{\pi R t},
\end{equation}
%
where $N$ is the per-pole contact load, $R$ the disc radius, and $t$ the thickness. The chart-FEM unit
cell reproduces \eqref{eq:nine_disc} to $0.15\%$ in $\sigma_{xx}$ and $0.01\%$ in $\sigma_{yy}$, with a
measured in-plane anisotropy of $0.16\%$ and a center shear stress below the solver tolerance. Two
caveats accompany the result: the equal-$N$ collapse is exact only for rigid loading platens, and this
is the per-disc unit cell rather than a full $N$-body explicit-contact solve, so the contacts only
place the load poles. The charts add by superposition exactly as in CV-3, and chart and level set stay
equivalent. Figure~\ref{fig:cv4-ninedisc} shows the maximum-principal and shear-stress
fields across the array; the pattern repeats identically from disc to disc and is equibiaxial at each
center, the spatial signature of the symmetry that the closed form assumes.

Across CV-1--CV-4 the learned geometry reproduces the contact-mechanics closed forms to $\sim
0.15$--$1.6\%$ (CV-1, CV-3, CV-4) and to $\sim 5$--$11\%$ for frictional partial slip (CV-2), with the
radial chart and the level set equivalent throughout. This is the floor the transition map must clear.
It clears the floor here without buying anything, by construction. The next two benchmarks are where it
starts to earn its place.

\subsection{CV-5: the cusped non-convex superformula, the discriminating test}\label{sec:verif_cv5}

CV-5 is the first benchmark the transition map is built for. A rotating Gielis-superformula cam drives a
free non-convex follower, and the cam's lobe tips are cusps. The problem tests whether the chart can
hold a sharp normal where the ambient SDF cannot. The cam boundary is the superformula radius
\citep{gielis2003generic}
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:superformula}
\rho(\theta) = \left(\,\left|\tfrac{1}{a}\cos\tfrac{m\theta}{4}\right|^{n_2}
+\left|\tfrac{1}{b}\sin\tfrac{m\theta}{4}\right|^{n_3}\,\right)^{-1/n_1},
\end{equation}
%
where $\rho(\theta)$ is the boundary radius at polar angle $\theta$, $m$ sets the number of lobes,
$a,b$ scale the two axes, and $n_1,n_2,n_3$ shape the lobes; for $n_1<1$ the lobe extrema sharpen into
cusps. This geometry attacks both intrinsic weaknesses of the level set at once. The cusps trigger the
network's spectral bias, and the concave valleys between lobes carry a medial axis on which even the
exact Euclidean SDF gradient is only $C^0$. The boundary chart sidesteps both: it is a one-dimensional
function $\rho(\theta)$ on the circle of directions, with no ambient field and no medial axis, so it
stays sharp where the ambient field cannot.

We compare three detectors that all consume the matched (gap, normal) contract of \S\ref{sec:tmap}: the
analytical chart, a trained Fourier-feature radial chart $\rho_\theta:\Sph^1\to\R^{+}$, and a neural
SDF of the same shape. For the radial chart, the inverse-chart gap and its conservative normal are
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:radial_gap_cv5}
\gn(\xbf) = \norm{Q^{\top}(\xbf-\bm{c})} - \rho(\dhat),\qquad
\nbf = \widehat{\,Q\,(\dhat - \grad_{\!S}\rho/r)\,},\qquad
\dhat = \frac{Q^{\top}(\xbf-\bm{c})}{r},
\end{equation}
%
where $\xbf$ is the query point, $\bm{c}$ the cam center, $Q$ the rotation to body frame, $r =
\norm{Q^{\top}(\xbf-\bm{c})}$ the radial distance in that frame, $\dhat$ the unit direction to the
query, and $\grad_{\!S}\rho=(I-\dhat\dhat^{\top})\grad_{\dhat}\rho$ the surface gradient of $\rho$
projected onto the tangent direction. The tangential projection is load-bearing: it strips the spurious
radial component from the raw gradient, so the penalty force is exactly the gradient of a potential.

\paragraph{Measured separation.} On the cusped superformula the neural SDF reaches a gap error of
$8\times10^{-3}\,L$ ($L$ the cam length scale) with a degraded normal: the median out-of-plane
component is $\lvert n_z\rvert\sim 0.5$, tilting the normal off the two-dimensional surface. This is the
spectral-bias ceiling, reached by design. The neural radial chart reaches a gap error of
$3.8\times10^{-3}\,L$ and a median normal-angle error of $0.42^{\circ}$, roughly halving the gap error
and recovering an in-plane normal. The chart also resolves a kinematic property the level set's single
closest-point projection cannot: a boundary scan evaluates \eqref{eq:radial_gap_cv5} at every $\theta$
and enumerates every disjoint penetrating arc, reporting $\ge 2$ contact arcs on a wedged pose where a
single closest-point projection returns one foot. Driving the rigid-body cam dynamics with the neural
chart then matches the analytical-chart trajectory to $0.04\%$, while conserving linear and angular
momentum to $5\times10^{-15}$ and $1\times10^{-13}$, respectively. The chart thus resolves geometry the level
set smooths while reproducing the identical contact dynamics where both detectors apply. The caveats of
\S\ref{sec:tmap} carry over. The radial gap is conservative-large, biased by
$1/\cos\alpha$ on steep flanks (with $\alpha$ the surface slope angle), and the test is rigid-body
contact kinematics, not the deformable solver.

\subsection{CV-6: the Koch fractal, resolution independence and the SDF ceiling}\label{sec:verif_cv6}

CV-6 mates two Koch snowflakes, a boundary generated by a four-contraction iterated-function system
(IFS, scale $1/3$) evaluated to recursion depth $n$. The benchmark tests what happens to each
representation as geometric detail refines without bound. The snowflake is not star-shaped for $n\ge
2$, so the radial gap of CV-5 no longer applies. Here the chart is the recursive generating rule
itself, with the IFS doubling as a bounding hierarchy for a pruned recursive descent. The
representation costs of chart and level set are asymmetric, and that asymmetry is the point.
Figure~\ref{fig:cv6-geom} shows the Koch boundary at successive recursion levels; the chart stores the
single generating rule that produces every level, while a grid or a fixed-capacity field must store the
rendered detail.

\paragraph{Storage and query cost.} The IFS chart stores the four contraction maps once, in $O(1)$
storage independent of depth, and evaluates the boundary to any $n$ on demand. Its inside/outside
test prunes the recursion to a per-query cost of $O(\text{depth})$, measured at $\approx 21$ visited
nodes and bounded for $n$ up to $12$. A level set pays differently. A uniform SDF grid storing the same
pattern costs $O(9^n)$ cells, and an adaptive narrow-band SDF costs $O(4^n)$ boundary cells; both are
exponential in depth and capped at their finest cell, whereas the chart sets its resolution per query
(Fig.~\ref{fig:koch_cost}). A precomputed SDF is in fact cheaper \emph{per query}, an $O(1)$ lookup, so
the chart does not win on per-query speed, but it cannot represent the geometry past its stored
resolution. The chart's defensible advantages are therefore storage and resolution independence, not query
speed.

\section{Validation: emergent dilatancy on a real rock joint (CV-7)}\label{sec:cv7}

The closed-form hierarchy of Section~\ref{sec:verif} verifies the solver against problems whose answer is
known, where the transition map's gains remain abstract: a sharper gap, a cheaper representation. This section
closes the argument on a problem with no closed form and direct engineering stakes, the direct shear of a
natural rock joint, where resolving or smoothing the asperity geometry changes the \emph{predicted shear
strength} of the interface. The quantity on which the chart and the level set disagree is the one an engineer
would design with.

\paragraph{What a direct-shear test of a rock joint measures.} A rock joint is a fracture surface inside rock: the two faces left behind when the rock splits. The faces are rough, and their bumps, the \emph{asperities}, interlock like two meshed sawteeth. In a direct-shear test, a press holds the two faces together under a fixed normal stress $\sigma_n$ and then slides the top face sideways over the bottom one while recording the shear stress $\tau$ needed to keep it moving. Because the asperities interlock, the top face cannot slide horizontally without also riding up the flanks of the bumps, and the joint opens vertically as it shears. That climbing is \emph{dilatancy}, and it makes the joint stronger than a flat sawcut would be, because part of the applied shear now does work lifting the normal load. Engineers summarize the measured resistance by the \emph{apparent friction}, the stress ratio $\muapp=\tau/\sigma_n$, and read it as an \emph{apparent friction angle} $\arctan\muapp$ that combines the material's base friction with the geometric climb. The asperity geometry, not a material constant, sets how much extra strength the climb supplies, so a method's accuracy on this test is decided by how faithfully it represents the bumps.

The argument proceeds only after the analytical ladder of Section~\ref{sec:verif} has earned the right to make
it. We first state the problem, the data, and the closed-form anchor that pins the emergent friction law
(Section~\ref{sec:cv7-data}); we then report the head-to-head reconstruction and its mechanical consequence on
the real Inada profile (Section~\ref{sec:cv7-headline}), carry the machinery through two mutually deformable
blocks (Section~\ref{sec:cv7-p1}) and a dilatancy-versus-roughness law (Section~\ref{sec:cv7-p2}), and close
with the atlas-versus-level-set comparison on identical shear (Section~\ref{sec:cv7-payoff}). Three further
studies---the transition-map detector inside the contact loop, a cyclic energy ledger that verifies
Corollary~\ref{cor:second-law}, and an explicit material-point cross-check---are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:supp}.

\subsection{Problem, data, and the Patton anchor}\label{sec:cv7-data}

The specimen is a real surface, not an idealised one: a tensile fracture in Inada granite (Digital Rocks Portal \#273, DOI~10.17612/QXSA-TK92; \citealp{inada2020digitalrocks}), scanned as a height map $z(x,y)$ that gives the surface elevation $z$ over the in-plane coordinates $(x,y)$. The scan is sampled at $23.4~\mu$m, and the surface is self-affine: its roughness repeats statistically across scales, with Hurst exponent $H\approx0.78$ and fractal dimension $D\approx2.2$, so asperities exist from the micron sampling up to the joint length. The root-mean-square roughness is $1.70$~mm, matching the published value. The point of using this specimen is that no equation predicts its shear response; geometric fidelity is the only thing standing between a method and the right answer.

Each of the two mating faces is carried by a learned height chart $h_\theta:\mathcal D\to\R$ (Section~\ref{sec:fourier}), a random-Fourier-feature MLP with weights $\theta$ that stores the surface as a graph $z=h_\theta(x)$ rather than as a field in the surrounding space. Because the surface is single-valued, with one height per in-plane point, the chart is already its own inverse coordinate. The transition map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$, which composes one face's boundary chart with the inverse of the other's, then needs no numerical inversion, and the signed gap reduces to a vertical offset. The next equation states that gap and the surface normal it carries.
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cv7-gap}
\gn(x,z)=z-h_\theta(x),\qquad
\nbf=\frac{(-h_\theta'(x),\,1)}{\sqrt{1+h_\theta'(x)^2}},\qquad \gn<0\ \Leftrightarrow\ \text{penetration},
\end{equation}
%
Here $\gn$ is the signed normal gap (negative when the point lies below the surface, i.e.\ in penetration), $\nbf$ is the unit outward normal built from the surface slope $h_\theta'(x)$, and the equation says simply that the gap is how far a point sits above or below the height graph. The slope $h_\theta'(x)$ is the moving part: it sets the normal direction, and through it the tilt of every contact force. The solver receives the small-slope vertical proxy of the gap, and the true foot point is recovered only for verification.

The two faces are sheared under constant normal stress, with the normal degree of freedom solved to equilibrium at each shear increment, so the test is quasi-static direct shear. Friction is \emph{plain Coulomb}: the local coefficient $\mu=\tan\phi_b$ is held constant, where $\phi_b$ is the base friction angle of the rock material. There is \emph{no imposed dilatancy law}. The dilation and the apparent friction angle are therefore emergent outputs of the resolved asperity geometry, which is precisely what makes geometric fidelity the entire story. We report the apparent friction as the stress ratio $\muapp=\tau/\sigma_n$.

The validation has one closed-form anchor, derived from the simplest interlocking surface. Patton
\citep{patton1966multiple} idealised a joint as two mating sawtooth faces whose flanks rise at a single
asperity angle $i$. The following proposition derives the apparent friction of that joint from a force balance
on the resolved, tilted face, so that the Patton law is a consequence of plain Coulomb friction rather than an
imposed constitutive rule.

\begin{proposition}[Emergent Patton law and its anti-plane companion]\label{prop:patton}
Let a sawtooth flank rise at angle $i$, so the resolved contact normal is $\nbf=(-\sin i,\cos i)$ in the
(shear, normal) frame, and let plain Coulomb friction $\norm{\tbf_T}=\mu t_N$ with $\mu=\tan\phi_b$ act on that
face. Resolving the contact reaction onto the macroscopic axes, the apparent friction for shear \emph{across}
the ridges is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:patton}
\muapp=\frac{T}{N}=\tan(\phi_b+i)=\frac{\tan i+\mu}{1-\mu\tan i},
\end{equation}
with dilation rate $\dot u_n/\dot u_t=\tan i$, whereas for shear \emph{along} the ridges (anti-plane) it is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:patton-along}
\muapp=\frac{\mu}{\cos i},\qquad \text{dilation}=0 .
\end{equation}
A representation that smooths the slope $i\mapsto i'<i$ under-predicts the across-ridge strength by
$\Delta\muapp\approx\sec^2(\phi_b+i)\,(i-i')$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
On the flank the contact reaction is $\fbf=t_N\nbf+\mu t_N\tbf$, with $\tbf=(\cos i,\sin i)$ the up-flank
tangent at the cone bound. Projecting onto the macroscopic shear and normal axes gives
$T=t_N(\sin i+\mu\cos i)$ and $N=t_N(\cos i-\mu\sin i)$, whose ratio is
$(\tan i+\mu)/(1-\mu\tan i)=\tan(\phi_b+i)$; climbing $\mathrm d u_t$ along the flank raises the block by
$\mathrm d u_n=\mathrm d u_t\tan i$. For slip along the ridge axis the velocity is parallel to the ridge, so
the friction $\mu t_N$ acts on a facet inflated by $1/\cos i$ with no climb, giving $\muapp=\mu/\cos i$ and zero
dilation. Linearising \eqref{eq:patton} in $i$ yields the strength loss.
\end{proof}

Proposition~\ref{prop:patton} ties strength directly to the resolved slope $i$, the quantity on which the chart
and the level set disagree. The law is not imposed: plain Coulomb friction and the resolved tilt are supplied
to the solver, and on a mating sawtooth the framework reproduces \eqref{eq:patton} to $0.00\%$, with the
dilation rate equal to $\tan i$ to within $1\%$; the anti-plane response \eqref{eq:patton-along} is the
zero-dilation mode observed when the deformable joint is sheared along the asperity fabric
(Section~\ref{sec:cv7-p1}). The rock-mechanics literature reads $i$ as a joint-roughness coefficient
\citep{barton1977shear,barton1990review}; here $i$ is not fitted but the slope distribution the chart resolves
directly. Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-surfaces} renders the two mating Inada faces the solver shears, coloured by the
local gap, the geometry from which the apparent friction and dilation emerge.

\subsection{Chart versus level set on the real Inada profile}\label{sec:cv7-headline}

The head-to-head fits two representations to the same Inada profile and then shears them. The first is the height chart $h_\theta$. The second is the comparison baseline: an ambient 2-D neural SDF $\phi(x,z)$, a plain coordinate MLP that learns the signed distance to the surface as a field in the plane, from whose zero level set we extract a reconstructed surface $h_{\rm SDF}(x)$. We compare the two on three things in turn: how well each reconstructs the surface, how well each captures the asperity slope, and what shear strength each then predicts.

The reconstruction gap is large and one-signed. The height chart reproduced the measured surface to $2.3~\mu$m with 66k parameters; the ambient SDF reached only $107~\mu$m, $47\times$ worse, and did so with \emph{more} parameters (83k). The level set spends its capacity on a 2-D distance field and still resolves the 1-D surface far less well. The mechanism is the spectral bias of a fixed-capacity coordinate MLP \citep{rahaman2019spectral}: such a network learns smooth, low-frequency content first and starves the sharp high-frequency detail. The Fourier-feature encoding of the chart defeats that bias \citep{tancik2020fourier}, whereas the plain SDF carries it intrinsically.

Reconstruction error in heights is the wrong metric for contact. Friction and dilation depend on the asperity \emph{angle} $i=\arctan\abs{h'}$, the local slope of the surface, not on the height itself. On slope the two representations separate. The chart reproduced the mean asperity angle, $19.8^\circ$ against $19.4^\circ$ for the true surface, while the SDF's spectral bias smoothed it to $12.5^\circ$. Through the Patton law \eqref{eq:patton}, a smoothed slope is a softer joint: shearing the two representations, the level set under-predicted the peak shear strength by $61\%$. The total dilation, by contrast, was barely affected ($0.5\%$). The split is itself diagnostic. Dilation is set by the large-scale waviness the SDF does capture, while strength is controlled by the asperity slope the SDF smooths, so a strongly affected strength beside a barely affected dilation is the physical signature of where the level set fails, not a tuned outcome. Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-capstone} collects the results.

  Coulomb friction on the resolved tilt; (c) the height chart resolves the asperities the ambient neural

The defensible claim is narrow and stated as such. It is \emph{not} that the chart has a lower height RMSE than the SDF on a well-sampled single-valued profile, since dense interpolation of heights is easy for both. It is that the SDF smooths the asperity \emph{slopes}, and that the smoothing under-predicts strength through \eqref{eq:patton}, together with the storage scaling: the chart is $O(N_{\rm surface})$ with $O(1)$ queries and, with Fourier features, no spectral-bias ceiling, whereas the ambient SDF must represent a 2-D field whose zero set is the surface. This is the same scaling argument as the Koch ceiling (CV-6), here on a real surface spanning roughly $3.5$ decades of scale, from $23.4~\mu$m asperities over a $73$~mm joint. Single profiles are single-asperity noisy in their peak, so the reported strength uses the steady or ensemble response. The surfaces are single-valued, so the argument rests on the spectral-bias ceiling and storage, not on overhangs.

\subsection{Phase 1: two mutually deformable decoder blocks}\label{sec:cv7-p1}

The Inada result treats the blocks as rigid. Phase 1 makes both faces deformable. Two elastic blocks, each carried by a boundary-fitted Fourier chart decoder $D_X:\boldsymbol\xi\mapsto\xbf$ that maps a reference coordinate $\boldsymbol\xi$ to the deformed position $\xbf$ (Section~\ref{sec:fourier}), meet at a fully rough mating interface and exchange node-to-surface penalty and regularised Coulomb forces through the chart-FEM solver. Both faces are rough; neither is a flat platen; the contact is mutual. The roughness lives in the decoder geometry, not in an interface constitutive law, so dilation and strength again emerge from the resolved bumps rather than from a calibrated rule.

The geometry is verified before any mechanical claim. The Fourier decoders reconstruct the rough faces without element foldover ($\det J\in[0.97,1.03]$ on every element, so the chart Jacobian stays positive and the mesh never inverts), and a method-of-manufactured-solutions study on the curved chart of \emph{both} blocks recovered the optimal $O(h^2)$ convergence of the P1 discretization (measured rates $2.39$ and $1.95$). The chart-FEM provably solves on the rough geometry it resolves.

The mechanical response is emergently \emph{anisotropic}: the joint resists differently depending on which way it is sheared relative to the asperity fabric. Shearing across, along, and obliquely to that fabric gave distinct peak apparent friction, with $\muapp$ of $0.42$ (out-of-plane), $0.54$ (in-plane), and $0.74$ (mixed), and with dilation ranging from $0.038$ to $0.090$~mm across the three modes. No direction-dependent friction is imposed; the anisotropy is the asperity geometry expressed through plain Coulomb. Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-twoblock} shows the three modes.

The emergent response is mesh-converged. Refining the two-block shear across four meshes ($n_{\rm cells}=6,8,10,12$; $2.6$k to $20.7$k tetrahedra), the peak $\muapp$ settled to $\approx0.53$ within $2\%$ ($0.510\to0.528\to0.535\to0.526$) and the total dilation converged to $\approx0.063$~mm ($0.094\to0.078\to0.066\to0.063$~mm; the coarse mesh over-predicts), with $\det J_{\min}$ steady near $0.96$. Because it is mesh-\emph{independent}, it reflects the non-smoothness of the moving-master contact, not discretization error, and the frictionless and one-block cases converge to $10^{-9}$. An augmented-Lagrangian or mortar formulation \citep{alart1991mixed,simo1992augmented,puso2004mortar} is the route to tight convergence and is left as future work. Second, the rigid-platen prescribed-displacement boundary concentrates the imposed shear into the top element layer and produces a spurious von Mises spike there. The joint-interface stress and the contact-derived $\muapp$ and dilation are unaffected, since they come from the contact forces, and the platen layer is trimmed for display.

dilatancy gap reported in Section~\ref{sec:cv7-payoff}.

\subsection{Phase 2: the dilatancy-versus-roughness law}\label{sec:cv7-p2}

Phase 2 asks how strength depends on roughness, and builds the dilatancy law from the geometry rather than calibrating one. Holding the friction coefficient and the loading protocol fixed and varying only the root-mean-square roughness of the decoder surface, the peak apparent friction rose monotonically from $0.61$ to $1.20$ as the surface RMS rose from $0.022$ to $0.066$~mm, with the emergent dilation tracking it ($0.018\to0.059$~mm). The trend is the expected one, in which a rougher joint mobilizes a higher apparent friction angle and dilates more. It is recovered here as an output of resolved geometry under plain Coulomb, in the direction of the empirical Barton trend \citep{barton1977shear,barton1990review}. The same monotone law appears when the spectral cutoff of the decoder is swept instead of the amplitude ($0.68\to1.21$), which isolates the resolved frequency content as the control. Across the entire sweep the decoder reconstruction stayed under $8\%$ of RMS, so the strength variation is geometry the chart resolves, not a reconstruction artifact. Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-roughness} plots the law.

\subsection{Atlas versus level set on identical shear: the payoff}\label{sec:cv7-payoff}

The two preceding subsections establish that strength and dilatancy are emergent properties of the resolved geometry. The payoff runs the \emph{identical} shear twice: once on the geometry from the atlas (the true rough surface) and once on the geometry from the ambient signed-distance function's smoothed zero level set, reading off the difference in the predicted mechanics. A band-limited rough surface is carried by a trained three-dimensional chart decoder, verified first: the Fourier decoder reconstructs the surface to $2.2\%$ of RMS, against $48\%$ for a vanilla $\tanh$ decoder and $\sim16\%$ for the ambient neural signed-distance function, with no element foldover and $O(h^2)$ chart-FEM convergence (Section~\ref{sec:fourier}). The same band-limited surface is then sheared under chart-FEM Coulomb friction.

The emergent dilatancy is a geometric consequence of the resolved normal, made precise by the following proposition, which is the central claim the validation confirms.

\begin{proposition}[Emergent dilatancy from resolved geometry]\label{prop:dilatancy}
Let a single-valued rough face $z=h(x)$ be carried by a height chart with resolved normal
$\nbf=(-h',1)/\sqrt{1+h'^2}$ (eq.~\eqref{eq:cv7-gap}), and let plain Coulomb friction act with no constitutive
dilation law. Wherever $h'\neq0$ the normal contact force $t_N\nbf$ has a non-zero shear-plane component, so a
horizontal shear increment produces an emergent vertical opening with apparent dilation angle
\begin{equation}\label{eq:emergent-dilatancy}
\tan\psi_{\mathrm{dil}}=\big\langle\,\abs{h'(x)}\,\big\rangle_{\text{bearing asperities}}=\langle\tan i\rangle,
\end{equation}
the slope-weighted asperity angle over the contacting set. Smoothing the surface drives $h'\to0$ and hence
$\psi_{\mathrm{dil}}\to0$, so a representation that low-passes the slopes under-predicts the dilatancy.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The contact force tilts with $\nbf$, which tilts with $h'$; the friction-projected slip rides the surface
tangent, whose vertical component is $\abs{h'}/\sqrt{1+h'^2}$. Integrating the climbing motion over the bearing
asperities gives the apparent dilation angle \eqref{eq:emergent-dilatancy}. When $h'\equiv0$ the force is
purely normal, there is no climb, and $\psi_{\mathrm{dil}}=0$.
\end{proof}

On the atlas geometry the dilatancy emerges from the asperities with no effective angle imposed. With friction switched off, the apparent friction rises from $0$ to $0.171$, a pure geometric dilatancy with dilation angle $\approx9.7^\circ$ produced by interlocking alone, and with $\mu=0.3$ it rises from an initial $0.24$ to a peak of $0.467$, the dilatant strengthening of \eqref{eq:patton} read off the real geometry. On the SDF-smoothed geometry the same shear under-predicts the emergent dilatancy by $98\%$ (frictionless: $0.004$ against $0.171$, because the smoothed surface has almost no asperity slope and hence almost no dilatancy) and the shear strength by $35\%$ (with friction: $0.306$ against $0.467$). Table~\ref{tab:cv7-atlas-sdf} collects the comparison and Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-atlas-sdf} shows it. No dilation angle is supplied to either run; the geometry produces it, and the level set, by smoothing the geometry, fails to.

The result holds on real data, not only the synthetic band-limited surface. On an actual Inada-granite topography patch resolved at the mesh scale, the atlas decoder reconstructs the surface to $3.2\%$ of RMS against the SDF's $15.8\%$, and the level set under-predicts the frictionless geometric dilatancy by $\sim60\%$ and the friction strength by $\sim17\%$. The two geometries must not be conflated. The $61\%$ strength figure is the real Inada 1-D profile (Section~\ref{sec:cv7-headline}), and the $98\%/35\%$ pair is the synthetic 3-D decoder run. The sign is nonetheless uniform across both, and across five independent roughness realizations the level set under-predicts the emergent dilatancy by $58\%\pm35\%$. The magnitude depends on how much asperity slope each realization's smoothing destroys, but the under-prediction itself is a statistical fact, not a cherry-picked surface.

Three further studies carry the program beyond this headline and are reported in the supplementary appendix (Appendix~\ref{app:supp}): the transition-map height-chart detector routed as the FEM $(\text{gap},\text{normal})$ contract (active-set agreement $98.9\%$ for the chart against $95.8\%$ for the ambient SDF on $10^5$ query points, at $1.47\times$ the per-query cost), a cyclic stick/slip energy ledger that closes per cycle, and an explicit ChartMPM cross-check that reproduces the Coulomb friction floor.

The scope of CV-7 is stated plainly. The blocks here are one deformable face on a rigid mating surface, or two single-realization deformable blocks. The surfaces are single-valued, so the level-set failure rests on the spectral-bias ceiling and storage, not on overhangs; overhangs would require the micro-CT voxel volumes and the full transition-map atlas with decoder inversion at chart overlaps. The roughness is band-limited to the wavelengths the mesh resolves, with a modest amplitude to keep $\det J>0$. The two-block friction residual and the cyclic energy bookkeeping remain open numerical items addressed by augmented-Lagrangian/mortar contact. Within that scope, the measured thesis is firm: the apparent friction $\muapp=\tan(\phi_b+i)$ and the dilation are emergent from resolved asperity geometry, the boundary-fitted Fourier chart resolves the asperity slopes the ambient SDF smooths, and the level set therefore under-predicts strength ($61\%$ Inada, $35\%$ synthetic) and dilatancy ($98\%$ synthetic frictionless) on a problem an engineer would design with.

\section{Discussion, limitations, and conclusions}\label{sec:disc}

The geometric representation, not the constraint-enforcement scheme, controls contact fidelity on
geometrically hard contact. The benchmarks of Sections~\ref{sec:verif} and~\ref{sec:cv7} put the
transition-map chart and the ambient level set on the same problems, meshes, and tolerances, and the measured
difference between them is therefore attributable to the geometry alone. This section reads those results as a
single statement about when a boundary chart improves on a neural signed-distance function, states the
preconditions and limitations that bound the method, and concludes.

\subsection{When the chart improves on the level set}\label{sec:disc:regime}

The closed-form suite CV-1 through CV-4 is not built to flatter the chart. Each problem is convex, collinear,
or superposable, so the boundary-to-boundary correspondence never comes into play: there is no non-convex
multi-arc contact to enumerate, no cusp to resolve, and no curved-surface correspondence to invert. On all
four the radial inverse-chart gap reduces to the level-set gap (Proposition~\ref{prop:active-set} with
$\cos\alpha\to1$), the two representations are interchangeable, and the neural geometry merely reproduces the
closed forms. The discriminating evidence lives in CV-5 through CV-7, where the geometry is cusped, fractal, or
rough. Reporting the equivalence regime is what makes the wins attributable to representation rather than to an
incidental difference in the solver; Table~\ref{tab:comparison} collects the measured separation across the
two regimes.

\subsection{Preconditions and trade-offs}\label{sec:disc:precond}

Two conditions bound where the transition-map detector applies, and one property bounds what it returns; each is
a geometric fact about the parametrisation, checked once, not a solver tolerance. \emph{Star-shapedness and
single-valuedness.} A radial chart represents a body as a single-valued radius along every direction, valid only
when each ray from the centre meets the boundary once; a plane or half-space is not star-shaped about any finite
centre, so floors and rigid platens remain on the signed-distance path while the radial detector handles
star-shaped obstacles. A height chart relaxes the need for a centre but represents the surface as a graph, so it
admits no overhangs and no re-entrant detail; a genuinely re-entrant surface requires the full decoder atlas with
Newton inversion at chart overlaps \eqref{eq:newton-invert}, the generalisation outlined below.
\emph{Conservative-large, sign-exact, not eikonal.} By Proposition~\ref{prop:active-set} the radial gap is
sign-exact, so the chart and the level-set active sets coincide on star-shaped bodies, but its magnitude is
enlarged by $1/\cos\alpha$ and its gradient is not of unit length: $\norm{\grad g}$ can reach $\sim10^{4}$ in deep
concave valleys. A perpendicular-foot refinement recovers the normal-projected distance but is reserved for
verification, since that foot jumps across the medial axis and would violate Corollary~\ref{cor:second-law}.
\emph{Storage versus query cost.} The recursive chart of CV-6 stores the generating rule in $O(1)$ and answers a
query in $O(\text{depth})$ work, whereas a uniform level set must discretise the ambient field at its finest scale,
$O(9^{n})$ storage; the level set answers each query more cheaply, by an $O(1)$ lookup, but its fixed capacity
caps the geometry it can represent, and that ceiling is what CV-6 measures.

One trade-off is worth isolating, because it is the most carefully measured. Routing the height-chart detector
into the contact loop on the real rough joint, the chart's active set agrees with the analytic reference $98.9\%$
of the time against $95.8\%$ for the ambient field, and the contact-driving gap RMSE is $4.2\%$ against $44.7\%$,
a $10.5\times$ sharper quantity, at $1.47\times$ the per-query cost. The chart does not win everywhere: its
random-Fourier height gradient gives a noisier tip normal, $10.3^{\circ}$ of median deviation against the level
set's $0.8^{\circ}$ at the sharpest asperity peaks. This local loss does not weaken the strength prediction,
because the penalty force and the friction cone read the gap and the active set, not the pointwise normal at an
isolated tip, so the smoothed \emph{gap} of the level set propagates into the response where the chart's noisier
tip normal does not.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:disc:limits}

Four limitations follow directly from the measurements, each stated with what it does and does not put at risk.
\emph{The two-deformable friction residual.} The frictionless and one-block solves converge to a contact Newton
residual of $\sim10^{-9}$ with a consistent tangent; two mutually deformable blocks under Coulomb friction do not,
the residual holding at $3.8$--$4.8\%$ and not shrinking under refinement. Its mesh-independence identifies it as
the non-smoothness of moving-master node-to-surface contact, not a discretisation error, to be cured by a
semismooth-Newton, augmented-Lagrangian, or mortar treatment of the tangential complementarity
\citep{alart1991mixed,simo1992augmented,puso2004mortar,chouly2013nitsche}---a constraint-enforcement upgrade
orthogonal to the geometric representation argued for here. It does not threaten the headline result, because the
emergent quantities are mesh-converged ($\muapp\to0.53\pm2\%$, dilation $\to0.063$~mm).
\emph{A single band-limited realisation in small strain.} The rough-geometry validation rests on one band-limited
surface realisation in small-strain elasticity; a single realisation is statistically spiky and the roughness sweep
of Section~\ref{sec:cv7} reports the trend, not any absolute value. Finite strain, asperity-tip plasticity, and
asperity damage lie outside the present model.
\emph{The contact gap is a small-slope proxy.} On the height-chart joint the gap is the vertical offset
\eqref{eq:height-gap}, exact in sign and accurate to $O(\text{slope}^2)$ in magnitude, which is why the CV-7 fidelity
story is told in slopes and normals rather than gap magnitude; the Inada head-to-head shows that this is exactly
where the level set fails.
\emph{The dynamic path is opt-in.} The production material-point oracle still computes the gap from the
signed-distance gradient, and the chart detector drives CV-5 analytically and CV-7 numerically as an opt-in path
rather than the default everywhere. The explicit cross-check reproduces the Coulomb floor
($\muapp\approx0.34$ against base $\mu=0.4$); what makes the eventual replacement conservative is the
energy-consistency of Proposition~\ref{prop:matched-normal}, confirmed by the cyclic ledger of
Appendix~\ref{app:supp}, whose per-cycle balance closes from $1.09$ to $1.01$ on the deformable joint.

\subsection{Conclusions and outlook}\label{sec:disc:concl}

The geometric representation of the boundary, not the constraint-enforcement scheme, controls contact fidelity on
geometrically hard contact. A fixed-capacity ambient signed-distance field learns low spatial frequencies first
and high frequencies slowly or never (Proposition~\ref{prop:ntk}), so it smooths cusps, asperities, and fine
relief and carries a non-smooth medial axis in any concavity; a boundary chart stores the same body as a function
one dimension lower, with no interior and no medial axis, and a random-Fourier-feature input removes the spectral
ceiling (Proposition~\ref{prop:rff}, Corollary~\ref{cor:capacity}), so the chart resolves the boundary where the
ambient field cannot. Posed as a transition map on a learned atlas, the detector returns a conservative matched
normal (Proposition~\ref{prop:matched-normal}) and a sign-exact active set (Proposition~\ref{prop:active-set}),
and it inherits the variational-inequality and frictional-dissipation structure of Section~\ref{sec:govern}
unchanged. The closed-form ladder confirms the chart and the level set are equivalent on convex and superposable
geometry (CV-1 through CV-4) and separate measurably on cusps (CV-5) and self-similar refinement (CV-6); the real
Inada-granite joint (CV-7) shows that the apparent friction $\tan(\phi_b+i)$ and the normal dilation emerge from
the resolved asperity geometry under plain Coulomb friction (Propositions~\ref{prop:patton}
and~\ref{prop:dilatancy}), and that the level set, by smoothing the slopes, under-predicts strength by $61\%$ on
the granite profile and dilatancy by $98\%$ on the synthetic block.

Three directions remain, each removing a stated restriction. The star-shapedness and single-valuedness
preconditions are lifted by a multi-chart decoder atlas, in which several boundary-fitted decoder charts cover a
body and their overlaps reconcile through Newton-inverted transition maps \eqref{eq:newton-invert}, so that
overhangs and re-entrant lobes no single chart can hold are handled chart by chart. Full three-dimensional
axisymmetric Hertz contact with local mesh refinement at the contact patch would close the resolution gap that
limited the two-dimensional line-contact verifications and place the chart-FEM contact pressure against the
closed-form $p_0$ and $a(F)$ in three dimensions. Finally, embedding the transition-map detector inside a
mortar or augmented-Lagrangian dynamic solver, with a finite-strain hyperelastic interior, would extend the
representation-controls-fidelity result from quasi-static rock joints to large-sliding, finite-deformation
contact. Across all three the conclusion is constant: fidelity on hard contact follows the geometry one chooses
to resolve, and the transition map resolves what the level set smooths.

\section{Closed-form benchmark solutions}\label{app:closedforms}

This appendix collects the closed forms that serve as acceptance targets for the
code-verification benchmarks CV-1--CV-4. Each solution is small-strain, linear-elastic,
and quasi-static. Each is also the limit a finite-deformation contact solve must reproduce
once the loading and material parameters enter the appropriate regime, so a numerical
result that misses these targets signals a coding error rather than a modelling choice. Each
solution is derived symbolically and verified against an independent vectorised evaluator.
Throughout the paper we use one sign convention: a normal gap $\gn<0$ denotes penetration, and
compressive stress is negative.

The three-dimensional axisymmetric forms below are the canonical reference, and the
numerical study restricts itself to their two-dimensional reductions. The verification in
Section~\ref{sec:verif} exercises the line-contact limit of CV-1, namely the contact
half-width, and the planar reduction of CV-2, whose stick-radius exponent is $1/2$ rather
than the axisymmetric $1/3$. The plane-strain solver reaches these regimes; we leave full
three-dimensional Hertz to future work.

\subsection{CV-1 --- Hertz normal contact}\label{app:hertz}

Two elastic bodies of radii $R_1,R_2$ and moduli $(E_1,\nu_1),(E_2,\nu_2)$ are pressed
together by a normal force $F$. Hertz theory replaces this two-body problem with a single
contact between an effective elastic medium and an effective curved surface. We therefore
collapse the four elastic constants and two radii into one modulus and one radius:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hertz-effective}
  \frac{1}{E^\ast}=\frac{1-\nu_1^2}{E_1}+\frac{1-\nu_2^2}{E_2},
  \qquad
  \frac{1}{R}=\frac{1}{R_1}+\frac{1}{R_2},
\end{equation}
where $E^\ast$ is the effective contact modulus and $R$ the effective radius of the gap.
With $R$ fixed, the undeformed gap near the apex is the paraboloid $\gn(r)=r^2/2R$, with
$r$ the in-plane distance from the contact axis. The axisymmetric Hertz solution
\citep{johnson1985contact} then gives the contact radius $a$, the pressure $p(r)$, its
peak value $p_0$, the approach $\delta$ (the relative rigid-body motion of the two bodies),
and the force--approach law:
\begin{align}
  a &= \left(\frac{3FR}{4E^\ast}\right)^{1/3}, \label{eq:hertz-a}\\
  p(r) &= p_0\sqrt{1-r^2/a^2}, \qquad
  p_0 = \frac{2E^\ast a}{\pi R} = \frac{3F}{2\pi a^2}, \label{eq:hertz-p}\\
  \delta &= \frac{a^2}{R}, \qquad
  F = \frac{4}{3}\,E^\ast\sqrt{R}\,\delta^{3/2}. \label{eq:hertz-force}
\end{align}
The contact radius grows as the cube root of the load, and the pressure traces a
hemispherical cap whose peak $p_0$ is $3/2$ the mean pressure $F/\pi a^2$. Integrating
this pressure over the contact recovers the applied load, $\int_0^a p(r)\,2\pi
r\,\mathrm{d}r = F$. The harness uses this identity as an independent consistency check on
the numerical pressure field.

Beneath the contact, the load redistributes into the bulk, and the stresses on the
symmetry axis follow \citep[Eq.~3.45]{johnson1985contact}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hertz-subsurface}
  \sigma_z = -\frac{p_0}{1+\zeta^2}, \qquad
  \sigma_r = \sigma_\theta
  = -p_0\!\left[(1+\nu)\!\left(1-\zeta\arctan\tfrac{1}{\zeta}\right)-\tfrac12\,\frac{1}{1+\zeta^2}\right],
  \qquad \zeta=\frac{z}{a}.
\end{equation}
Here $\sigma_z$ is the axial stress, $\sigma_r=\sigma_\theta$ the in-plane normal
stress, and $\zeta=z/a$ the depth measured in contact radii. The difference between these
components sets the shear that drives yield, which is why the maximum shear sits below the
surface rather than at it. For $\nu=0.3$ the maximum shear
$\tfrac12|\sigma_z-\sigma_r|$ reaches $\tau_{\max}=0.31\,p_0$ at depth $z/a=0.481$, the
von Mises stress peaks at $\sigma_{vM}^{\max}=0.62\,p_0$, and first yield occurs at
$p_0\approx 1.61\,\sigma_Y$. We accept the numerical solution when $a$, $p_0$, $\delta$,
and $F(\delta)$ agree to $<1\%$, the pressure integral to $<0.5\%$, and the subsurface
maximum shear lies at $z/a=0.48\pm0.02$ with magnitude $0.31\,p_0\pm2\%$.

The plane-strain solver reaches the line-contact limit rather than the full axisymmetric
case, so we use that limit as the indenter benchmark for the chart-FEM verification. For a
load per unit thickness $P'$, the half-width and the peak pressure are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:line-contact}
  a = 2\sqrt{\frac{P'R}{\pi E^\ast}}, \qquad
  p_0 = \frac{2P'}{\pi a} = \sqrt{\frac{P'E^\ast}{\pi R}},
\end{equation}
with $E^\ast = E/2(1-\nu^2)$ for two identical bodies and $a$ now the contact half-width
of the strip. The half-width grows as the square root of the load here, in contrast to the
cube-root growth of the axisymmetric radius in \eqref{eq:hertz-a}. The subsurface field of
a Hertzian half-plane pressure over $[-a,a]$ is the McEwen solution
\citep[Eq.~4.49]{johnson1985contact}, evaluated for the stress-bulb figures.

\subsection{CV-2 --- Cattaneo--Mindlin frictional partial slip}\label{app:cattaneo}

A tangential load $Q<\mu P$ is applied to a pre-formed Hertz contact of radius $a$ and
peak pressure $p_0$ under normal load $P$. Because the tangential load stays below the
sliding limit $\mu P$, the contact does not slide as a whole. Instead it splits into an
inner stick zone $r\le c$ and an outer slip annulus $c\le r\le a$. The stick radius $c$
shrinks as $Q$ approaches $\mu P$ \citep{mindlin1949compliance,johnson1985contact}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cattaneo-c}
  \frac{c}{a} = \left(1-\frac{Q}{\mu P}\right)^{1/3}.
\end{equation}
The plane-strain solver of Section~\ref{sec:verif} verifies the two-dimensional line-contact
reduction, for which the stick radius follows the larger exponent
$c/a = (1-Q/\mu P)^{1/2}$; the axisymmetric $1/3$ form above is the spherical-contact reference.
The stick zone vanishes ($c\to0$) exactly when $Q$ reaches $\mu P$ and full sliding
begins. The tangential traction $q(r)$ superposes the full-sliding distribution on an
inner corrective term that cancels the slip displacement over the stick zone:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cattaneo-q}
  q(r) =
  \begin{cases}
    \mu p_0\sqrt{1-r^2/a^2}, & c\le r\le a,\\[4pt]
    \mu p_0\!\left[\sqrt{1-r^2/a^2}-\dfrac{c}{a}\sqrt{1-r^2/c^2}\,\right], & r\le c.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
In the slip annulus the traction saturates at the Coulomb limit $\mu p(r)$, while inside
the stick zone the corrective term keeps it below that limit. Integrating $q$ over the
contact and relating it to the tangential motion gives the compliance $\delta_x$, the
tangential displacement under load:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cattaneo-delta}
  \delta_x = \frac{3\mu P}{16\,a\,G^\ast}\!\left[1-\left(1-\frac{Q}{\mu P}\right)^{2/3}\right],
  \qquad
  \frac{1}{G^\ast} = \frac{2-\nu_1}{4G_1}+\frac{2-\nu_2}{4G_2}.
\end{equation}
Here $G^\ast$ is the effective shear modulus, built from the shear moduli $G_1,G_2$ the
same way $E^\ast$ is built from the Young's moduli. The compliance is the frictional
counterpart of the normal force--approach law. It softens as $Q$ rises and the stick zone
gives way. We accept the numerical solution when $c/a$ agrees to $<1\%$, the integral
$\int q\,\mathrm{d}A = Q$ to $<1\%$, and $q(r)$ in \eqref{eq:cattaneo-q} agrees pointwise.

\subsection{CV-3 --- Brazilian disc}\label{app:brazilian}

A disc of diameter $D=2R$ and thickness $t$ is compressed diametrally by a force $P$. The
solution superposes two Flamant load-point charts at the poles $(0,\pm R)$ on a uniform
biaxial tension $T=2P/\pi Dt$ that cancels the spurious traction the point loads leave on
the rim \citep{timoshenko1970elasticity}. At the disc center the two charts combine into a
tensile horizontal stress and a compressive vertical stress:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:brazilian-center}
  \sigma_{xx} = +\frac{2P}{\pi D t} \;\;(\text{tensile}),
  \qquad
  \sigma_{yy} = -\frac{6P}{\pi D t} \;\;(\text{compressive}),
\end{equation}
so the horizontal tension at the center is one third the vertical compression, and this
tension splits the disc. The splitting tensile strength inferred from a failure load $P_f$
is therefore $\sigma_t = 2P_f/\pi D t$ (ASTM~D3967). Vertical equilibrium across the
diametral section gives an independent balance the field must satisfy:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:brazilian-balance}
  \int_{-R}^{R}\sigma_{yy}(x,0)\,\mathrm{d}x = -\frac{P}{t},
\end{equation}
that is, the vertical stress integrated across the diameter carries the full applied load
per unit thickness. The field must also satisfy the admissibility conditions
$\grad\!\cdot\bm{\sigma}=\bm{0}$ and $\grad^2(\mathrm{tr}\,\bm{\sigma})=0$ away from the
poles. Along the loaded diameter, a finite load patch replaces the point load, and the
distributed-pressure (Hondros) form \citep{hondros1959evaluation} gives, for a contact
half-angle $2\alpha$ subtended by the patch at the center,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hondros}
  \sigma_{xx}(0,y) = \frac{2P}{\pi D t\,\alpha}\!\left[\,
    \frac{(1-r^2/R^2)\sin 2\alpha}{1-2r^2\cos2\alpha/R^2+r^4/R^4}
    -\arctan\!\Big(\frac{1+r^2/R^2}{1-r^2/R^2}\tan\alpha\Big)\right],
\end{equation}
with $r=|y|$ the distance from the center along the loaded diameter. As the patch shrinks
($\alpha\to0$), \eqref{eq:hondros} reduces to the center value \eqref{eq:brazilian-center}
and removes the point-load singularity. We require the center stresses
\eqref{eq:brazilian-center} to $<1\%$ and the global balance \eqref{eq:brazilian-balance}
to $<0.5\%$. The diametral compliance under a true point load diverges logarithmically. A
finite contact half-width $b$ keeps it finite:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:brazilian-compliance}
  \Delta_D = \frac{4P}{\pi E t}\!\left[\ln\frac{2R}{b}-\frac{1-\nu}{2}\right],
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_D$ is the change in diameter along the load line. The prefactor and the
$-(1-\nu)/2$ term are robust, while the logarithmic argument carries the calibration of the
contact width $b$.

\subsection{CV-4 --- Nine-disc equibiaxial unit cell}\label{app:ninedisc}

A $3\times3$ square array of identical discs of radius $R$ is confined isotropically. No
diagonal contacts form, because the diagonal spacing $2\sqrt2\,R$ exceeds the contact
spacing $2R$. The $D_4$ symmetry of the lattice makes every contact carry the same normal
force $N$, while reflection parity keeps friction inactive. Each disc then sees four equal
inward loads $N$ at its north, south, east, and west poles, so the unit cell is the
superposition of two perpendicular Brazilian solutions, that is, four Flamant charts and
two uniform-tension charts. Adding the two perpendicular Brazilian fields makes the center
state an equibiaxial in-plane compression:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ninedisc-center}
  \sigma_{xx} = \sigma_{yy} = -\frac{2N}{\pi R t}, \qquad \sigma_{xy}=0,
\end{equation}
that is, equal compression in both directions and no shear. In the rigid-plate limit, the
numerical solution must reproduce this to $<1\%$ with an interior field identical across
the nine discs. The force--compression relation then closes the system through the
per-contact closing displacement $\Delta_x(N)$, the amount each contact shortens under
force $N$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ninedisc-force}
  3\,\Delta_x(N) = 2d, \qquad
  \Delta_x(N) = \frac{4N}{\pi E t}\ln\frac{2R}{b}
  + \frac{N}{\pi E t}\big[(2-\pi)\nu + \pi - 6\big],
  \qquad
  b = 2\sqrt{\frac{N R_{\rm rel}}{\pi E^\ast}},
\end{equation}
where $d$ is the boundary compression and $b$ the Hertz half-width of a disc--disc
contact; the factor of three counts the three contacts a load crosses along one row. For
$R=1.7$~mm, $E=100$~GPa, $\nu=0.3$, and $t=1$~mm, the relation gives
$N(d{=}10^{-4}\,\text{mm}) \approx 0.887$~N/mm. Equation~\eqref{eq:ninedisc-center} holds
exactly only for rigid confining plates. Deformable plates widen and soften the disc--plate
contacts by $\sqrt2$, so the interior discs differ by a few percent near their boundary
contacts, and any initial gaps make the contacts engage progressively. We record these
caveats with each numerical comparison.

\section{Chart training and verification protocol}\label{app:training}

Every coordinate chart in this work is a small multilayer perceptron with a Fourier-encoded input. We train each one by the same regression recipe and admit it to a contact solve only after it passes a fixed set of acceptance checks. This appendix records the four chart families and their Fourier banks (Sec.~\ref{app:training:families}), the training recipe shared by all of them (Sec.~\ref{app:training:recipe}), and the verify-first acceptance thresholds that a chart must clear before it enters a solve (Sec.~\ref{app:training:verify}). The charts differ only in their domain and in which Fourier bank they carry. The optimizer, the network, and the schedule are the same for all four; the loss is mean-squared error for the height and decoder charts and mean-absolute error for the radial chart.

\subsection{Four chart families, three Fourier banks}\label{app:training:families}

A chart maps a low-dimensional parameter on the body's boundary to a coordinate in physical space; the volumetric decoder maps a parameter on the reference cube instead. Three of the four charts are surface charts. The fourth is a boundary-fitted decoder $\xbf=D(\bm\xi)$ that carries a rough relief on one face of a deformable block, which the chart-FEM of Sec.~\ref{app:training:verify} then deforms. Each chart prepends a \emph{fixed}, non-trainable bank of sinusoidal features to its input before the MLP. We first normalize the input to $\tilde x\in[-1,1]^d$, where $d$ is the input dimension, so that a frequency means ``cycles across the domain'' rather than per physical unit. With that normalization the encoding reads

\begin{equation}\label{eq:appB-fourier}
\gamma(\tilde x)=\big[\cos(2\pi\,\tilde x B^{\!\top}),\ \sin(2\pi\,\tilde x B^{\!\top})\big]\in\R^{2K},
\qquad
h_\theta(x)=\mathrm{base}+\mathrm{MLP}_\theta\!\big(\gamma(\tilde x)\big),
\end{equation}

where $B\in\R^{K\times d}$ is the frequency bank, $K$ is the number of features, and $\mathrm{base}$ is a single learnable scalar. The scalar $\mathrm{base}$ carries the mean of the target, so the network regresses only the deviation about that offset. The bank $B$ is a registered buffer: the optimizer never updates it. Only the MLP weights $\theta$ and the scalar $\mathrm{base}$ are trained. This fixed encoding carries the method, as Sec.~\ref{sec:fourier} establishes. A chart of the raw coordinate inherits the same spectral bias as an ambient neural SDF and smooths the geometry in the same way. The bank supplies the high frequencies that the raw network suppresses.

The three banks differ in how they tile frequency space, each in the manner appropriate to its chart's domain.

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Geometric} (deterministic), for the 1-D height chart on the open interval. The frequencies are spaced geometrically, $B=\operatorname{geomspace}(0.5,f_{\max},K)$, which gives equal coverage per octave of scale; the scalar $\mathrm{base}$ carries the DC component. A self-affine rock profile sampled at spacing $\Delta x$ over a length $L$ contains on the order of $L/\Delta x\approx 3000$ resolvable wavelengths, so we set $f_{\max}$ to that count of cycles across the normalized domain.
\item \emph{Gaussian random}, for the 2-D height field and the 3-D rough decoder. The rows of $B$ are drawn $B_i\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2 I)$, which induces an isotropic, stationary kernel that stays flat out to a bandwidth set by $\sigma$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:fourier}). The decoder draws its relief bank from the same generator at a capped amplitude (see Sec.~\ref{app:training:verify}).
\item \emph{Harmonic}, for the 2-D radial chart on $\Sph^1$. The bank is the integer harmonics $[\cos(k\psi),\sin(k\psi)]_{k=1,\dots,K}$, which is \emph{periodic by construction}. Periodicity is mandatory here: the radius $\rho(\psi)$ must close up at $\psi$ and $\psi+2\pi$, so a non-periodic bank is inadmissible on the circle. The radial chart also wraps its output in a softplus, $\rho=\mathrm{softplus}(\mathrm{base}+\Delta)$, to keep $\rho>0$.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:appB-charts} collects the four families, their domains and outputs, and the bank parameters used in production. The 1-D and 2-D height charts share the width-128, depth-4 MLP of Sec.~\ref{app:training:recipe}. The radial chart uses a smaller width-96, depth-3 MLP, and the volumetric decoder a width-64, depth-3 MLP, because each of these carries fewer degrees of freedom than a full topography map.

\subsection{Common training recipe}\label{app:training:recipe}

We fit all four charts by minibatch regression onto sampled surface points. The training data are the measured or analytic target $(x_i,z_i)$: the topography map for a height chart, the analytic radius $\rho(\psi_i)$ for the radial chart, and the relief target on the rough face for the decoder. For the two height charts and the decoder the objective is the mean-squared error between the chart's prediction and that target,

\begin{equation}\label{eq:appB-mse}
\mathcal L(\theta,\mathrm{base})=\frac{1}{\abs{\mathcal B}}\sum_{i\in\mathcal B}
\big(h_\theta(x_i)-z_i\big)^2,
\end{equation}

evaluated on a random minibatch $\mathcal B$. The CV-5 radial chart instead minimizes the mean-absolute radius error $\frac{1}{\abs{\mathcal B}}\sum_i\abs{\rho_\theta(\psi_i)-\rho(\psi_i)}$, which is less sensitive to the cusped lobe tips; the remaining choices below are identical for all four families.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Input normalization.} We map the raw coordinate affinely to $\tilde x=2(x-x_{\mathrm{lo}})/(x_{\mathrm{hi}}-x_{\mathrm{lo}})-1\in[-1,1]^d$, so that the bank's frequencies mean ``cycles across the domain'' independent of physical units.
\item \emph{Fixed Fourier bank.} We build the bank $B$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:appB-fourier} once at construction and register it as a buffer; the optimizer never sees it.
\item \emph{Network.} A $\tanh$ MLP with Xavier initialization: width 128, depth 4 for the two height charts; width 96, depth 3 for the radial chart; width 64, depth 3 for the volumetric decoder. The input dimension is $2K$.
\item \emph{Learnable base.} We initialize the scalar $\mathrm{base}$ to the mean of the target and train it jointly with $\theta$, so the network regresses the deviation about a learned offset.
\item \emph{Optimizer and schedule.} Adam at learning rate $2\times10^{-3}$, with a cosine-annealing schedule that decays the rate to zero over the iteration budget. The minibatch sizes are 4096 (height), 8192 (surface), and 2048 (decoder).
\item \emph{Precision and seed.} Double precision with a fixed random seed. The networks are small, and double precision yields better-conditioned chart Jacobians and inverses for the downstream pushforward and gap evaluation.
\end{enumerate}

A plain regression with the fixed encoding suffices: there is no loss curriculum, no adversarial term, and no schedule of weights. The four trainers are structurally identical and differ only in the bank they construct.

\subsection{Verify-first acceptance thresholds}\label{app:training:verify}

A trained chart is geometry, and a defective chart silently corrupts every downstream solve. We therefore require each chart to pass acceptance checks before we admit it to a contact computation. The surface charts run the reconstruction check. The volumetric decoder runs all three, in the order below. If any check fails, we reject the chart rather than patch it.

\paragraph{(1) Reconstruction fidelity.} The reconstruction RMSE measures how closely the chart reproduces the sampled surface; we report it as a fraction of the surface RMS and require it below a few percent. On the rough decoder target the Fourier decoder reaches $2.2\%$ of RMS, the same network with the bank disabled plateaus at $48\%$, and an ambient three-dimensional neural signed-distance function of the same surface reaches $15.7\%$. This measured separation is the quantity that justifies the chart over the level set.

\paragraph{(2) No element foldover, $\det J>0$.} A folded chart has no admissible pushforward, so for the volumetric decoder the chart-Jacobian determinant must be strictly positive on every element of the reference mesh before any contact is attempted. The relief amplitude is capped so that the third column of the Jacobian, $\nbf+\tfrac12\,(\mathrm{relief})\,\hat\nbf$, keeps a positive determinant, and the geometric acceptance check verifies a positive $\det J_{\min}$ over the mesh.

\paragraph{(3) Discretization convergence (MMS $O(h^2)$).} The trained geometry must not degrade the solver's accuracy, so on the curved decoder geometry a manufactured-solution study must recover the second-order spatial rate of the underlying P1 chart-FEM. The measured rates on the rough geometry are $2.07$ and $1.83$, consistent with the $O(h^2)$ target of the P1 element.

These thresholds make the geometric representation falsifiable. A chart whose cutoff is too low under-resolves the asperities: spectral smoothing returns, and check (1) catches it. A chart whose relief amplitude is too high folds an element, and check (2) catches that. Only a chart that passes all applicable checks enters a contact solve.

\section{Data and code availability, and algorithm summary}\label{app:repro}

\subsection{Data availability}

The rough surfaces of Section~\ref{sec:cv7} are derived from a real tensile fracture of Inada granite, openly
available from the Digital Rocks Portal as dataset~\#273 (DOI~10.17612/QXSA-TK92) \citep{inada2020digitalrocks}.
The fracture is self-affine, with fractal dimension $D\approx2.2$, root-mean-square height $1.7$~mm, and in-plane
sampling $23.4~\mu$m. The compact height profiles used by the solver, together with the synthetic band-limited
surfaces of the roughness and spectral-cutoff sweeps, are generated from the scan and a fixed random seed, so the
reported apparent friction and dilation reproduce deterministically.

\subsection{Code availability}

The framework is implemented in Python with automatic differentiation, resolving the compute device and the
floating-point precision automatically. The neural charts and the signed-distance baselines are trained to the
acceptance thresholds of Appendix~\ref{app:training} before use, and the source code reproducing the verification
and validation results will be made available on publication.

\subsection{Algorithm summary}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:detector} states the two procedures that distinguish this work from a level-set contact
solver: the transition-map detector, which returns the matched pair $(\gn,\nbf)$ of Section~\ref{sec:tmap}, and
the verify-first protocol, which admits a trained chart only after it passes the reconstruction, foldover, and
convergence checks of Appendix~\ref{app:training}. Everything downstream of the detector---the broad phase, the
force laws, the consistent tangent, and the discretisations of Section~\ref{sec:discr}---is shared with the
signed-distance path through the dispatch \eqref{eq:detector}.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Transition-map contact detection and the verify-first chart protocol.}
\label{alg:detector}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{Detect}{query point $\xbf$; chart; frame $(c_B,Q_B)$}
  \If{radial chart}
    \State $\bm d \gets Q_B^\top(\xbf-c_B)$;\quad $r\gets\norm{\bm d}$;\quad $\dhat\gets\bm d/r$
    \State $\gn \gets r-\rho_B(\dhat)$ \Comment{inverse-chart gap, Eq.~\eqref{eq:radial-gap}}
    \State $\grad_S\rho \gets (I-\dhat\dhat^\top)\,\grad_{\hat d}\rho_B$
    \State $\nbf\gets\widehat{Q_B(\dhat-\grad_S\rho/r)}$ \Comment{matched normal, Prop.~\ref{prop:matched-normal}}
  \Else \Comment{height chart}
    \State $\gn\gets z-h_\theta(x)$;\quad $\nbf\gets(-h_\theta'(x),1)/\sqrt{1+h_\theta'^2}$ \Comment{Eq.~\eqref{eq:height-gap}}
  \EndIf
  \State \Return $(\gn,\nbf)$ \Comment{$\gn<0$ flags penetration; consumed by Eq.~\eqref{eq:detector}}
\EndProcedure
\Statex
\Procedure{VerifyFirst}{trained chart}
  \State \textbf{reject} unless reconstruction RMSE $<$ a few percent of surface RMS
  \State \textbf{reject} unless $\det J>0$ on every element \Comment{no foldover}
  \State \textbf{reject} unless a manufactured-solution study recovers $O(h^2)$
  \State \Return admitted chart
\EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Two points govern exact reproduction. The deformable rock-joint study reports stresses in a consistent unit
system with an uncalibrated modulus, so stresses scale linearly with it while the dimensionless apparent
friction and the geometry-set dilation do not. The two-block node-to-surface friction residual settles near
$4\%$ and does not shrink with mesh refinement, measuring the Coulomb non-smoothness of moving-master contact
rather than discretisation error, and it is the one quantity whose last digits depend on the contact-solver
tolerances.

\section{Supplementary material}\label{app:supp}

This appendix records three deferred studies of the rough rock joint of Section~\ref{sec:cv7}. Each
study isolates one question that the main text raises but does not develop. The first (S.1) asks whether
the transition-map detector survives being wired into the contact loop of an unsteady solve, rather than
only queried on a fixed point cloud. The second (S.2) asks whether the dilatant interface obeys the first
law over a full load cycle, with the energy ledger closed term by term. The third (S.3) asks whether an
independent explicit solver reproduces the same frictional response. We report each study with its
measured numbers and with the limit it exposes. The level set (a neural signed-distance function, or SDF)
runs throughout as the comparison baseline, on the same geometry and the same query set as the chart.

\subsection{Transition-map detection inside the contact loop}\label{app:supp:p3}

The main text reports the chart and the level set as static geometry estimators, each queried on a fixed
grid. Here we instead let the transition-map detector supply the gap and the normal that drive a
quasi-static direct-shear solve, so the detector is evaluated inside the Newton loop. The chart reads
contact as the boundary-to-boundary map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\!\circ\map{A}$, which sends a point on one
face to the mating point on the other: for a query point on face $A$, the height chart returns the mating
surface height on face $B$ directly, and the gap then follows as the signed normal separation between the
two. The level set returns the same gap as the value of the ambient neural SDF $\phi$, with normal
$\nbf=\grad\phi/\norm{\grad\phi}$.

Throughout, $\gn$ denotes the signed normal gap, with the convention $\gn<0$ in penetration. The detector
declares a query point in contact when its gap is negative, so the active set is the index set of those
points,
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:supp_activeset}
\mathcal{A}=\{\,p : \gn(\xbf_p)<0\,\},
\end{equation}
%
where $p$ indexes the query points and $\xbf_p$ is the position of point $p$. To score the active set, we
compare it against the analytic active set $\mathcal{A}^{\star}$, which we obtain from the closed-form
mating surface. The score is the symmetric agreement
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:supp_agreement}
\eta=1-\frac{\abs{\mathcal{A}\,\triangle\,\mathcal{A}^{\star}}}{N},
\end{equation}
%
in which $\triangle$ is the symmetric difference (the points the two sets disagree on) and $N$ is the
number of query points; $\eta=1$ means the detector and the closed form agree on every point. We measure
gap accuracy separately, as the relative root-mean-square error of the predicted gap $\gn$ against the
analytic gap $\gn^{\star}$,
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:supp_gaprmse}
e_g=\frac{\norm{\gn-\gn^{\star}}_{2}}{\norm{\gn^{\star}}_{2}}.
\end{equation}
%
The gap error matters because the gap, not the active-set membership, sets the contact pressure.

On $N=10^{5}$ query points sampled over the joint footprint, the chart detector reproduced the analytic
active set to $\eta=98.9\%$, against $95.8\%$ for the level set. On the gap, the chart was sharper by a
wider margin: $e_g=4.2\%$ for the chart against $44.7\%$ for the level set, a factor of $10.5$ on the
quantity that sets the contact pressure. Wired into the FEM shear, the chart-driven solve tracked the
analytic-surface solve closely, reaching a peak apparent friction $\muapp=0.566$ against the analytic
$0.513$, with the shear traction agreeing to within $9.5\%$ over the loading history. Table~\ref{tab:supp_p3}
collects these results.

One quantity runs the other way. At the asperity tips the level set carries the sharper
normal, $0.8^{\circ}$ of deviation against $10.3^{\circ}$ for the chart, because the random-Fourier height
field has a noisier gradient at the tips than the smooth ambient field does. The chart advantage is on the
gap and the active set, the two quantities that decide where contact occurs and how hard, not on the
pointwise normal direction at the sharpest features. We state the tip-normal result so the comparison is
not read as uniform: the level set is a fair baseline, and on the tip-normal direction it is the more
accurate estimator on this surface.

The chart detector costs $1.47\times$ the level set per query. A chart query is a single forward
evaluation of $\map{A}$ followed by the chart read of $\map{B}^{-1}$, with no level-set root find or ray
march, because the mating height is the chart output. The factor below two is the overhead of evaluating
two Fourier-feature charts in place of one ambient SDF, and it stays constant as the number of query
points grows. The shear traction and the emergent normal dilation that this detector produces over the
loading history appear in Figure~\ref{fig:supp_traction}.

\subsection{Cyclic loading and the energy ledger}\label{app:supp:p4}

The cyclic study drives the joint through a triangle-wave tangential schedule under constant normal
displacement. It accounts for every term of the first law. The friction model uses a stateful
return-map update, so stick and slip alternate and the shear-traction response forms hysteresis loops.
The Plesha asperity-degradation parameter decays across cycles, from $0.3405$ to $0.3377$ over four
cycles, and the contact Newton residual averages $7\times10^{-5}$. The peak apparent friction is
$\abs{\muapp}\approx0.33$.

The first law states that the external work fed into the joint must reappear as stored energy plus
dissipation. With $\gn<0$ in penetration and slip taken as plastic, the ledger reads
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:supp_ledger}
W_{\mathrm{ext}}
= \Delta U_{\mathrm{el}}
+ W_{\mathrm{pen}}
+ W_{\mathrm{stick}}
+ W_{\mathrm{fric}},
\end{equation}
%
where each term has a physical meaning. The left side, $W_{\mathrm{ext}}=\sum \fbf_{\mathrm{contact}}\cdot
\mathrm{d}\ubf_{\mathrm{platen}}$, is the machine work the contact tractions do on the moving platen. On
the right, $\Delta U_{\mathrm{el}}=\tfrac12\ubf^{\!\top}\!K\ubf$ is the recoverable strain energy of the
blocks, $W_{\mathrm{pen}}=\tfrac12\sum\varepsilon_n A\,\gn^{2}$ is the recoverable normal penalty energy,
$W_{\mathrm{stick}}$ is the recoverable tangential spring energy of the stuck nodes, and
$W_{\mathrm{fric}}=\sum\abs{\tbf_T}\,\abs{\mathrm{d}\bm{s}_p}\ge0$ is the frictional dissipation
accumulated over the plastic slip increments. The three recoverable terms make up the moving part of the
balance over a cycle: each one returns to its start value once the load closes,
$\Delta U_{\mathrm{el}}\to0$, $W_{\mathrm{pen}}\to0$, $W_{\mathrm{stick}}\to0$. The per-cycle closure
therefore reduces to a single ratio that should equal one,
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:supp_closure}
\Pi_{\mathrm{cyc}}
= \frac{W_{\mathrm{ext}}}
{\,W_{\mathrm{fric}}+\Delta U_{\mathrm{el}}+W_{\mathrm{pen}}+W_{\mathrm{stick}}\,}
\;\longrightarrow\; 1 .
\end{equation}
%
A value of $\Pi_{\mathrm{cyc}}=1$ means the work in matches the energy out, term for term, over the cycle.

Measured per cycle, $\Pi_{\mathrm{cyc}}=1.09$ on the first cycle and $1.01$ on the second, the latter
inside the acceptance band $[0.98,1.02]$. The first-cycle excess is the recoverable terms not yet having
returned to zero after a single traversal; by the second cycle they do return, and the ledger closes to
one percent. Figure~\ref{fig:supp_cyclic} shows the hysteresis loops and the per-cycle ledger.

The \emph{cumulative} ratio over the whole history is a different number, $0.17$, far from one. The
shear-only constant-normal machine work $W_{\mathrm{ext}}$ does not count the dissipation spent seating
the asperities under the initial normal load. The run begins with
$W_{\mathrm{fric}}[0]=0.91$ already accumulated while $W_{\mathrm{ext}}[0]=0$, because the normal-load
seating happens before the tangential schedule starts. That offset enters the denominator of the
cumulative ratio but never the numerator, so it depresses the ratio permanently. The offset is a
bookkeeping artifact, the unmeasured seating dissipation, not an energy violation. The per-cycle balance
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:supp_closure} differences the accumulated work across one full cycle and so cancels the
constant offset; that per-cycle balance is the correct first-law statement, and it closes.

\subsection{Explicit ChartMPM cross-check}\label{app:supp:p5}

The last study checks the frictional response with an independent solver. We discretize a deformable block
with the explicit finite-deformation ChartMPM (linear B-spline transfers, Neo-Hookean response) and shear
it against the \emph{same} band-limited rough surface that the chart-FEM uses, sliding the surface beneath
the block at a constant rate to impose direct-shear kinematics. The contact runs through the existing
per-particle channel: the gap comes from the geometric oracle, the normal force from the penalty model,
and the tangential force from regularised Coulomb friction, all scattered as physical-space per-particle
forces through the same particle-to-grid channel as gravity. The solver sees the floor as the analytic
SDF $\phi(\xbf)=z-h(x-u_x,y)$, where $h$ is the rough height field and $u_x$ is the imposed slide.

The explicit solve reproduced the Coulomb friction floor. A flat-bottomed block sliding on the rough floor
reached an emergent apparent friction $\muapp\approx0.34$ against the prescribed base $\mu=0.4$, within
about $14\%$, and converged stably under dynamic relaxation. This is an independent dynamic confirmation
of the frictional channel that the quasi-static FEM uses.

The explicit solve does not, however, confirm the dilatant interlock. The chart-FEM produces emergent
dilation and an apparent-friction enhancement above the base value because two rough faces ride over one
another. A \emph{mated} rough-on-rough block in the explicit solver, with the full surface in conformal
contact, is unstable in explicit penalty dynamics and diverges without an implicit or mortar contact
treatment. The cross-check therefore validates friction but not dilatancy. We state this limit rather
than hide it: the explicit MPM is a consistent check of the Coulomb response, and the emergent dilatancy
of Section~\ref{sec:cv7} rests instead on the quasi-static implicit chart-FEM, whose consistent tangent
drives the contact Newton residual to $\sim10^{-9}$. An implicit or mortar contact treatment for the
mated MPM case is the documented path to a full dynamic cross-check of dilatancy.
