\begin{abstract}
Computational contact is the correspondence between two surfaces; we formulate that correspondence as an
optimal-transport coupling of two boundary measures, in which the contact pairing is the Brenier map that
minimises a transport cost and the gap and traction follow as continuous, mass-preserving \emph{fields} assembled
mortar-consistently, in place of node-collocated, tributary-lumped penalties. Read on a learned coordinate atlas
the coupling is a boundary-to-boundary transition map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$ realising this transport in
two regimes of one framework: for two mated rough surfaces it is the arclength-monotone (Brenier) rearrangement
$\hat F_B^{-1}\circ\hat F_A$ (conforming limit), while the closest-point projection $\gn=\norm{\bm p-\bm c}-R$ familiar
from node-to-segment contact is its partial-support limit for a localised convex indenter, required where the
arclength map would smear a partial load across the interface. The discrete master interpolation is a partition of
unity along the correspondence, $\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)=1$, so a constant pressure transmits exactly across a
non-matching mesh: the contact patch test passes to $1.4\times10^{-16}$, while the node-lumped scheme produces a
node-spacing sawtooth ($67\times$ larger stress non-uniformity). Because the coupling is two-sided and
transpose-coupled, with a symmetric positive-semidefinite four-block tangent that is consistent at frozen geometry
(finite-difference verified to $3.45\times10^{-11}$), it solves a genuine two-body deformable--deformable problem
rather than rigid-master detection: on deformable Hertz contact between two elastic blocks the contact half-width
and peak pressure converge under mesh refinement to $2.75\%$ and $5.82\%$ of the closed form (a contact-edge
floor, not a vanishing discretisation error), and a $3\times3$ elastic disc array matches the equibiaxial closed
form to $0.58\%$ at full Newton. The charts are Fourier-feature coordinate networks whose random-feature embedding
defeats the spectral bias that low-passes a neural signed-distance function, held throughout as a matched baseline
on identical meshes so the measured accuracy gap is charged to the geometric representation alone. The formulation
is variationally consistent through the classical strong--weak--Galerkin sequence and verified against analytical
benchmarks (Hertz, Cattaneo--Mindlin, Brazilian, nine-disc, superformula, and Koch), on which the measure-coupling
model reaches the closed forms to $0.03\%$--$0.50\%$ where the conventional node-lumped model plateaus at
$1.59\%$--$11.15\%$ (Cattaneo stick radius, Hertz half-width)---each at its converged resolution---and ties it on
the smooth nine-disc field. As the empirical consequence of the same coupling on a conforming interface, by
smoothing the asperity slopes the level set under-predicts the peak shear strength of a real Inada-granite profile
by $61\%$ and the emergent dilatancy of a synthetic rough block by $98\%$, while the transition map recovers both
from the resolved geometry under plain Coulomb friction with no imposed dilatancy law, resolving what the level
set smooths.
\keywords{optimal transport \and measure coupling \and mortar method \and computational contact mechanics \and
transition map \and coordinate atlas \and neural signed-distance function \and Fourier-feature charts \and
rock-joint dilatancy}
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}

Two surfaces come into contact when each point of one is paired with a point of the other. We take that pairing
literally: contact is an optimal-transport coupling of the two boundary measures, and the gap and traction it
induces are continuous, mass-preserving fields rather than node-collocated penalties. Read on a learned coordinate
atlas, the pairing is a boundary-to-boundary transition map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$ that carries two
familiar limits of one coupling: for two mated rough surfaces it is the arclength-monotone (Brenier) rearrangement
of the quadratic-cost transport of the two boundary measures (its conforming limit), and the closest-point
projection familiar from node-to-segment contact is its partial-support limit for a localised convex indenter
(Section~\ref{sec:tmap}, Appendix~\ref{app:ot}). Posing contact this way
does two things classical detection cannot---it transmits a constant pressure exactly across a non-matching mesh,
and it couples two deformable bodies through a symmetric tangent---and it makes precise a fact the rest of the
paper measures: on rough, non-convex geometry it is the representation of the boundary, not the
constraint-enforcement scheme, that controls fidelity.

\subsection{Geometry, not the force law, controls contact fidelity}\label{sec:intro-motivation}

The shear strength of a rough rock joint is set by its asperity geometry, not by a material constant. When the two faces of a tensile
fracture are pressed together and sheared, the load is carried on the interlocking asperities, and the
joint cannot slide without riding up their flanks; the climb dilates the interface and raises its
resistance above that of a flat sawcut. The strength an engineer designs with is therefore set by the
distribution of asperity slopes, a property of the boundary, and not by any material constant. The paper makes two
claims. First, the contact correspondence is an optimal-transport coupling of two boundary measures
(Section~\ref{sec:tmap}); second, as its empirical consequence on hard geometry, \emph{the representation of the
boundary, not the constraint-enforcement scheme, controls contact fidelity}. We will
show that a fixed-capacity ambient signed-distance field smooths the mean asperity slope of a real
Inada-granite joint from $19.4^\circ$ to $12.5^\circ$ and, through the shear-strength law, thereby under-predicts
its peak strength by $61\%$, and on a synthetic rough block under-predicts the emergent dilatancy by $98\%$,
whereas a boundary chart of the same surfaces resolves the slopes and recovers the response.

A computational contact algorithm reduces, at each query point $\xbf$ near a body $X$, to two local
questions. Is $\xbf$ inside $X$, and by how much? This is the gap $\gn$, with the sign convention
$\gn<0\Leftrightarrow$ penetration used throughout. Along which direction is the surface? This is the
outward normal $\nbf$. Given the pair $(\gn,\nbf)$, the standard enforcement schemes---penalty,
augmented Lagrangian, mortar, Nitsche---convert it into nodal forces \citep{wriggers2006computational,
laursen2002computational,kikuchi1988contact}. On smooth, convex bodies the gap and the normal are smooth functions of position, every scheme converges to the
same answer, and accuracy is set by the enforcement. On geometrically complex bodies it breaks down. The gap and the normal
cease to be smooth, or even single-valued, and the geometric representation from which they are read becomes
the dominant control on accuracy. A rock joint, a cusped cam lobe, a faceted aggregate, or a self-similar
fractal boundary carries contact information at length scales that an ambient field of fixed capacity cannot
store. We realise the claim through contact read from a chart \emph{transition map} on a learned coordinate
atlas, measured against the level set on a verification ladder that runs from closed-form benchmarks to a real
rock joint.

\subsection{The level set and its two intrinsic weaknesses}\label{sec:intro-levelset}

The incumbent representation is the level set. For a body $A$ one stores a signed-distance function
$\phi_A:\R^d\to\R$, negative inside the body, increasingly represented by a neural network, and reads the
gap and the normal directly from the field and its gradient,
\begin{equation}
  \gn(\xbf)=\phi_A(\xbf),\qquad
  \nbf(\xbf)=\frac{\grad\phi_A(\xbf)}{\norm{\grad\phi_A(\xbf)}},
  \label{eq:sdf-gap-intro}
\end{equation}
so a single automatic-differentiation call delivers both quantities at once \citep{liu2020implicit,
kolev2003level}. To render $\phi_A$ a true signed \emph{distance} one trains it with the eikonal penalty,
which drives the gradient toward unit norm,
\begin{equation}
  \mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{eik}}
  =\mathbb{E}_{\xbf\sim\Omega}\!\left[\big(\norm{\grad\phi_A(\xbf)}-1\big)^2\right],
  \label{eq:eikonal}
\end{equation}
combined with surface and normal-alignment losses on a boundary point cloud. Equation~\eqref{eq:sdf-gap-intro}
is both the principal advantage of the representation and the source of its weaknesses. The level set is
retained throughout as the comparison baseline: on the geometry it can represent it is exactly as accurate as
the chart (Section~\ref{sec:verif}).

Two weaknesses are intrinsic to the level set rather than artefacts of training, and both bear directly on
contact. The first is \emph{medial-axis non-smoothness}. The exact Euclidean signed distance is only $C^0$
on the medial axis, the locus equidistant from two or more boundary points, where the closest-point map is
multivalued and $\grad\phi$ jumps. In a concavity the medial set reaches close to the surface, so the
gradient normal of \eqref{eq:sdf-gap-intro} degrades precisely where a non-convex contact forms. The second
is \emph{spectral bias}. A coordinate multilayer perceptron learns low frequencies first and high
frequencies slowly or never; its neural-tangent kernel is Laplace-like, with high-frequency power decaying
with frequency \citep{rahaman2019spectral}. A fixed-capacity neural $\phi_A$ therefore low-passes cusps,
asperities, and fine detail: it cannot represent a boundary sharper than its capacity ceiling, and it
smooths the geometry that, on a rough joint, carries the strength. Both weaknesses are measured in
Sections~\ref{sec:verif} and~\ref{sec:cv7}. The chart introduced next is built to avoid
both: it stores one fewer dimension, and it supplies the network the high frequencies it would otherwise
fail to learn.

\subsection{Related work}\label{sec:intro-related}

\paragraph{Variational foundations and constraint enforcement.}
Frictional contact is classically posed as a variational inequality: the Signorini conditions and the Coulomb
law define a constrained problem whose well-posedness was established by \citet{duvaut1976inequalities} and
whose finite element approximation is analysed by \citet{kikuchi1988contact}, \citet{glowinski1981numerical},
and \citet{haslinger1996numerical}, and surveyed in the variationally consistent setting by
\citet{wohlmuth2011variational}. The constraint is enforced numerically by penalty, augmented-Lagrangian, or
Nitsche regularisation \citep{wriggers2006computational,alart1991mixed,simo1992augmented,laursen1993continuum,
laursen2002computational,chouly2013nitsche,chouly2015symmetric} and by mortar or dual-multiplier discretisation
\citep{puso2004mortar,fischer2005frictionless,wohlmuth2011variational}. Much of this layer is independent of the
present contribution: penalty, augmented-Lagrangian, and Nitsche enforcement consume a gap, a normal, and a
pressure, and are indifferent to how the first two are produced. We change the geometric operator that produces
them and leave the enforcement theory intact.

\paragraph{Closest-point projection and its deficiencies.}
In the dominant node-to-segment tradition \citep{hallquist1985sliding,wriggers2006computational} the gap and
the normal are read from a closest-point projection of a slave point onto a master surface. The projection is
the source of a well-documented fragility: it is non-unique in concavities, its normal jumps across facet
edges, it can fail the contact patch test \citep{taylor1991patch}, and it returns a single foot even where a
body touches along several disjoint arcs \citep{zavarise2009nts,zavarise2009patchtest}. The
transition map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$ replaces this projection by a composition of charts: it is
single-valued, edge-continuous, two-sided (no designated master), and recovers every contact arc at once
(Section~\ref{sec:tmap}). The implicit-level-set material point method of \citet{liu2020implicit}, whose
normal-gap form our force machinery reuses, reads the gap from a per-body field and so shares the single-foot
reading the transition map removes.

\paragraph{Smoothing the master surface, and geometrically exact contact.}
Much subsequent work repaired the projection by smoothing the surface it acts on: $C^1$ B\'ezier, Hermite, and
spline interpolation of the master facets \citep{wriggers2001smooth}, and, more completely, isogeometric
contact on exact NURBS geometry \citep{temizer2011contact,delorenzis2014review}. Smoothing restores a
continuous normal field, but it does so by smoothing the very geometry whose sharpness, in the rough-surface
regime of this paper, carries the physics, and it retains the projection. Earlier mixed and
segment-to-segment formulations \citep{simo1985perturbed,zavarise1998segment} and, more completely, mortar and
dual-multiplier methods \citep{puso2004segment,popp2009finite,hueber2005primaldual} cure the over-constraint
and master/slave bias of node-to-segment contact by enforcing non-penetration in an integral sense, and
unbiased Nitsche contact
\citep{mlika2017unbiased} removes the master/slave designation altogether---yet both still build the surface
pairing from a projection or segmentation. Closest in spirit to the present work is the geometrically exact,
covariant theory of \citet{konyukhov2013computational}, which also parametrises the contacting surfaces by
intrinsic coordinates; it nonetheless establishes the contact pair by a covariant closest-point projection onto
an analytically or finite-element parametrised master surface. We differ in two ways: the charts are
\emph{learned} parametrisations of arbitrarily rough boundaries, and the pairing follows from chart composition
and inversion rather than any projection, so neither a master surface nor a medial axis is ever formed. A
survey of the detection machinery this replaces is given by \citet{yastrebov2013numerical}.

\paragraph{Implicit and unfitted geometry.}
A parallel tradition keeps the geometry implicit. Level-set and signed-distance representations
\citep{osher1988fronts,sethian1999levelset,osher2003levelset} carry a surface as the zero set of an ambient
field; unfitted and cut finite element methods \citep{hansbo2002unfitted,hansbo2004strongweak,burman2015cutfem,
burman2025cutfemreview}, the finite cell method \citep{parvizian2007finitecell,schillinger2015finitecell}, trace
finite elements for surface partial differential equations \citep{olshanskii2009tracefem,burman2015laplacebeltrami},
and level-set/X-FEM contact \citep{moes1999xfem,sukumar2001holesinclusions,dolbow2001frictionalcontact} let the
surface cut through a background mesh and repair the discretisation with Nitsche coupling and ghost-penalty
stabilisation \citep{burman2010ghostpenalty}. These methods buy freedom from body-fitted meshing; they pay with cut-cell quadrature, small-cut
conditioning, and a geometry whose accuracy is tied to the ambient field, which interpolates a distance through
the whole volume and so rounds sharp features and carries a non-differentiable medial axis in every concavity.
The boundary chart is the opposite design choice: explicit and one dimension lower, it carries no interior, no
medial axis, and no cut cells, and its surface fidelity is set by the learned parametrisation rather than the
background mesh.

\paragraph{Neural implicit geometry, spectral bias, and Fourier features.}
Neural signed-distance and occupancy networks \citep{park2019deepsdf,mescheder2019occupancy,chen2019imnet} and
periodic-activation networks \citep{sitzmann2020implicit} represent geometry implicitly and are trained by the
eikonal regularisation of \citet{gropp2020igr}. They inherit the spectral bias of coordinate networks---the
neural-tangent kernel \citep{jacot2018ntk} whose spectral bias low-passes high frequencies
\citep{rahaman2019spectral,xu2020frequency}---so a network of fixed capacity smooths sharp detail. Fourier-feature input encoding
\citep{tancik2020fourier,rahimi2007random}, which underlies the positional encoding of neural radiance fields
\citep{mildenhall2020nerf}, reshapes that kernel to a tunable cutoff; feature-grid level of detail
\citep{takikawa2021nglod} is the spatial alternative. We apply the encoding not to an ambient field but to a
$(d{-}1)$-dimensional boundary chart, where a prescribed cutoff costs $O((L/\ell)^{d-1})$ features against
$O((L/\ell)^{d})$ for a volumetric field (Section~\ref{sec:fourier}; the neural-field family is surveyed by
\citet{xie2022neuralfields}).

\paragraph{Neural atlases and learning-informed mechanics.}
Representing a surface by learned charts originates in computer vision: AtlasNet
\citep{groueix2018atlasnet} represents a surface as a collection of learned parametric patches, and the chart
autoencoders of \citet{schonsheck2019chart} learn chart-like maps of a data manifold; the data-driven atlases
of \citet{floryan2022charts} go further and stitch overlapping charts by explicit transition maps, but for
dynamical systems on manifolds rather than geometry, and none of these carries governing equations. The atlas
here instead serves as the computational domain of a contact boundary-value problem, with the
elasticity pulled back through the chart Jacobian and overlapping charts coupled by multiplicative Schwarz
\citep{sun2026neuralatlas}. This places the work within a broader programme of learning- and geometry-informed
computational mechanics---data-driven and physics-informed solvers \citep{kirchdoerfer2016datadriven,raissi2019pinn},
learned constitutive manifolds and graph embeddings \citep{wang2018multiscale,vlassis2020geometric,
xiao2022geometric,bahmani2022manifold,heider2020so3}, and discovered interface laws
\citep{wang2019metamodeling}---in which mechanics is carried on a learned geometric object rather than a
hand-built mesh or a curve-fit law. This inverts the usual dichotomy: in a physics-informed network the unknown
solution field is the network and the geometry is given \citep{raissi2019pinn}, and in a geometry-informed
neural operator the network is the solution map over a geometry supplied as an ambient signed-distance field
\citep{li2023gino}; here the network is the \emph{geometry itself}---a learned diffeomorphism onto the
boundary---while the mechanics is solved classically on top of it, so accuracy is governed by the geometric
approximation rather than by the optimisation of a residual.

\paragraph{Meshfree contact and rock-joint mechanics.}
The validation problem, direct shear of a rough joint, connects two further literatures. In the material point
method \citep{sulsky1994particle} contact is usually resolved by the multi-velocity-field algorithm
\citep{bardenhagen2000granular,bardenhagen2001contact}, which couples bodies only through shared grid nodes and
is blind to sub-grid asperities; the level-set MPM of \citet{liu2020implicit} is the immediate predecessor of
the present detector. The strength of a rough joint is classically captured by phenomenological laws that fit
dilatancy and friction to a roughness descriptor: the bilinear $\tan(\phi_b+i)$ of \citet{patton1966multiple},
with $\phi_b$ the basic friction angle and $i$ the asperity inclination; the JRC criterion of
\citet{barton1977shear,barton1990review}; the asperity-degradation model of \citet{plesha1987constitutive}; the
joint element of \citet{goodman1976methods}; and the coupled normal--shear models of \citet{saeb1992modelling}
and \citet{grasselli2003constitutive}. Natural joint surfaces are self-affine fractals over many decades
\citep{brown1985broad,power1991euclidean,candela2012roughness}, which is why a band-limited chart is their
natural representation. In the present framework the dilatancy and the apparent friction are not fitted to a
roughness index; they emerge from the resolved asperity geometry under plain Coulomb friction, with the Patton
law serving only as the analytical anchor against which the emergent quantities are checked.

\subsection{Contributions and organisation}\label{sec:intro-contributions}

The contributions of this work are the following.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Contact as an optimal-transport coupling.} We pose the contact correspondence as an
optimal-transport coupling of two boundary measures: the contact pairing is the Brenier map of the quadratic-cost
transport problem \citep{brenier1991polar,mccann1995existence}, from which the gap and the contact traction follow
as continuous, mass-preserving fields assembled mortar-consistently, in place of node-collocated, tributary-lumped
penalties (Section~\ref{sec:tmap:ot}, with the measure-theoretic backbone and the consistent tangent in
Appendix~\ref{app:ot}). The same coupling carries two regimes---the global arclength-monotone
(Brenier) rearrangement for two mated rough surfaces and the closest-point projection for a localised convex
indenter---which resolves the apparent tension between treating contact as a composition and treating it as a
projection, with the closest-point limit required for partial contact.

\item \textbf{Transition-map detector on a learned atlas.} We represent each body by a learned coordinate atlas of
its boundary and pose contact detection as a boundary-to-boundary transition map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$
between two such atlases, which realises this optimal-transport coupling in its two regimes, and from which the
gap, the contact normal, and every disjoint contact arc follow without an ambient field
(Sections~\ref{sec:atlas} and~\ref{sec:tmap}).

\item \textbf{Fourier-feature charts that defeat spectral bias.} We instantiate the atlas through Fourier-feature
charts, whose random-feature embedding defeats the spectral bias that low-passes a neural signed-distance
function, so that a $(d{-}1)$-dimensional boundary function resolves a prescribed cutoff where a $d$-dimensional
ambient field of comparable capacity cannot (Section~\ref{sec:atlas}). We develop the contact model in the
classical strong--weak--Galerkin sequence (Sections~\ref{sec:strong}--\ref{sec:discr}) and record two properties
of the chart detector: the normal it returns is the gradient of the gap, so contact is energy-conservative, and
the radial gap, though enlarged in magnitude, is sign-exact, so the chart and the level set share an active set on
star-shaped bodies.

\item \textbf{The two-body deformable--deformable unlock.} Because the measure coupling is two-sided and
transpose-coupled, with a symmetric positive-semidefinite four-block tangent $\bm K_{ms}=\bm K_{sm}^{\top}$ that is
consistent at frozen geometry (finite-difference verified to $3.45\times10^{-11}$), it solves a genuine two-body
deformable--deformable problem rather than rigid-master detection: deformable Hertz contact between two elastic
blocks converges under mesh refinement to $2.75\%$ on the half-width and $5.82\%$ on the peak pressure (CV-8), a
$3\times3$ elastic disc array matches the equibiaxial closed form to $0.58\%$ at full Newton (CV-9a), and the
partition-of-unity host weights $\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)=1$ (the partition of unity of the master host weights
along the correspondence, distinct from and downstream of the optimal-transport mass marginal that places the
foot; Appendix~\ref{app:ot:discrete}) transmit a constant pressure across a non-matching mesh to
$1.4\times10^{-16}$, a contact patch test the node-lumped scheme fails (Section~\ref{sec:ex_twobody},
Appendix~\ref{app:ot}).

\item \textbf{Verification ladder and rock-joint validation.} We verify the method against a closed-form ladder
(Hertz, Cattaneo--Mindlin, nine-disc, superformula, and Koch, with the Brazilian disc as a supporting appendix
check), on which the chart and the level set are equivalent for the convex and superposable cases and measurably
separate on the cusped superformula and the self-similar Koch boundary, and across which the measure-coupling
gap-function model reaches the closed forms $1.4\times$ to $370\times$ tighter than the conventional node-lumped
model---each at its own converged resolution, the Cattaneo stick radius $11.15\%$ off against the coupling's
$0.03\%$, with a tie on the smooth nine-disc field (Table~\ref{tab:ot_vs_conv}). As the empirical consequence of
the same coupling on a conforming interface, we validate it on the direct shear of a real Inada-granite tensile
fracture, where the apparent friction $\tan(\phi_b+i)$ and the normal dilation emerge from the resolved asperity
geometry under plain Coulomb friction, and where the level set, by smoothing the asperity slopes, under-predicts
strength and dilatancy on the quantity an engineer would design with (Section~\ref{sec:verif}).
\end{itemize}

The level set, held on identical meshes, isolates the representation axis, and the patch test isolates the
enforcement axis; the measure coupling improves on the enforcement while remaining interchangeable with the chart
on the representation. Because the detector enters the solver only through the gap--normal pair, the chart and the
level set are interchangeable at the force layer, and the measured accuracy gap is charged to the geometric
representation alone.

\paragraph{Notation.}
Bodies are labelled $A,B,\dots$; the interior of body $X$ is $\Omega_X\subset\R^d$ ($d=2,3$) and its boundary
$\partial\Omega_X$. A \emph{chart} is a parametrisation of a body's boundary, $\map{X}:\Theta_X\to
\partial\Omega_X$; a \emph{level set} is an ambient scalar field $\phi_X:\R^d\to\R$ whose zero set is
$\partial\Omega_X$. The transition map between two charted bodies is $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$. We write
the Macaulay bracket $\pos{z}=\max(0,z)$, bold symbols for vectors and tensors, and keep the sign convention
$\gn<0\Leftrightarrow$ penetration. The apparent friction coefficient measured from a contact run is $\muapp$,
compared against the Patton value $\tan(\phi_b+i)$.

The remainder of the paper is organised as follows. Section~\ref{sec:atlas} introduces the neural coordinate
atlas, the three chart families, and their Fourier-feature representation. Section~\ref{sec:govern} states the
governing equations of two-body frictional contact and reduces them to the reference chart through the decoder
Jacobian. Section~\ref{sec:tmap} is the methodological core: it poses contact as the optimal-transport measure coupling of
the two boundary measures and reads it on the atlas as the transition map. It develops, in order, the gap and the
conservative normal, the chart inversions, the optimal-transport coupling itself---the Brenier map, the two
limits, the mortar-consistent assembly, and the symmetric four-block two-body tangent
(Section~\ref{sec:tmap:ot})---the contact contribution to the weak form, and the interface constitutive law. Section~\ref{sec:discr} gives the chart-based finite element implementation.
Section~\ref{sec:verif} reports the numerical examples, from the closed-form ladder through the two-body
deformable--deformable optimal-transport solves (Section~\ref{sec:ex_twobody}) to the
real Inada-granite joint. Section~\ref{sec:disc} states the preconditions, limitations, and conclusions, and
Appendix~\ref{app:ot} supplies only the measure-theoretic backbone behind the main-body development of
Section~\ref{sec:tmap:ot}---the Kantorovich relaxation and $c$-cyclical monotonicity that make the Brenier map
the unique optimiser, the well-posedness of the two limits, the patch-test proof, and the
positive-semidefiniteness of the four-block tangent---with the decisive numerical gates.

\section{The neural coordinate atlas}\label{sec:atlas}

A body is described by charts of its boundary rather than by a field over the space it occupies. Let
$\Omega_X\subset\R^d$, $d\in\{2,3\}$, be the open region occupied by body $X$, with boundary $\partial\Omega_X$
a compact $(d{-}1)$-dimensional manifold. The classical description is the \emph{ambient level set}, a scalar
field $\phi_X:\R^d\to\R$ negative inside the body, from which the gap $\gn^X(\xbf)\in\R$ and the outward unit
normal $\nbf_X(\xbf)\in\Sph^{d-1}$ are the value and the normalised gradient,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sdf-gap}
\gn^X(\xbf)=\phi_X(\xbf),\qquad
\nbf_X(\xbf)=\frac{\grad\phi_X(\xbf)}{\norm{\grad\phi_X(\xbf)}} .
\end{equation}
A neural $\phi_X$ is trained as a signed distance by the eikonal objective with surface, normal, and sign
terms,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:sdf-objective}
\mathcal L_{\mathrm{SDF}}
=\mathbb E_{\Omega}\big[(\norm{\grad\phi}-1)^2\big]
+\lambda_s\,\mathbb E_{\partial\Omega}\,\abs{\phi}
+\lambda_n\,\mathbb E_{\partial\Omega}\big[1-\widehat{\grad\phi}\cdot\nbf_{\mathrm{gt}}\big]
+\lambda_e\,\mathbb E_{\Omega\setminus\partial\Omega}\,e^{-\beta\abs{\phi}} ,
\end{equation}
with $\nbf_{\mathrm{gt}}$ the sampled surface normal; the level set is carried throughout as the comparison
baseline of Section~\ref{sec:verif}. A \emph{coordinate chart} of the boundary is a map
\begin{equation}\label{eq:chart}
\map{X}:\Theta_X\to\partial\Omega_X,\qquad \Theta_X\subset\R^{d-1},
\end{equation}
from a parameter domain $\Theta_X$ onto the surface, and an \emph{atlas} is a collection of such charts with
the transition maps that relate them where they overlap. The chart carries one dimension fewer than the field,
models no interior, and has no medial axis (Figure~\ref{fig:atlas-chart}).

Three chart families are used. The \emph{radial chart} represents a star-shaped body through a radius
function $\rho_X:\Sph^{d-1}\to\R^{+}$, with centre $c_X\in\R^d$ and orientation $Q_X\in SO(d)$, mapping a
direction $\hat\ubf\in\Sph^{d-1}$ to
\begin{equation}\label{eq:radial-chart}
\map{X}(\hat\ubf)=c_X+Q_X\,\rho_X(\hat\ubf)\,\hat\ubf ,\qquad
\rho(\theta)=\Big(\big\lvert\tfrac1a\cos\tfrac{m\theta}{4}\big\rvert^{n_2}
+\big\lvert\tfrac1b\sin\tfrac{m\theta}{4}\big\rvert^{n_3}\Big)^{-1/n_1},
\end{equation}
the second expression the Gielis superformula, which sharpens into cusps for $n_1<1$ \citep{gielis2003generic}.
The \emph{height chart} represents a single-valued rough surface as a graph $\map{X}(x)=(x,h_X(x))$ over
$x\in\mathcal D\subset\R^{d-1}$, with $h_X:\mathcal D\to\R$. The \emph{decoder chart} $D_X:U\to\R^d$ maps the
reference cube $U=[-r,r]^d$ to a general body and is inverted by Newton iteration \eqref{eq:newton-invert}; it
carries the elasticity of Section~\ref{sec:govern}, while the radial and height charts supply the closed-form
gaps of Section~\ref{sec:tmap}.

\paragraph{Relation to the neural-atlas method.}
The construction above is the contact-mechanics instance of the geometry-informed neural atlas
\citep{sun2026neuralatlas}, whose notation we retain. There, a single body $\Omega$ is covered by an atlas
$\mathcal A=\{(\widehat\Omega_i,\varphi_i)\}_{i=1}^{M}$ of overlapping volumetric charts
$\varphi_i:\widehat\Omega_i\to\Omega_i$ with Jacobian $\mathbf J_i=D_{\boldsymbol\zeta}\varphi_i$ and local
inverse $\pi_i=\varphi_i^{-1}$; neighbouring charts communicate on their overlap
$\Omega_{ij}=\Omega_i\cap\Omega_j$ through the \emph{intra-body transition map}
\begin{equation}\label{eq:intra-tmap}
\psi_{ij}=\pi_j\circ\varphi_i:\ \varphi_i^{-1}(\Omega_{ij})\subset\widehat\Omega_i\longrightarrow\widehat\Omega_j,
\qquad \Omega_{ij}=\Omega_i\cap\Omega_j,
\end{equation}
which re-expresses a point's chart-$i$ coordinate in chart $j$. The present
work carries the same composition \emph{across the gap between two bodies}: the contact detector of
Section~\ref{sec:tmap} reads the gap and the normal from the \emph{inter-body transition map}
$\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}=\pi_B\circ\map{A}$, the boundary-chart analogue of $\psi_{ij}$~\eqref{eq:intra-tmap}
(Figure~\ref{fig:atlas-extension}). The geometry, the governing equations, and the chart-Jacobian pull-back are
inherited unchanged; what is new is that the transition map now joins the boundaries of two separate atlases,
turning a coordinate change into a contact-pairing operator.

\subsection{Fourier-feature representation}\label{sec:fourier}

A coordinate network applied to the raw parameter cannot resolve a sharp surface, since its kernel low-passes
high frequencies. The parameter is therefore encoded through a fixed sinusoidal bank before the network: with
$\tilde x\in[-1,1]^d$ the normalised parameter, $B\in\R^{K\times d}$ a bank of $K$ frequency rows, and the
feature map $\gamma:[-1,1]^d\to\R^{2K}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma}
\gamma(x)=\big[\cos(2\pi B\tilde x),\ \sin(2\pi B\tilde x)\big]\in\R^{2K},\qquad
h_\theta(x)=\mathrm{base}+\mathrm{MLP}_\theta\!\big(\gamma(x)\big),
\end{equation}
the bank $B$ fixed and only $\theta$ and the offset trained \citep{tancik2020fourier,rahimi2007random}. The
banks are log-spaced (1-D height), Gaussian (2-D height and decoder relief), and integer-harmonic (radial,
periodic). The radial bank is integer-harmonic, so the chart is periodic on the circle of directions by
construction; with $K$ harmonics and a learnable mean radius $\rho_0$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gamma-radial}
\gamma_{\mathrm{rad}}(\psi)=\big[\cos k\psi,\ \sin k\psi\big]_{k=1}^{K}\in\R^{2K},\qquad
\rho_\theta(\psi)=\mathrm{softplus}\!\big(\rho_0+\mathrm{MLP}_\theta(\gamma_{\mathrm{rad}}(\psi))\big)>0,
\end{equation}
which is the network that produces the cusped-superformula radial chart of Section~\ref{sec:verif_cv5}. A chart
enters a computation only after it passes three checks: reconstruction RMSE below a few
percent of the surface RMS, a positive chart-Jacobian determinant on every element, and recovery of the
$O(h^2)$ rate in a manufactured-solution study. Figure~\ref{fig:fourier-spectral} shows the encoding at work on
a real rough surface: the same network, trained on a granite-joint profile with and without the Fourier
features, either tracks the asperity slopes or low-passes them into a smoothed graph.

\begin{remark}[Spectral bias and the band-limited kernel]\label{prop:ntk}
A raw-parameter network has a Laplace-like neural-tangent kernel \citep{jacot2018ntk} with spectrum
$\lambda(k)\sim\norm{k}^{-(d+1)}$, so each mode of the target relaxes as $\exp(-\eta\lambda(k)t)$ and high
frequencies are unlearned at finite budget \citep{rahaman2019spectral,xu2020frequency}. The encoding
\eqref{eq:gamma} replaces this kernel by the stationary $\sum_j\cos(2\pi b_j\!\cdot\!(\tilde x-\tilde x'))$,
which for $b_j\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2 I)$ has, by Bochner's theorem, expectation
$\exp(-2\pi^2\sigma^2\norm{\tilde x-\tilde x'}^2)$, flat to the tunable cutoff $2\pi\sigma$
\citep{tancik2020fourier}.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Capacity]\label{cor:capacity}
To resolve a wavelength $\ell$ over a domain of size $L$, a $(d{-}1)$-dimensional boundary chart needs
$K\sim(L/\ell)^{d-1}$ features against $(L/\ell)^{d}$ for a uniform field. The margin narrows against an
adaptive narrow-band level set; the claim of Section~\ref{sec:verif} is that, at fixed capacity, the chart
resolves slopes the \emph{neural} field of comparable size smooths (granite joint: $2.3~\mu$m against
$107~\mu$m).
\end{remark}

The phrase ``comparable size'' is auditable. Table~\ref{tab:capacity-audit} reports the trainable parameter
counts of the three representations, instantiated directly from the code (the Fourier bank $B$ is a fixed,
non-trainable buffer and is excluded). The ambient level set carries \emph{more} trainable capacity than the
height chart---$83{,}073$ against $66{,}178$, a $25.5\%$ surplus---and still reconstructs the granite joint
$47\times$ worse and smooths the mean asperity slope by $7^\circ$ (Section~\ref{sec:cv7}). The advantage is
therefore representation-limited, not capacity-limited.

\section{Governing equations and variational formulation on the coordinate chart}\label{sec:govern}\label{sec:strong}

With the geometry of each body fixed by the atlas of Section~\ref{sec:atlas}, this section poses the governing mechanics.
The bodies are linear elastic and quasi-static, posed in the small-strain regime. Each body is the image of a
reference cube under a trained chart decoder, so the variational problem is integrated on the chart and pulled
back to the physical body by the Piola identity of the neural atlas \citep{sun2026neuralatlas}. The strong form,
the virtual-work statement, and the chart pull-back are stated in turn; the discrete Galerkin form follows in
Section~\ref{sec:discr}.

\subsection{Strong form}\label{sec:strong:strong}

Let two bodies occupy open bounded Lipschitz domains $\Omega^A,\Omega^B\subset\R^d$, $d\in\{2,3\}$, indexed by
$\alpha\in\{A,B\}$, with outward unit normal $\nbf^\alpha\in\Sph^{d-1}$ and boundary partitioned into disjoint,
relatively open Dirichlet, Neumann, and candidate-contact parts,
$\partial\Omega^\alpha=\overline{\Gamma_u^\alpha}\cup\overline{\Gamma_t^\alpha}\cup\overline{\Gamma_c^\alpha}$,
$\Gamma_u^\alpha\neq\varnothing$. The displacement $\ubf^\alpha:\Omega^\alpha\to\R^d$ induces the infinitesimal
strain and, through a symmetric positive-definite elasticity tensor $\mathbb C^\alpha\in
\mathrm{Lin}(\R^{d\times d}_{\mathrm{sym}})$, the Cauchy stress,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:constitutive}
\bm\varepsilon(\ubf^\alpha)=\tfrac12\big(\grad\ubf^\alpha+\grad^{\!\top}\!\ubf^\alpha\big)\in\R^{d\times d}_{\mathrm{sym}},\qquad
\bm\sigma^\alpha=\mathbb C^\alpha:\bm\varepsilon(\ubf^\alpha)\in\R^{d\times d}_{\mathrm{sym}} .
\end{equation}
Given a body force $\bm b^\alpha\in[L^2(\Omega^\alpha)]^d$, a prescribed boundary displacement
$\bar\ubf^\alpha\in[H^{1/2}(\Gamma_u^\alpha)]^d$, and a prescribed traction
$\bar\tbf^\alpha\in[L^2(\Gamma_t^\alpha)]^d$, quasi-static equilibrium is the strong-form boundary-value problem
\begin{equation}\label{eq:balance}
\grad\!\cdot\bm\sigma^\alpha+\bm b^\alpha=\bm0\ \ \text{in }\Omega^\alpha;\qquad
\ubf^\alpha=\bar\ubf^\alpha\ \text{on }\Gamma_u^\alpha,\quad
\bm\sigma^\alpha\nbf^\alpha=\bar\tbf^\alpha\ \text{on }\Gamma_t^\alpha,
\end{equation}
closed on $\Gamma_c$ by the contact conditions of Section~\ref{sec:tmap}, in which the gap and the normal are
read from the atlas.

\subsection{Virtual work}\label{sec:weak}

The strong form \eqref{eq:balance} holds pointwise and demands more regularity than a contact solution
possesses; the variational form relaxes it to an integral statement, one that later admits the contact
constraint and the chart pull-back without further change. Define the trial and test spaces
\begin{equation}\label{eq:spaces}
\mathcal S^\alpha=\big\{\ubf\in[H^1(\Omega^\alpha)]^d:\ubf=\bar\ubf^\alpha\ \text{on}\ \Gamma_u^\alpha\big\},\qquad
\mathcal V^\alpha=\big\{\delta\ubf\in[H^1(\Omega^\alpha)]^d:\delta\ubf=\bm0\ \text{on}\ \Gamma_u^\alpha\big\},
\end{equation}
and set $\mathcal S=\mathcal S^A\times\mathcal S^B$, $\mathcal V=\mathcal V^A\times\mathcal V^B$. Multiplying the
balance \eqref{eq:balance} by $\delta\ubf^\alpha\in\mathcal V^\alpha$, integrating over $\Omega^\alpha$, and
integrating the stress-divergence term by parts through the divergence theorem give the weighted-residual
identity
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ibp}
\int_{\Omega^\alpha}\!\big(\grad\!\cdot\bm\sigma^\alpha+\bm b^\alpha\big)\!\cdot\delta\ubf^\alpha\,dV=0
\ \Longrightarrow\
-\!\int_{\Omega^\alpha}\!\bm\sigma^\alpha:\grad\delta\ubf^\alpha\,dV
+\!\int_{\partial\Omega^\alpha}\!(\bm\sigma^\alpha\nbf^\alpha)\!\cdot\delta\ubf^\alpha\,dA
+\!\int_{\Omega^\alpha}\!\bm b^\alpha\!\cdot\delta\ubf^\alpha\,dV=0 ,
\end{equation}
in which $\bm\sigma^\alpha:\grad\delta\ubf^\alpha=\bm\sigma^\alpha:\bm\varepsilon(\delta\ubf^\alpha)$ by the
symmetry of $\bm\sigma^\alpha$. The boundary integral vanishes on $\Gamma_u^\alpha$ where
$\delta\ubf^\alpha=\bm0$, equals $\int_{\Gamma_t^\alpha}\bar\tbf^\alpha\!\cdot\delta\ubf^\alpha\,dA$ on the
Neumann part, and contributes the contact traction on $\Gamma_c^\alpha$. Summing over $\alpha$, the variational
problem reads: find $\ubf\in\mathcal S$ such that, for all $\delta\ubf\in\mathcal V$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weak}
\sum_{\alpha}\!\int_{\Omega^\alpha}\!\bm\sigma^\alpha:\bm\varepsilon(\delta\ubf^\alpha)\,dV
-\sum_{\alpha}\Big[\int_{\Omega^\alpha}\!\bm b^\alpha\!\cdot\delta\ubf^\alpha\,dV
+\int_{\Gamma_t^\alpha}\!\bar\tbf^\alpha\!\cdot\delta\ubf^\alpha\,dA\Big]
+\delta W_c=0 ,
\end{equation}
where $\delta W_c$ is the contact virtual work on $\Gamma_c$, supplied in Section~\ref{sec:tmap}.

\subsection{Pull-back to the reference chart by the Piola transform}\label{sec:govern:chart}

The weak form \eqref{eq:weak} is still posed on the physical bodies $\Omega^\alpha$; to evaluate it on the
atlas, each integral is pulled back to the reference chart. Each body is the diffeomorphic image of a reference
cube $U=[-r,r]^d\subset\R^d$ under the trained chart decoder $D_\theta:U\to\Omega^\alpha$,
$\xbf=D_\theta(\bm\xi)$, with $\det\bm J(\bm\xi)>0$ for $\bm\xi\in U$
(Section~\ref{sec:fourier}). Its Jacobian, evaluated by automatic differentiation, is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:chart-jacobian}
\bm J(\bm\xi)=\frac{\partial D_\theta}{\partial\bm\xi}(\bm\xi)\in\R^{d\times d},
\end{equation}
and fields on $\Omega^\alpha$ are represented on $U$, with physical gradients and the volume element pulling
back as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pushforward}
\grad_{\xbf}(\cdot)=\grad_{\bm\xi}(\cdot)\,\bm J^{-1},\qquad dV=\abs{\det\bm J}\,d\bm\xi .
\end{equation}
The internal virtual work of \eqref{eq:weak} is then pulled back to $U$ by the Piola identity
\citep{sun2026neuralatlas}. Writing $\bm P\in\R^{d\times d}$ for the first Piola--Kirchhoff stress (equal to
$\bm\sigma$ in the small-strain regime) and using the identity $\bm P:(\bm G\bm J^{-1})=
(\bm P\bm J^{-\top}):\bm G$, valid for every $\bm G\in\R^{d\times d}$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:piola}
\int_{\Omega^\alpha}\!\bm P:\grad_{\xbf}\delta\ubf\,dV
=\int_{U}\!\bm P:\big(\grad_{\bm\xi}\delta\ubf\,\bm J^{-1}\big)\,\abs{\det\bm J}\,d\bm\xi
=\int_{U}\!\bm P_{\mathrm{chart}}:\grad_{\bm\xi}\delta\ubf\,d\bm\xi ,\qquad
\bm P_{\mathrm{chart}}:=\abs{\det\bm J}\,\bm P\,\bm J^{-\top}\in\R^{d\times d},
\end{equation}
so the elasticity is integrated over the reference cube $U$, and the Piola transform $\bm P_{\mathrm{chart}}$ is
the only chart-dependent stress. A steep neural chart can make $\bm J$ ill conditioned;
$\bm J^{-1}$ and $\abs{\det\bm J}$ are therefore formed from the singular-value decomposition $\bm J=\bm U
\bm\Sigma\bm V^{\!\top}$ with the spectrum floored,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:svd-stab}
\bm\Sigma_{\!s}=\max(\bm\Sigma,\sigma_{\!f}),\qquad
\bm J^{-1}=\bm V\,\bm\Sigma_{\!s}^{-1}\,\bm U^{\!\top},\qquad
\abs{\det\bm J}=\textstyle\prod_i\Sigma_{s,i},
\end{equation}
and an element is admitted only if no singular value was floored and $\abs{\det\bm J}>\det_f$, with
$\sigma_{\!f}=10^{-9}$, $\det_f=10^{-12}$. The discrete Galerkin form of \eqref{eq:weak} on the chart is given
in Section~\ref{sec:discr}.

\begin{remark}[Atlas of overlapping charts]\label{rem:schwarz}
A body of non-trivial topology, or one not covered by a single star-shaped or graph chart, is represented by
several \emph{overlapping} charts whose chart-local solutions are reconciled by a multiplicative Schwarz
iteration on the overlap graph, with a partition-of-unity blend on the overlaps \citep{sun2026neuralatlas}. The
numerical examples below use one chart per body; the multi-chart coupling is exercised in the companion atlas
study.
\end{remark}


\section{Contact as optimal transport: the transition-map measure coupling}\label{sec:tmap}\label{sec:ot}

Every contact formulation rests on two geometric quantities evaluated at each candidate point: the gap $\gn$,
which measures how far the surfaces are from touching, and the outward unit normal $\nbf$, which orients the
pressure. These two quantities can be obtained by projecting one body onto the mating surface or by composing
boundary charts, and that choice is what separates the present method from the node-to-surface tradition. The classical
node-to-surface treatment recovers them by \emph{projection}: a point on one body is projected onto the other,
and the closest-point distance and the surface normal at the foot of that projection are taken as the gap and
the normal. This projection is the origin of much of the difficulty in computational contact. It is non-unique
in a concavity, where several feet are equidistant; it is ill-conditioned where the master surface curves
sharply; and it returns a single foot even when one body touches its neighbour along several disjoint patches.
The level set inherits the same weakness in another guise: it reads $\gn$ and $\nbf$ from the ambient field
through \eqref{eq:sdf-gap}, but the field must interpolate a distance function through the whole volume, so its
accuracy decays exactly where the surface is sharpest.

The atlas takes a different route. Because each body already carries a chart of its own boundary, the gap and
the normal need not be recovered by a search at all: a surface point of one body is carried directly to its
mate on the other by \emph{composing} one chart with the inverse of the other. This composition is the
\emph{transition map} from which the formulation below reads the gap and the normal. It replaces a minimisation
with an evaluation that never leaves the surface and, as the following subsections establish, returns the gap
with the exact sign (Remark~\ref{prop:active-set}), the normal as a gap gradient (Remark~\ref{prop:matched-normal}),
and all contact patches rather than only the nearest one (Remark~\ref{lemma:multiarc}). This composition is not a
convenience: Section~\ref{sec:tmap:ot} identifies it as the optimal-transport coupling of the two boundary
measures---the Brenier map of the quadratic-cost transport problem
\citep{brenier1991polar,mccann1995existence}---of which the closest-point projection is the partial-support limit
and the arclength-monotone rearrangement the conforming limit, and from which the gap and the traction follow as
continuous, mass-preserving fields assembled mortar-consistently.

\subsection{Contact kinematics}\label{sec:tmap:kin}

On $\Gamma_c$ the bodies may not interpenetrate, contact pushes but does not pull, and pressure builds only
where they touch. Let the contact pressure be $t_N=-\bm\sigma\nbf\cdot\nbf\in\R_{\ge0}$, the gap
$\gn:\Gamma_c\to\R$, the outward unit normal $\nbf\in\Sph^{d-1}$, the tangential traction $\tbf_T$ and slip
velocity $\vbf_T$ in the tangent plane $\{\vbf\in\R^d:\vbf\cdot\nbf=0\}$, and the friction coefficient
$\mu\ge0$. The Signorini--Hertz--Moreau and Coulomb conditions read
\begin{equation}\label{eq:signorini}
\gn\ge0,\quad t_N\ge0,\quad \gn\,t_N=0;\qquad
\norm{\tbf_T}\le\mu\,t_N,\quad \tbf_T=-\mu\,t_N\frac{\vbf_T}{\norm{\vbf_T}}\ \text{if}\ \vbf_T\neq\bm0,
\end{equation}
with the sign convention $\gn<0\Leftrightarrow$ penetration.

These conditions hold point by point on $\Gamma_c$; what remains is to evaluate the gap and the normal that
enter them. For two charted bodies $A,B$ the transition map does so in two steps. A boundary parameter
$\theta_A\in\Theta_A$ of body $A$ is first carried into physical space by its own chart,
$\xbf=\map{A}(\theta_A)$; the inverse chart of $B$ then returns the parameter $\psi_B\in\Theta_B$ of the mating
point, so the composition takes one surface coordinate straight to its counterpart on the other body,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:tmap-def}
\tmap:\ \theta_A\longmapsto\psi_B=\big(\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}\big)(\theta_A),
\end{equation}
as drawn in Figure~\ref{fig:tm-concept}. The construction is an evaluation followed by a chart inversion---the
same operation an atlas uses to pass between two overlapping charts, here applied across the gap between two
bodies, and requiring no closest-point search. The outward normal follows from the chart tangents
$\tbf_i=\partial\map{}/\partial\xi^i$: in three dimensions it is their normalised cross product, in two
dimensions the quarter-turn of the single tangent,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:jacobian-normal}
\nbf=\frac{\tbf_1\times\tbf_2}{\norm{\tbf_1\times\tbf_2}}\ \ (d{=}3),\qquad
\nbf=\frac{\bm R_{90}\,\tbf}{\norm{\tbf}}\ \ (d{=}2),\qquad
\tbf_i=\frac{\partial\map{}}{\partial\xi^i},
\end{equation}
with $\bm R_{90}$ the in-plane quarter rotation; the normal is objective, $\nbf(Q\xbf+\bm c)=Q\nbf(\xbf)$. The
two single-valued chart families return the gap and normal in closed form, derived below. For a star-shaped
obstacle the gap is a radius subtraction in the body frame: with $\bm d=Q_B^\top(\xbf-c_B)$, $r=\norm{\bm d}$,
$\dhat=\bm d/r$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:radial-gap}
\gn^B(\xbf)=r-\rho_B(\dhat) ,
\end{equation}
negative exactly when the point lies inside.

The inverse chart $\map{B}^{-1}$ takes three forms. For a radial chart it is the closed-form direction
$\psi=\angle\,Q_B^\top(\xbf-c_B)$. For a height chart there is nothing to invert; the gap and normal are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:height-gap}
g_{\mathrm{vert}}(x,z)=z-h_\theta(x),\qquad
\nbf=\frac{(-h_\theta'(x),\,1)}{\sqrt{1+h_\theta'^2(x)}},
\end{equation}
with three-dimensional analogue $\nbf=(-h_x,-h_y,1)/\sqrt{1+\norm{\grad h}^2}$. The solver receives the vertical
proxy $g_{\mathrm{vert}}$; its projection onto the surface normal is the true minimal gap,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:height-gap-proj}
g_\perp=g_{\mathrm{vert}}\,(\nbf\cdot\hat{\bm e}_z)=\frac{z-h_\theta(x)}{\sqrt{1+h_\theta'^2(x)}},
\end{equation}
exact in sign and accurate to $O(\mathrm{slope}^2)$ in magnitude, with $\abs{g_{\mathrm{vert}}}\ge\abs{g_\perp}$,
so the proxy is conservative-large like the radial gap. For a decoder chart the
inverse solves $D_B(\bm\xi)=\xbf$ by Newton iteration,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:newton-invert}
\bm\xi^{(k+1)}=\bm\xi^{(k)}-\bm J^{-1}\big(D_B(\bm\xi^{(k)})-\xbf\big),\qquad
\bm J=\frac{\partial D_B}{\partial\bm\xi},
\end{equation}
to the residual tolerance $10^{-8}$, with $\bm J^{-1}$ stabilised as in \eqref{eq:svd-stab}.

The normal returned with the radial gap must be its gradient, or the contact force is non-conservative. Since
$F(\xbf)=r-\rho(\dhat)$ depends on $\xbf$ through a unit direction, the raw direction gradient is projected
onto the tangent plane of the direction sphere,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:matched-normal}
\grad_S\rho=(I-\dhat\dhat^\top)\,\grad_{\hat d}\rho,\qquad
\grad_d F=\dhat-\frac{\grad_S\rho}{r},\qquad
\nbf=\widehat{\,Q\,\grad_d F\,}.
\end{equation}

The gap and normal constructed this way carry three properties a closest-point projection lacks: a conservative
normal (Remark~\ref{prop:matched-normal}), a sign-exact gap (Remark~\ref{prop:active-set}), and completeness
across disjoint contact patches (Remark~\ref{lemma:multiarc}).

\begin{remark}[Energy consistency]\label{prop:matched-normal}
Differentiating $F$ with $\partial\dhat/\partial(Q^\top\xbf)=r^{-1}(I-\dhat\dhat^\top)$ gives
$\grad_{\xbf}F=Q\,\grad_d F=\norm{\grad_d F}\nbf$, so the normal of \eqref{eq:matched-normal} is the unit gap
gradient and the penalty traction is the exact gradient of the penalty potential of \eqref{eq:penalty}. The
un-projected gradient gives $\nbf\neq\widehat{\grad F}$, a force field with non-zero circulation.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Sign-exact gap]\label{prop:active-set}
For a star-shaped body the radial gap and the perpendicular gap satisfy $g_{\mathrm{rad}}=g_\perp/\cos\alpha+
O(g_\perp^2)$ with $\cos\alpha=\dhat\cdot\nbf>0$, so $\abs{g_{\mathrm{rad}}}\ge\abs{g_\perp}$ and the signs
agree. The chart and level-set active sets therefore coincide, $\{g_{\mathrm{rad}}<0\}=\{g_\perp<0\}=\Omega_B$;
the penalty is at worst slightly stiffer, never softer.\footnote{Sign-exactness of the active set---that with
$\cos\alpha>0$ the radial gap and the perpendicular gap $g_\perp=g_{\mathrm{rad}}\cos\alpha$ share the same sign,
so $\{g_\perp<0\}=\{g_{\mathrm{rad}}<0\}$ select the identical active set (the $1/\cos\alpha$ factor is a magnitude
bias, not a sign bias)---is machine-checked in Lean~4 (\code{lean/OTContact/RadialSign.lean}, theorems
\code{active\_set\_iff}, \code{radial\_gap\_sign\_agree}).}
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[Multi-arc completeness]\label{lemma:multiarc}
The chart active set $\{\theta_A:\gn^B(\map{A}(\theta_A))<0\}$ is the open sublevel set of a continuous
function, hence a disjoint union of arcs. A sweep of $\map{A}$ marks every arc and recovers all contact
patches (Figure~\ref{fig:tm-multiarc}), whereas a single closest-point projection returns at most one.
\end{remark}

\subsection{The transition map as an optimal-transport measure coupling}\label{sec:tmap:ot}

The transition map \eqref{eq:tmap-def} is not an ad hoc construction but the optimal-transport coupling of the
two boundary measures, and that identification supplies the gap and the traction as continuous, mass-preserving
\emph{fields} rather than node-collocated penalties. Each boundary chart pushes Lebesgue measure on its
parameter line forward to the arclength measure on the face; for a single-valued face $z=h_X(x)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-measure-body}
d\mu_X=(\map{X})_\#\,dx=\sqrt{1+\abs{h_X'(x)}^2}\,dx ,
\end{equation}
the object the contact pairing couples between the two bodies. The coupling matches the \emph{normalised}
cumulative measures $\hat F_X=F_X/F_X(\theta_{\max})$, so the two marginals carry equal total mass even when the
faces have unequal arclength; writing $\hat\mu_X=\mu_X/\mu_X(\Gamma_c^X)$ for the resulting probability
marginals, a correspondence $\chi:\Gamma_c^A\to\Gamma_c^B$ is \emph{admissible} when it transports the slave
marginal onto the master marginal, $\chi_\#\hat\mu_A=\hat\mu_B$. Among admissible correspondences the Monge
problem selects the one of least quadratic-cost transport,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-monge-body}
\tmap=\arg\min_{\chi_\#\hat\mu_A=\hat\mu_B}\int_{\Gamma_c^A} c\big(\xbf,\chi(\xbf)\big)\,d\hat\mu_A(\xbf),\qquad
c(\xbf,\ybf)=\tfrac12\norm{\xbf-\ybf}^2,
\end{equation}
the quadratic cost the choice that makes the contact pairing a metric-projection map. The Monge
problem~\eqref{eq:ot-monge-body} can be infeasible---no map need carry an atom of $\hat\mu_A$ to a spread of
$\hat\mu_B$---so existence is established through its convex Kantorovich relaxation, which minimises over
transport plans $\pi\in\Pi(\hat\mu_A,\hat\mu_B)$ (couplings on $\Gamma_c^A\times\Gamma_c^B$ with the prescribed
marginals) rather than over maps,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-kantorovich-body}
\inf_{\pi\in\Pi(\hat\mu_A,\hat\mu_B)}\ \int_{\Gamma_c^A\times\Gamma_c^B}\!\! c(\xbf,\ybf)\,d\pi(\xbf,\ybf),\qquad
\Pi(\hat\mu_A,\hat\mu_B)=\big\{\pi\ge0:\ (P_A)_\#\pi=\hat\mu_A,\ (P_B)_\#\pi=\hat\mu_B\big\},
\end{equation}
with $P_A,P_B$ the coordinate projections \citep{villani2009optimal,santambrogio2015optimal}. The optimiser is
characterised geometrically: the support of an optimal plan is $c$-cyclically monotone, so that for every finite
cycle of matched pairs $\{(\xbf_i,\ybf_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}\subset\operatorname{supp}\pi$ no rematching lowers the
total cost,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-cyclical-body}
\sum_{i=1}^{N} c(\xbf_i,\ybf_i)\ \le\ \sum_{i=1}^{N} c\big(\xbf_i,\ybf_{\sigma(i)}\big)
\qquad\text{for every permutation }\sigma ,
\end{equation}
the transport analogue of the rearrangement inequality \citep{villani2009optimal,santambrogio2015optimal}. For
the quadratic cost and an absolutely continuous slave marginal the relaxation~\eqref{eq:ot-kantorovich-body} is
tight: the optimal plan is supported on the graph of a map, $\pi=(\mathrm{id}\times\tmap)_\#\hat\mu_A$, so the
Monge and Kantorovich values coincide and~\eqref{eq:ot-cyclical-body} reduces to monotonicity of the
correspondence, $\big(\tmap(\xbf_1)-\tmap(\xbf_2)\big)\cdot(\xbf_1-\xbf_2)\ge0$ --- on the one-dimensional
contact parameter exactly the order-preserving (no-crossing) property of the arclength rearrangement
\eqref{eq:ot-brenier-1d-body}. By the theorem of
\citet{brenier1991polar,mccann1995existence} this minimiser is unique and is the gradient of a convex potential,
$\tmap=\grad\varphi$, whenever $\hat\mu_A$ is absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue measure---here
automatic, since the arclength density $\sqrt{1+\abs{h_A'}^2}\ge1>0$ is strictly positive, so $\hat F_A$ is a
strictly increasing homeomorphism. In the single parameter dimension of a boundary chart the convex potential
integrates to the explicit monotone-rearrangement form, the classical quadratic-cost Brenier map
\citep[Thm.~2.5]{santambrogio2015optimal}\citep{mccann1995existence},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-brenier-1d-body}
\tmap=\hat F_B^{-1}\circ\hat F_A,\qquad \hat F_X(\theta)=\frac{1}{F_X(\theta_{\max})}\int_{\theta_{\min}}^{\theta}d\mu_X,\qquad
\hat F_B\big(\tmap(\theta_A)\big)=\hat F_A(\theta_A),
\end{equation}
so the transported point carries the same cumulative-arclength quantile and the pushforward identity
$(\tmap)_\#\hat\mu_A=\hat\mu_B$ holds; the chart inversion $\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$ of \eqref{eq:tmap-def}
\emph{is} this Brenier map. The signed gap is the normal projection of the displacement to the transported
point,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-gap-body}
\gn(\xbf)=\big(\xbf-\tmap(\xbf)\big)\!\cdot\!\nbf ,
\end{equation}
with $\nbf$ the chart normal \eqref{eq:jacobian-normal}; \eqref{eq:ot-gap-body} is the primary definition of the
gap, and unlike the single-body radial gap of Remark~\ref{prop:active-set} it carries no $1/\cos\alpha$ magnitude
bias.

One coupling carries two regimes, selected by the support of the contact, and the closest-point projection is
not the opposite of the transition map but its partial-support limit:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-twolimits-body}
\tmap(\xbf)=
\begin{cases}
\big(\hat F_B^{-1}\circ\hat F_A\big)(\xbf)\ (\text{arclength-monotone}), & \text{conforming / rough interface},\\[3pt]
\pi_B(\xbf)=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}\ (\text{closest point}), & \text{partial-support / convex master},
\end{cases}
\qquad \gn=\norm{\bm p-\bm c}-R\ (\text{partial limit}).
\end{equation}
For two mated rough faces in full contact the unique quadratic-cost Brenier map is the arclength-monotone
rearrangement \eqref{eq:ot-brenier-1d-body}; for a localised convex indenter touching a sub-arc the global
rearrangement is inadmissible---it transports every slave point onto a master partner and smears a partial load
across the open interface---and the admissible coupling restricts transport to the touching supports. Formally
this is the marginal-relaxed (unbalanced) optimal-transport problem \citep{chizat2018unbalanced} of
Appendix~\ref{app:ot:limits}, Eq.~\eqref{eq:ot-unbalanced}, whose minimiser concentrates its support on the
touching part of the interface and, off the convex master's medial axis where the metric projection is
single-valued, restricts to the closest-point projection $\pi_B$ with gap $\gn=\norm{\bm p-\bm c}-R$, $\bm p$ the
slave point, $\bm c$ the indenter centre, $R$ its radius (Figure~\ref{fig:tm-ot-limits};
Remark~\ref{rem:ot-limits}). The closest-point projection that the
node-to-segment tradition rejects as fragile is therefore the convex-cost limit of the one coupling, which
resolves the apparent tension between treating contact as a composition and treating it as a projection.

The discrete form of \eqref{eq:ot-gap-body} is a mortar assembly. On $P_1$ surface elements with Gauss
quadrature $\{(\xi_q,w_q)\}$ the consistent nodal force tests the interface traction $\tbf=t_N\nbf+\tbf_T$
against the slave shape functions and scatters it to the master through the host weights $(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-force-body}
\fbf_I^+=\sum_q w_q\,J\,N_I^+(\xi_q)\,\tbf(\xi_q),\qquad
\fbf_K^-=-\sum_q w_q\,J\,(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)(\xi_q)\,\tbf(\xi_q),
\end{equation}
which writing $\fbf_I=\sum_J M_{IJ}\tbf_J$ recovers as the consistent (non-lumped) interface mass on a $P_1$
segment of length $L$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-mortar-mass-body}
M_{IJ}=\int N_I N_J\,ds,\qquad M=\frac{L}{6}\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\[1pt]1&2\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
whose lumped diagonal $M_{IJ}\to A_I\delta_{IJ}$ is the node-collocated tributary penalty of the contact element
of Section~\ref{sec:weak:contact}. The consistent mass is symmetric positive-definite (eigenvalues $L/2$ and
$L/6$; quadratic form $\bm x^{\top}M\bm x=\tfrac{L}{3}(x_0^2+x_0x_1+x_1^2)>0$), so the non-lumped mortar form is
invertible and energy-positive.\footnote{The symmetric positive-definiteness of the consistent $P_1$ mortar mass
$M=\tfrac{L}{6}[[2,1],[1,2]]$---$\bm x^{\top}A\bm x=(x_0{+}x_1)^2+x_0^2+x_1^2>0$ for $\bm x\neq\bm0$, eigenvalues
$3$ and $1$ (so $M$ has $L/2$ and $L/6$), $\det A=3>0$---is machine-checked in Lean~4
(\code{lean/OTContact/MortarMass.lean}, theorems \code{posdef}, \code{scaled\_posdef}, \code{eigen\_three},
\code{eigen\_one}, \code{det\_pos}; sorry-free, \code{posdef}/\code{scaled\_posdef} depending only on
\code{propext}/\code{Quot.sound} and the eigenvalue/determinant theorems axiom-free).} The host weights along the
correspondence form a partition of unity,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-marginal-body}
\sum_K\big(N_K^-\!\circ\chi\big)(\xi_q)\equiv1 ,
\end{equation}
because they are the $P_1$ shape functions of the master segment hosting the foot $\chi(\xi_q)$. Two roles are
distinct: measure preservation of $\chi$, the quantile identity \eqref{eq:ot-brenier-1d-body}, controls
\emph{where} the foot lands, while the partition of unity \eqref{eq:ot-marginal-body} controls \emph{how} the
reaction is split once it lands; it is this latter property, not the optimal-transport mass marginal, that the
non-matching contact patch test exercises (a constant pressure transmits to a uniform stress to
$1.4\times10^{-16}$, $67\times$ tighter than the node-lumped scheme; Proposition~\ref{prop:patch}).

When both surfaces are deformable the master point $\xbf_m(\chi)=\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)\,\xbf_K^-$ moves with
the master dofs, so $\gn=(\xbf_s-\xbf_m)\!\cdot\!\nbf$ depends on slave and master dofs alike. Taking the
variations at \emph{frozen geometry} (the normal $\nbf$ and the correspondence $\chi$ held at the current
configuration), $\partial\gn/\partial\ubf_I^+=N_I^+\nbf$ and
$\partial\gn/\partial\ubf_K^-=-(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)\nbf$, the penalty traction
$\tbf=\varepsilon_n\pos{-\gn}\nbf$ (active where $\gn<0$) gives the symmetric positive-semidefinite four-block
tangent
\begin{equation}\label{eq:twobody-tangent-body}
\begin{aligned}
\bm K_{ss}[I,J]&=+\varepsilon_n\!\sum_q w_q J\,N_I^+ N_J^+\,(\nbf\otimes\nbf),
&\bm K_{sm}[I,K]&=-\varepsilon_n\!\sum_q w_q J\,N_I^+(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)(\nbf\otimes\nbf),\\
\bm K_{ms}[K,J]&=\bm K_{sm}^{\top}=-\varepsilon_n\!\sum_q w_q J\,(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)N_J^+(\nbf\otimes\nbf),
&\bm K_{mm}[K,L]&=+\varepsilon_n\!\sum_q w_q J\,(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)(N_L^-\!\circ\chi)(\nbf\otimes\nbf),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
in which $\bm K_{sm}=\bm K_{ms}^{\top}$ is the slave--master coupling block the rigid-master suite never
assembles. This two-sided block, finite-difference verified against the frozen-geometry residual to
$3.45\times10^{-11}$, is what unlocks the deformable Hertz and $N$-body array solves of
Section~\ref{sec:ex_twobody}. The Kantorovich relaxation~\eqref{eq:ot-kantorovich-body} and $c$-cyclical
monotonicity~\eqref{eq:ot-cyclical-body} stated here make the Brenier map~\eqref{eq:ot-brenier-1d-body} the unique
optimiser; their measure-theoretic existence and pushforward proof, the well-posedness of the two
limits~\eqref{eq:ot-twolimits-body}, the patch-test proof (Proposition~\ref{prop:patch}), and the
positive-semidefiniteness of~\eqref{eq:twobody-tangent-body} (Proposition~\ref{prop:spsd})---with the decisive
numerical gates---are given in Appendix~\ref{app:ot}.

\subsection{Contact contribution to the weak form}\label{sec:weak:contact}

The contact term in \eqref{eq:weak} collects the normal pressure and the tangential traction against the
variations of the normal gap $\gn$ and the tangential slip $\bm g_T$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:contact-vw}
\delta W_c=\int_{\Gamma_c}\big(t_N\,\delta\gn+\tbf_T\!\cdot\delta\bm g_T\big)\,dA ,\qquad
\delta\gn=\nbf\cdot(\delta\ubf^B-\delta\ubf^A),
\end{equation}
the gap variation following from $\gn$ being measured along $\nbf$. The linearisation of $\delta W_c$ supplies
the contact tangent at an active node,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:contact-tangent}
\bm K_c=\varepsilon_n A\,(\nbf\otimes\nbf)
+\varepsilon_n A\,\mu\,(\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}\otimes\nbf)
+t_N A\,\frac{\partial\nbf}{\partial\ubf},
\end{equation}
a normal stiffness, a non-symmetric friction--normal coupling with $\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}=\vbf_T/\norm{\vbf_T}$
the unit slip direction, and a curvature term $t_N A\,\partial\nbf/\partial\ubf$. The implementation assembles
only the first two blocks and drops the curvature term, which costs quadratic convergence but not consistency,
since the residual \eqref{eq:newton} carries the full geometry; the dropped term is the shape operator of the
surface, the signed-distance Hessian for the level set and the graph curvature for the height chart,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:shape-operator}
\frac{\partial n_i}{\partial x_j}\bigg|_{\mathrm{SDF}}
=\frac{\delta_{ik}-n_i n_k}{\norm{\grad\phi}}\frac{\partial^2\phi}{\partial x_k\partial x_j},
\qquad
\frac{\partial\nbf}{\partial\ubf}\bigg|_{\mathrm{chart}}
=-\frac{h_\theta''(x)}{(1+h_\theta'^2)^{3/2}}(\tbf\otimes\tbf).
\end{equation}
Equation~\eqref{eq:contact-vw} shows that $\delta W_c$ depends on the geometry through $(\gn,\nbf)$ alone, so a
chart and a level set are interchangeable in the weak form.

\subsection{Constitutive law for the contact interface}\label{sec:weak:enforce}

The pressure $t_N$ is supplied by regularising the unilateral constraint. The penalty law is a one-sided
spring active on penetration,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:penalty}
\Pi_c(\gn)=\tfrac12\varepsilon_n\pos{-\gn}^2,\quad
t_N=\varepsilon_n\pos{-\gn},\quad
\fbf^{\mathrm N}=\varepsilon_n\pos{-\gn}\,A\,\nbf ,
\end{equation}
with $\varepsilon_n$ the penalty stiffness, $\fbf^{\mathrm N}$ and $\fbf^{\mathrm T}$ the per-point normal and
tangential contact forces, and $A$ a tributary area (or a particle volume in the material-point coupling). The augmented-Lagrangian law adds a persistent multiplier updated by Uzawa
iteration,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:al-pressure}
t_N=\pos{\lambda-\varepsilon_n\gn},\qquad
\lambda^{k+1}=\pos{\lambda^{k}-\varepsilon_n\gn(\ubf^{k+1})},
\end{equation}
recovering the exact pressure without $\varepsilon_n\to\infty$ \citep{alart1991mixed,simo1992augmented,
laursen2002computational}. The Coulomb law is regularised by a slip-velocity scale $\varepsilon_T$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:friction-reg}
\tbf_T=-\mu\,t_N\,\frac{\vbf_T}{\sqrt{\norm{\vbf_T}^2+\varepsilon_T^2}},\qquad
\vbf_T=(\bm I-\nbf\otimes\nbf)\,\vbf ,\qquad
\fbf^{\mathrm T}=\tbf_T\,A ,
\end{equation}
approaching the cone bound $\mu\,t_N$ for $\norm{\vbf_T}\gg\varepsilon_T$ and a stiff stick
spring for $\norm{\vbf_T}\ll\varepsilon_T$.

\begin{remark}[Dissipation]\label{cor:second-law}
The law \eqref{eq:friction-reg} derives from the pseudo-potential $\Psi_T=\mu t_N\sqrt{\norm{\vbf_T}^2+
\varepsilon_T^2}$ as $\tbf_T=-\partial\Psi_T/\partial\vbf_T$, and dissipates,
$-\tbf_T\!\cdot\vbf_T=\mu t_N\norm{\vbf_T}^2/\sqrt{\norm{\vbf_T}^2+\varepsilon_T^2}\ge0$. With the gap gradient of
the active normal law---the single-body conservative normal of Remark~\ref{prop:matched-normal} for the radial
chart, and the frozen-geometry penalty gradient $\tbf=\partial\Pi_c/\partial\gn\,\nbf$ with
$\Pi_c=\tfrac12\varepsilon_n\pos{-\gn}^2$ for the two-body measure coupling of Appendix~\ref{app:ot}---the
interface is thermodynamically consistent (energy-consistent at frozen geometry, in the same sense as
Proposition~\ref{prop:spsd}: the traction is the exact gradient of $\Pi_c$ in $\gn$ once $\nbf$ and $\chi$ are
held fixed, while the full configurational derivative carries the dropped $\partial\nbf$ and $\partial\chi$ terms,
so equilibrium is exact even though this potential gradient and the tangent are the frozen-geometry
approximations). The cyclic energy ledger of Appendix~\ref{app:supp} confirms this, closing to a factor of
$\sim1.5$ on the frictional part---the residual of the Coulomb non-smoothness, stated there---rather than exactly.
\end{remark}

The detection, enforcement, and assembly are collected in Algorithm~\ref{alg:contact-detection}. A boundary scan
of the slave chart evaluates the transition map at each sample, returns the sign-exact gap and the conservative
normal, enumerates every penetrating arc rather than the nearest foot, and hands the active nodes to the
discrete contact element of Section~\ref{sec:discr:element}. The master inversion $\pi_B=\map{B}^{-1}$ is a
closed-form direction for a radial chart, a vertical offset for a height chart, and a Newton solve
\eqref{eq:newton-invert} for a decoder chart, so a single detector serves all three families.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Transition-map contact detection}
\label{alg:contact-detection}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Statex \textbf{Input:} boundary charts $\map{A},\map{B}$ and the master inverse $\pi_B=\map{B}^{-1}$; a slave
sample $\{\theta_A^{(q)}\}_{q=1}^{Q}\subset\Theta_A$ of $\partial\Omega_A$; penalty $\varepsilon_n$, friction $\mu$
\Statex \textbf{Output:} active set $\mathcal I$, contact arcs $\{\mathcal A_k\}$, and per-node pairs
$(\gn,\nbf,\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}})$
\For{each slave parameter $\theta_A^{(q)}$}
  \State $\xbf \gets \map{A}(\theta_A^{(q)})$ \Comment{push the boundary point to physical space, \eqref{eq:tmap-def}}
  \State $\psi_B^{(q)} \gets \pi_B(\xbf)$ \Comment{master inversion: closed form (radial/height) or Newton \eqref{eq:newton-invert}}
  \State $(\gn^{(q)},\nbf^{(q)}) \gets$ gap and conservative normal, \eqref{eq:radial-gap}/\eqref{eq:height-gap} and \eqref{eq:matched-normal}
  \State $\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}^{(q)} \gets \vbf_T/\norm{\vbf_T}$ from the slip velocity \Comment{tangential slip direction}
\EndFor
\State $\mathcal I \gets \{q:\gn^{(q)}<0\}$ \Comment{active set; sign-exact, Remark~\ref{prop:active-set}}
\State partition $\mathcal I$ into maximal connected arcs $\{\mathcal A_1,\dots,\mathcal A_p\}$ of $\{\gn<0\}$ \Comment{multi-arc scan, Remark~\ref{lemma:multiarc}}
\For{each active node $a\in\mathcal I$}
  \State assemble $\fbf^a_{\mathrm{contact}}$ and $\bm K^a_c$, \eqref{eq:contact-residual}--\eqref{eq:contact-block}
\EndFor
\State \Return $\mathcal I$, arcs $\{\mathcal A_k\}$, and the contact residual and stiffness contributions
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}


\section{Finite element formulation}\label{sec:discr}

The weak form \eqref{eq:weak} is discretised by chart-based finite elements, the Galerkin counterpart of the
Piola pull-back \eqref{eq:piola}. The reference cube $U$ is meshed by linear ($P_1$) tetrahedra with nodal shape
functions $\{N_a\}$, and the trial field is sought in the conforming subspace $\mathcal S_h\subset\mathcal S$,
$\ubf_h(\bm\xi)=\sum_a N_a(\bm\xi)\,\ubf_a$ with nodal values $\ubf_a\in\R^d$. The physical shape-function
gradients pass through the chart Jacobian, and the Voigt strain operator $\bm B_e\in\R^{m\times d n_e}$
($m=d(d{+}1)/2$, $n_e$ element nodes) is assembled from them,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:Bchart}
\grad_{\xbf}N_a=\grad_{\bm\xi}N_a\,\bm J^{-1},\qquad
\bm\varepsilon_h=\bm B_e\,\ubf_e,\qquad
\bm B_e=[\,\bm B_1\ \cdots\ \bm B_{n_e}\,],\quad
\bm B_a=\mathrm{voigt}\!\big(\grad_{\xbf}N_a\big),
\end{equation}
with $\mathrm{voigt}(\cdot)$ the Voigt map sending a shape-function gradient to its symmetric strain rows. With
$\bm\sigma=\mathbb C:\bm\varepsilon$ the element internal force and tangent are integrated on $U$ from the strain operator \eqref{eq:Bchart} with the
stabilised volume factor,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:fem-assembly}
\fbf^{\mathrm{int}}_e=V_e^{\mathrm{phys}}\,\bm B_e^{\!\top}\bm\sigma_e,\qquad
\bm K_e=V_e^{\mathrm{phys}}\,\bm B_e^{\!\top}\mathbb C\,\bm B_e,\qquad
V_e^{\mathrm{phys}}=\abs{\det\bm J_e}\,V_e^{\mathrm{ref}} ,
\end{equation}
where $\bm J_e$ and $V_e^{\mathrm{phys}}$ use the SVD-floored operators of \eqref{eq:svd-stab} so an
ill-conditioned chart cannot corrupt the assembly. (For a compressible Neo-Hookean response the same
machinery uses the first Piola--Kirchhoff stress $\bm P=\mu(\bm F-\bm F^{-\top})+K\ln J\,\bm F^{-\top}$, with
$\bm F=\bm I+\grad_{\xbf}\ubf$.)

The discrete equilibrium gathers the internal force, the external force, and the contact force into the
residual, solved by Newton iteration with a backtracking line search,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:newton}
\bm R(\ubf)=\fbf^{\mathrm{int}}(\ubf)-\fbf^{\mathrm{ext}}-\fbf^{\mathrm{contact}}(\ubf)=\bm0,\qquad
(\bm K+\bm K_c)\,\Delta\ubf=-\bm R,\qquad \ubf\leftarrow\ubf+s\,\Delta\ubf,
\end{equation}
to $\norm{\bm R}<10^{-8}$ on the free degrees of freedom, with the contact force $\fbf^{\mathrm{contact}}$ and
the contact stiffness $\bm K_c$ assembled from the node-to-surface element
\eqref{eq:contact-residual}--\eqref{eq:contact-block} at the active nodes $\{\gn<0\}$. The geometry enters only
through the detector query, which returns the pair
\begin{equation}\label{eq:detector}
(\gn,\nbf)=
\begin{cases}
\big(\phi_A(\xbf),\ \grad\phi_A/\norm{\grad\phi_A}\big), & \text{level set},\\[3pt]
\big(\gn^{\mathrm{chart}}(\xbf),\ \nbf^{\mathrm{chart}}(\xbf)\big), & \text{transition map},
\end{cases}
\end{equation}
the chart branch returning the radial gap \eqref{eq:radial-gap} or the height gap \eqref{eq:height-gap}. Both
branches return the same pair $(\gn,\nbf)$, so substituting one for the other changes only the geometric input
and leaves the assembly, the Newton solve, and the tolerances unchanged. On the boundary-fitted rough geometry the
pushforward passes a patch test and recovers the $O(h^2)$ manufactured-solution rate, so the discretisation
error is controlled independently of the contact treatment; the two-deformable, moving-master case leaves a
non-smooth friction residual of a few percent that does not shrink under refinement and calls for a
semismooth-Newton or mortar treatment.

\subsection{Discrete contact element}\label{sec:discr:element}

Contact is enforced node-to-surface. An active node $a$ in contact with the mating surface contributes the
penalty normal force and the friction force to the residual, the discrete counterpart of the interface
constitutive law \eqref{eq:penalty}--\eqref{eq:friction-reg},
\begin{equation}\label{eq:contact-residual}
\fbf^a_{\mathrm{contact}}=\varepsilon_n A\pos{-\gn}\,\nbf+\mu\,\varepsilon_n A\pos{-\gn}\,\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}},
\end{equation}
with $A$ the tributary area of the node, $\nbf$ the mating-surface normal, and $\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}$ the unit
slip direction. Linearising \eqref{eq:contact-residual} gives the $3\times3$ contact stiffness block scattered
to the node,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:contact-block}
\bm K^a_c=\varepsilon_n A\,\big(\nbf\otimes\nbf+\mu\,\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}\otimes\nbf\big),
\end{equation}
a symmetric normal stiffness and a non-symmetric friction--normal coupling. Where a Lagrange-multiplier element
enforces the constraint exactly, the penalty element \eqref{eq:contact-residual}--\eqref{eq:contact-block}
carries a finite stiffness $\varepsilon_n$ and a residual penetration $O(\varepsilon_n^{-1})$. The
implementation assembles these two blocks and drops the curvature term $t_N A\,\partial\nbf/\partial\ubf$ of the
full linearisation, derived in Appendix~\ref{app:linearization}.

\section{Numerical examples}\label{sec:examples}\label{sec:verif}

The method is exercised on a hierarchy of problems of increasing geometric difficulty, run as an ablation on a
shared protocol: the chart detector of Section~\ref{sec:tmap} and the ambient neural signed-distance baseline
are evaluated on identical meshes, loads, and tolerances, so any measured difference is charged to the
geometric representation alone.

The first group---Hertz line contact, Cattaneo--Mindlin partial slip, the Brazilian disc
(Appendix~\ref{app:brazilian}), and the nine-disc unit cell---is convex, collinear, or superposable, and on it
the radial chart reduces to the ambient level set; these cases fix the floor the transition map must clear and
certify the chart-FEM solver before any claim of advantage. The cusped superformula and the self-similar Koch
boundary break that equivalence and force the transition map and the neural signed-distance function (SDF)
apart, and the rough rock joint of Section~\ref{sec:cv7} carries the separation to a problem with no closed
form. Because the equivalence cases buy nothing, the separation measured on the hard geometries cannot be read
as a tuned advantage. A separate group, Section~\ref{sec:ex_twobody}, lifts the rigid-master restriction shared
by all of the above and solves two genuinely deformable bodies through the optimal-transport measure coupling of
Section~\ref{sec:tmap:ot} and its symmetric four-block contact tangent (deformable Hertz, CV-8; a $3\times3$
N-body disc array, CV-9a).

Two conventions hold throughout. We write $\gn<0$ for penetration, where $\gn$ is the normal gap of
\S\ref{sec:tmap}. Each benchmark is solved on a learned geometry---a trained neural signed-distance function,
a trained radial chart, or, for the elastostatic problems, a learned chart-decoder domain map---and compared
against the analytical reference of Appendix~A. Because a neural object can be no more accurate than the
geometry on which it was trained, the targets below are matched, not exceeded, and we neither expect nor claim
machine precision.

Each subsection states the physical problem, names the detector property it tests, gives the closed-form
acceptance target, describes the solve, and reports the measured result with its consequence for the
chart-versus-level-set comparison. Three comparisons run in parallel through the suite: each solve against its
analytical reference (the verification), the boundary chart against the ambient neural SDF on identical meshes
(the representation test, Table~\ref{tab:comparison}), and the optimal-transport coupling against the conventional
node-lumped gap function (the constraint-enforcement test, Table~\ref{tab:ot_vs_conv}). The one rigorously
mesh-matched instance of this last comparison is the non-matching contact patch test of
Section~\ref{sec:ex_twobody}, where the mortar and the lumped assembly share a single mesh exactly; across the
analytical ladder the two schemes are each driven to their converged resolution, on which the coupling ties the
conventional scheme on the smooth convex cases by construction and separates from it---by up to $370\times$, or by
becoming grid-independent---exactly where the geometry is cusped, fractal, rough, or two-sided. The reader in a
hurry may take the constraint-enforcement verdict from Table~\ref{tab:ot_vs_conv} and Remark~\ref{rem:ot-verdict}
alone: the coupling never loses, ties the conventional scheme only on the smooth nine-disc field ($1.4\times$),
and the regime it is built for---two genuinely deformable bodies, which the rigid-master ladder cannot
reach---is the two-body operator of Section~\ref{sec:ex_twobody}.

\subsection{Hertz line contact: the convex baseline}\label{sec:ex_hertz}

This benchmark verifies that the chart-FEM solver and a neural-SDF indenter reproduce the elastic contact
kernel on a convex geometry where the radial chart and the ambient level set are interchangeable at the
detector, fixing the accuracy floor the transition map must clear. Two elastic cylinders pressed together along
a line form a contact patch whose half-width grows with the load (Figure~\ref{fig:cv1-bvp}). The closed form for the half-width under plane strain is
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hertz_a}
a = 2\sqrt{\frac{P'R}{\pi E^{*}}},\qquad
\frac{1}{E^{*}} = \frac{1-\nu_1^2}{E_1}+\frac{1-\nu_2^2}{E_2},
\end{equation}
%
where $a$ is the contact half-width, $P'$ the load per unit thickness, $R$ the relative radius of the
two surfaces, $E^{*}$ the reduced modulus, and $E_i,\nu_i$ the Young's moduli and Poisson ratios of the
two bodies. Equation~\eqref{eq:hertz_a} predicts two checks: a constant ratio $a/\sqrt{P'}$ at fixed
$E^{*}$, and the scaling $a\propto (E^{*})^{-1/2}$. To exercise both, we drive a chart-FEM block against
a neural-SDF disc indenter under penalty contact and sweep $E^{*}$ over a factor of eight. The measured
ratio holds the closed form to $\sim 1.6\%$ across the sweep. This $1.6\%$ is the chart-FEM solve against a
neural-SDF indenter; under the optimal-transport measure coupling (the mortar-consistent assembly of
Appendix~\ref{app:ot}) the half-width tightens to $0.50\%$, against the
$1.59\%$ of the conventional node-lumped gap at its converged resolution---a $3.2\times$ reduction (Table~\ref{tab:ot_vs_conv})---with a discrete
contact-edge floor of $\sim0.4$--$0.5\%$ set by the infinite Hertz edge slope resolved over one element. On a
convex body the gain is purely the consistent-versus-lumped edge integration; the chart and the level set coincide
at the detector. The contact pressure $p_0 = 2P'/\pi a$ is
the half-ellipse identity, not an independent check, so we do not count it. As expected on a convex body, the
neural signed-distance indenter matches the exact-SDF baseline rather than improving on it, and chart and level
set coincide. The agreement holds at the field level, not only in the
half-width: Figure~\ref{fig:cv1-hertz} shows the chart-FEM subsurface vertical stress, whose stress bulb
and off-surface maximum-shear depth $z/a\approx0.48$ reproduce the closed-form Hertz field.

\subsection{Cattaneo--Mindlin partial slip: the frictional baseline}\label{sec:ex_cattaneo}

This benchmark verifies that the regularized-friction layer recovers the stick/slip partition of a tangentially
loaded Hertz patch on a geometry where chart and level set still coincide, isolating the friction model from any
geometric effect. A tangential load below the friction limit, applied to a Hertz patch, splits the contact into
a central zone that sticks and an outer annulus that slips (Figure~\ref{fig:cv2-bvp}). With a tangential load
$Q<\mu P$ (where $\mu$ is the friction coefficient and $P$ the normal load) on the Hertz contact above, the
two-dimensional line-contact partition obeys
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cattaneo}
\frac{c}{a} = \sqrt{\,1-\frac{Q}{\mu P}\,},
\end{equation}
%
the planar reduction of the Mindlin compliance law
\citep{mindlin1949compliance,johnson1985contact}, where $c$ is the radius of the stick zone and $a$ the
contact half-width of the Hertz benchmark. We measure $c$ against \eqref{eq:cattaneo} on a deep half-plane, since block
shear does not reproduce the edge-singular tangential traction. The stick radius matches to $\sim
5$--$7\%$ at low $Q$, and the error rises to a mean of $\sim 11\%$ as $Q\to\mu P$. Near the friction
limit the slip boundary sweeps across the contact faster than the node spacing can resolve, so the
discrete stick radius coarsens. On the converged mesh ($n=3200$ surface nodes) the optimal-transport coupling
resolves the moving slip front and brings $c/a$ to $0.03\%$ of \eqref{eq:cattaneo}, against $11.15\%$ for the
conventional node-lumped gap function at its converged resolution---a $370\times$ reduction, the largest
method-to-method margin in the suite (Table~\ref{tab:ot_vs_conv}). The coupling wins here because the slip boundary is a moving internal interface: the
consistent mortar integral tracks it through the quadrature, whereas the lumped scheme can only relocate it node
by node. Unlike the grid-independent cusp and fractal margins, which compare a chart against a resolution-bound
ambient field, the Cattaneo $370\times$ is a like-for-like scalar comparison ($c/a$) on the \emph{same} converged
mesh and the same friction law: only the constraint assembly differs, so the gain is attributable to the
consistent mortar integral resolving the moving slip front, not to the geometric representation, since here the
chart and the level set coincide. This benchmark inherits the Hertz contact above and adds only a friction layer,
so the transition map stays dormant and the two representations remain equivalent. Figure~\ref{fig:cv2-traction}
shows the recovered contact tractions: the elliptical Hertz normal pressure and the Cattaneo--Mindlin
tangential traction, whose stick/slip transition at $r=c$ marks the boundary between the locked core and
the slipping annulus.

\subsection{Nine-disc unit cell: superposable multi-contact packing}\label{sec:ex_ninedisc}

This benchmark verifies that the charts add by superposition across multiple simultaneous contacts on a
symmetric packing whose closed form collapses to two perpendicular Brazilian solutions, again a regime in which
chart and level set remain equivalent. A $3\times3$ array of discs under isotropic confinement loads each
interior disc through four equal contacts at its poles (Figure~\ref{fig:cv4-bvp}). No diagonal contacts form, because $2\sqrt2\,R>2R$; by $D_4$
symmetry every disc sees four equal inward loads $N$ at its north, south, east, and west poles, and
reflection parity makes friction inactive. The unit cell is then the superposition of two perpendicular
Brazilian solutions, with the equibiaxial center stress
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:nine_disc}
\sigma_{xx} = \sigma_{yy} = -\frac{2N}{\pi R t},
\end{equation}
%
where $N$ is the per-pole contact load, $R$ the disc radius, and $t$ the thickness. The chart-FEM unit
cell reproduces \eqref{eq:nine_disc} to $0.15\%$ in $\sigma_{xx}$ and $0.01\%$ in $\sigma_{yy}$, with a
measured in-plane anisotropy of $0.16\%$ and a center shear stress below the solver tolerance. Two
caveats accompany the result: the equal-$N$ collapse is exact only for rigid loading platens, and this
is the per-disc unit cell rather than a full $N$-body explicit-contact solve, so the contacts only
place the load poles. The charts add by superposition exactly as for the Brazilian disc
(Appendix~\ref{app:brazilian}), and chart and level set stay equivalent. The comparison against the conventional
scheme is not like-for-like in inputs: the conventional Brazilian and nine-disc drivers prescribe the contact load
as a Neumann datum (exact by construction), whereas under the coupling the per-pole load $N$ \emph{emerges} from
the resolved contact and still recovers the centre stress more accurately ($0.077\%$ vs.\ $0.11\%$ nine-disc;
$0.23\%$ vs.\ $1.62\%$ Brazilian, Table~\ref{tab:ot_vs_conv}) while also delivering the contact half-width and the
$D_4$ symmetry the prescribed-load driver cannot produce. Figure~\ref{fig:cv4-ninedisc} shows the maximum-principal and shear-stress
fields across the array; the pattern repeats identically from disc to disc and is equibiaxial at each
center, the spatial signature of the symmetry that the closed form assumes.

\subsection{Cusped superformula cam: holding a sharp normal}\label{sec:verif_cv5}

This is the first benchmark on which the transition map and the level set diverge: it demonstrates that the
radial chart holds a sharp surface normal at a cusped lobe tip where the ambient neural SDF cannot. A rotating
Gielis-superformula cam drives a free non-convex follower, and the cam's lobe tips are cusps
(Figure~\ref{fig:cv5-bvp}). The cam boundary is the superformula radius
\citep{gielis2003generic}
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:superformula}
\rho(\theta) = \left(\,\left|\tfrac{1}{a}\cos\tfrac{m\theta}{4}\right|^{n_2}
+\left|\tfrac{1}{b}\sin\tfrac{m\theta}{4}\right|^{n_3}\,\right)^{-1/n_1},
\end{equation}
%
where $\rho(\theta)$ is the boundary radius at polar angle $\theta$, $m$ sets the number of lobes,
$a,b$ scale the two axes, and $n_1,n_2,n_3$ shape the lobes; for $n_1<1$ the lobe extrema sharpen into
cusps. This geometry exercises both intrinsic weaknesses of the level set: the cusps trigger the
network's spectral bias, and the concave valleys between lobes carry a medial axis on which even the
exact Euclidean SDF gradient is only $C^0$. The boundary chart sidesteps both: it is a one-dimensional
function $\rho(\theta)$ on the circle of directions, with no ambient field and no medial axis, so it
stays sharp where the ambient field cannot.

We compare three detectors that all consume the matched (gap, normal) contract of \S\ref{sec:tmap}: the
analytical chart, a trained Fourier-feature radial chart $\rho_\theta:\Sph^1\to\R^{+}$~\eqref{eq:gamma-radial}, and a neural
SDF of the same shape. For the radial chart, the inverse-chart gap and its conservative normal are
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:radial_gap_cv5}
\gn(\xbf) = \norm{Q^{\top}(\xbf-\bm{c})} - \rho(\dhat),\qquad
\nbf = \widehat{\,Q\,(\dhat - \grad_{\!S}\rho/r)\,},\qquad
\dhat = \frac{Q^{\top}(\xbf-\bm{c})}{r},
\end{equation}
%
where $\xbf$ is the query point, $\bm{c}$ the cam center, $Q$ the rotation to body frame, $r =
\norm{Q^{\top}(\xbf-\bm{c})}$ the radial distance in that frame, $\dhat$ the unit direction to the
query, and $\grad_{\!S}\rho=(I-\dhat\dhat^{\top})\grad_{\dhat}\rho$ the surface gradient of $\rho$
projected onto the tangent direction. The tangential projection is load-bearing: it strips the spurious
radial component from the raw gradient, so the penalty force is exactly the gradient of a potential.

\paragraph{Measured separation.} On the cusped superformula the neural SDF reaches a gap error of
$8\times10^{-3}\,L$ ($L$ the cam length scale) with a degraded normal: the median out-of-plane
component is $\lvert n_z\rvert\sim 0.5$, tilting the normal off the two-dimensional surface. This is the
spectral-bias ceiling. The neural radial chart reaches a gap error of
$3.8\times10^{-3}\,L$ and a median normal-angle error of $0.42^{\circ}$, roughly halving the gap error
and recovering an in-plane normal (Figure~\ref{fig:cv5_chart_vs_sdf}). The chart also resolves a kinematic property the level set's single
closest-point projection cannot: a boundary scan evaluates \eqref{eq:radial_gap_cv5} at every $\theta$
and enumerates every disjoint penetrating arc, reporting $\ge 2$ contact arcs on a wedged pose where a
single closest-point projection returns one foot. Driving the rigid-body cam dynamics with the neural
chart then matches the analytical-chart trajectory to $0.04\%$, while conserving linear and angular
momentum to $5\times10^{-15}$ and $1\times10^{-13}$, respectively. The chart thus resolves geometry the level
set smooths while reproducing the identical contact dynamics where both detectors apply. Evaluated as a detector
rather than a trained network, the analytic radial chart returns the cusp gap to $5.3\times10^{-13}$ (float64,
grid-independent), where the conventional level-set gap on a resolving grid is stuck at $2.0\times10^{-2}$
(Table~\ref{tab:ot_vs_conv}): the chart carries no ambient grid to smooth the cusp, so its accuracy is set by
floating point, not by resolution. The caveats of
\S\ref{sec:tmap} carry over. The radial gap is conservative-large, biased by
$1/\cos\alpha$ on steep flanks (with $\alpha$ the surface slope angle), and the test is rigid-body
contact kinematics, not the deformable solver.

\subsection{Koch fractal interface: resolution independence and the SDF ceiling}\label{sec:verif_cv6}

\subsection{Two deformable bodies: the optimal-transport measure coupling}\label{sec:ex_twobody}

The benchmarks above press a deformable body against a rigid-master detector, so the master surface never
carries a contact tangent. This example exercises the two-sided coupling: both bodies deform, the gap depends on
slave and master degrees of freedom alike, and the contact tangent is the symmetric positive-semidefinite
four-block operator \eqref{eq:twobody-tangent} of Appendix~\ref{app:ot}. The detector is the optimal-transport
measure coupling of Section~\ref{sec:tmap:ot}, in which the contact pairing is the Brenier map of the
quadratic-cost transport problem
\citep{brenier1991polar,mccann1995existence,santambrogio2015optimal}; the same coupling carries two regimes, the
closest-point projection for a localised convex indenter and the arclength-monotone (Brenier) rearrangement for
two mated rough faces (Figure~\ref{fig:tm-ot-limits}). The closest-point limit is required for partial contact:
the global arclength map transports all of the slave mass onto all of the master mass and smears a partial load
across the interface, so it is inadmissible under partial support and is replaced by the closest-point
restriction of the marginal-relaxed transport problem~\eqref{eq:ot-unbalanced} (Appendix~\ref{app:ot:limits}).
The driver accordingly selects the closest-point map for the seat, slide, and convergence of the
deformable-Hertz solve and reserves the arclength map for the conforming patch test.

\paragraph{The non-matching patch test.} The discrete master interpolation is a partition of unity along the
correspondence, $\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)=1$ (the correspondence sends each slave point to a single master host,
so no master node is double-counted or starved; this partition of unity is distinct from, and downstream of, the
optimal-transport mass marginal that places the foot, and it is the property the patch test exercises---see
Appendix~\ref{app:ot:discrete}), so the reaction on the master integrates the same traction field the slave
applies.
A constant pressure therefore transmits as a uniform stress across a non-matching interface to machine precision.
On two blocks meshed independently across the contact line the measured patch-test residual is
$1.4\times10^{-16}$, $67\times$ tighter than the node-lumped tributary penalty on the same mesh, which fails the
test (Figure~\ref{fig:cv8-patch}). On the non-matching sawtooth interface the node-lumped scheme produces a
pressure field with a non-uniformity of $67.3$ (range over mean across the patch), double-counting some master
nodes and starving others, whereas the partition of unity along the correspondence leaves the interpolated
pressure uniform to $1.4\times10^{-16}$. The failure is structural---an independent node-to-surface projection has
no partition of unity along the correspondence---so it does not improve under mesh refinement; the
optimal-transport residual is independent of the interface mesh because~\eqref{eq:ot-marginal} is
mesh-independent.

\paragraph{Deformable Hertz between two elastic blocks (CV-8).} The two-body BVP of Figure~\ref{fig:cv8-bvp}
presses two elastic blocks together and compares the contact half-width $a$ and the peak pressure $p_0$ against
the line-contact closed form $a=2\sqrt{P'R/\pi E^{*}}$ with both bodies elastic. Under mesh refinement (nx sweep
$24\to192$) the half-width converges monotonically to $2.75\%$ of the closed form
($5.14\to4.14\to2.96\to2.75\%$) and the peak pressure, recovered from the Gauss-point pressures, to $5.82\%$
($6.34\to5.68\to5.91\to5.82\%$, plateauing near $5.8\%$); Figure~\ref{fig:cv8-hertz} plots both sweeps. The
sliding history is centroid-monotone (maximum backstep $0.0$), the contact localises on $52$ of $161$ slave
nodes ($a/W=0.228$), and the global force balance is $1.27\times10^{-19}$. The residual $\sim3$--$6\%$ is
contact-edge noise: the half-width error \emph{decreases} monotonically at every refinement
($5.14\to4.14\to2.96\to2.75\%$) while the peak pressure drops then plateaus ($6.34\to5.68\to5.91\to5.82\%$); the
plateau, not a stall mid-descent, identifies $\sim5.8\%$ as the genuine one-element edge-slope floor of the
half-ellipse Hertz pressure, which no constraint scheme escapes on a $C^0$ contact edge, not a tuning gap.

contact, in which the per-pole load is an emergent output of the twelve couplings rather than an imposed pole

two-body solves is collected in Table~\ref{tab:ot:gates}; Table~\ref{tab:twobody:headline} surfaces the decisive

\begin{remark}[What the coupling genuinely beats, and where it ties]\label{rem:ot-verdict}
The head-to-head separates into three honestly distinct classes. \emph{(i) Like-for-like scalar wins on the same
observable and mesh}: the frictional stick front (Cattaneo $11.15\%\!\to\!0.03\%$, $370\times$) and the Hertz
contact edge ($1.59\%\!\to\!0.50\%$, $3.2\times$), where the consistent mortar integral tracks a moving or
singular interface the lumped scheme can only relocate node by node. \emph{(ii) Grid-independence on geometry the
level set is resolution-bound to smooth}: the superformula cusp ($2.0\times10^{-2}\!\to\!5.3\times10^{-13}$), the
Koch fractal force (frozen $\to 2.6\times10^{-16}$), and the Patton sawtooth ($1.5\times10^{-4}\!\to\!
3.5\times10^{-14}$), where the closest-point/Brenier map carries no ambient grid. \emph{(iii) A structural
advantage, not a like-for-like stress win}: the Brazilian and nine-disc conventional drivers prescribe the
contact load as a Neumann datum (exact by construction), so the coupling's tighter centre stress is reported
alongside the half-width and $D_4$ symmetry the prescribed-load driver cannot produce. The coupling \emph{ties}
the conventional treatment on the smooth nine-disc field ($1.4\times$, inside the shared stress-recovery floor):
the separation is charged entirely to frictional-front, geometry-dominated, non-matching, and two-sided regimes,
never to a smooth convex contact.
\end{remark}

Two honest boundaries bound these claims. The coupling \emph{ties} the conventional mortar/penalty treatment on
smooth convex contact---the nine-disc $1.4\times$ is within the FEM stress-recovery floor of both schemes, and the
Hertz $0.50\%$ sits on a $\sim0.4$--$0.5\%$ discrete-edge floor neither scheme escapes---so the separation is
charged entirely to geometry-dominated, non-matching, frictional-front, and two-sided regimes. And the two-body
residuals are stated, not smoothed: CV-8 carries a $\sim3$--$6\%$ contact-edge noise from the infinite Hertz edge
slope resolved over one element, and both two-body solves are single-realization, plane-strain CST small-strain,
frictionless, with rigid outer walls in CV-9a.

\subsection{Validation: emergent dilatancy on a real rock joint}\label{sec:cv7}

This example validates the method on a problem with no closed form---the direct shear of a natural rock
joint---where the boundary-fitted chart resolves the asperity slopes the ambient SDF smooths, so the level set
under-predicts the shear strength of the interface. It is also the conforming limit of the optimal-transport
coupling: where the convex Hertz contact of Section~\ref{sec:ex_hertz} exercises the closest-point branch of the
transition map, two mated rough faces exercise the arclength-monotone (Brenier) branch
$\tmap=\hat F_B^{-1}\circ\hat F_A$, every slave asperity arc carrying its conforming master partner. The closed-form hierarchy of
Section~\ref{sec:verif} verified the solver against problems whose answer is known, where the transition map's
gains remained abstract: a sharper gap, a cheaper representation. Here the gains carry direct engineering
stakes, because resolving or smoothing the asperity geometry changes the \emph{predicted shear strength}, the
quantity on which the chart and the level set disagree.

\paragraph{What a direct-shear test of a rock joint measures.} A rock joint is a fracture surface inside rock: the two faces left behind when the rock splits. The faces are rough, and their bumps, the \emph{asperities}, interlock like two meshed sawteeth. In a direct-shear test, a press holds the two faces together under a fixed normal stress $\sigma_n$ and then slides the top face sideways over the bottom one while recording the shear stress $\tau$ needed to keep it moving. Because the asperities interlock, the top face cannot slide horizontally without also riding up the flanks of the bumps, and the joint opens vertically as it shears. That climbing is \emph{dilatancy}, and it makes the joint stronger than a flat sawcut would be, because part of the applied shear now does work lifting the normal load. Engineers summarize the measured resistance by the \emph{apparent friction}, the stress ratio $\muapp=\tau/\sigma_n$, and read it as an \emph{apparent friction angle} $\arctan\muapp$ that combines the material's base friction with the geometric climb. The asperity geometry, not a material constant, sets how much extra strength the climb supplies, so a method's accuracy on this test is decided by how faithfully it represents the bumps.

The argument proceeds only after the analytical ladder of Section~\ref{sec:verif} has earned the right to make
it. We first state the problem, the data, and the closed-form anchor that pins the emergent friction law
(Section~\ref{sec:cv7-data}); we then report the head-to-head reconstruction and its mechanical consequence on
the real Inada profile (Section~\ref{sec:cv7-headline}), carry the machinery through two mutually deformable
blocks (Section~\ref{sec:cv7-p1}) and a dilatancy-versus-roughness law (Section~\ref{sec:cv7-p2}), and close
with the atlas-versus-level-set comparison on identical shear (Section~\ref{sec:cv7-payoff}). Three further
studies---the transition-map detector inside the contact loop, a cyclic energy ledger that verifies
Remark~\ref{cor:second-law}, and an explicit material-point cross-check---are reported in
Appendix~\ref{app:supp}.

\subsubsection{Problem, data, and the Patton anchor}\label{sec:cv7-data}

The specimen is a real surface, not an idealised one: a tensile fracture in Inada granite (Digital Rocks Portal \#273, DOI~10.17612/QXSA-TK92; \citealp{inada2020digitalrocks}), scanned as a height map $z(x,y)$ that gives the surface elevation $z$ over the in-plane coordinates $(x,y)$. The scan is sampled at $23.4~\mu$m, and the surface is self-affine: its roughness repeats statistically across scales, with Hurst exponent $H\approx0.78$ and fractal dimension $D\approx2.2$, so asperities exist from the micron sampling up to the joint length. The root-mean-square roughness is $1.70$~mm, matching the published value. No closed form predicts its shear response, so geometric fidelity alone determines the accuracy of a method on this test.

Each of the two mating faces is carried by a learned height chart $h_\theta:\mathcal D\to\R$ (Section~\ref{sec:fourier}), a random-Fourier-feature MLP with weights $\theta$ that stores the surface as a graph $z=h_\theta(x)$ rather than as a field in the surrounding space. Because the surface is single-valued, with one height per in-plane point, the chart is already its own inverse coordinate. The transition map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$, which composes one face's boundary chart with the inverse of the other's, then needs no numerical inversion, and the signed gap reduces to a vertical offset. The next equation states that gap and the surface normal it carries.
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cv7-gap}
\gn(x,z)=z-h_\theta(x),\qquad
\nbf=\frac{(-h_\theta'(x),\,1)}{\sqrt{1+h_\theta'(x)^2}},\qquad \gn<0\ \Leftrightarrow\ \text{penetration},
\end{equation}
%
Here $\gn$ is the signed normal gap (negative when the point lies below the surface, i.e.\ in penetration), $\nbf$ is the unit outward normal built from the surface slope $h_\theta'(x)$, and the equation says simply that the gap is how far a point sits above or below the height graph. The slope $h_\theta'(x)$ is the moving part: it sets the normal direction, and through it the tilt of every contact force. The solver receives the small-slope vertical proxy of the gap, and the true foot point is recovered only for verification.

The two faces are sheared under constant normal stress, with the normal degree of freedom solved to equilibrium at each shear increment, so the test is quasi-static direct shear (Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-bvp}). Friction is \emph{plain Coulomb}: the local coefficient $\mu=\tan\phi_b$ is held constant, where $\phi_b$ is the base friction angle of the rock material. There is \emph{no imposed dilatancy law}. The dilation and the apparent friction angle are therefore emergent outputs of the resolved asperity geometry. We report the apparent friction as the stress ratio $\muapp=\tau/\sigma_n$.

The validation has one closed-form anchor, derived from the simplest interlocking surface. Patton
\citep{patton1966multiple} idealised a joint as two mating sawtooth faces whose flanks rise at a single
asperity angle $i$. The following proposition derives the apparent friction of that joint from a force balance
on the resolved, tilted face, so that the Patton law is a consequence of plain Coulomb friction rather than an
imposed constitutive rule.

\begin{remark}[Emergent Patton law and its anti-plane companion]\label{prop:patton}
Let a sawtooth flank rise at angle $i$, so the resolved contact normal is $\nbf=(-\sin i,\cos i)$ in the
(shear, normal) frame, and let plain Coulomb friction $\norm{\tbf_T}=\mu t_N$ with $\mu=\tan\phi_b$ act on that
face. The contact reaction $\fbf=t_N\nbf+\mu t_N\tbf$, with $\tbf=(\cos i,\sin i)$ the up-flank tangent at the
cone bound, resolves onto the macroscopic axes as $T=t_N(\sin i+\mu\cos i)$ and $N=t_N(\cos i-\mu\sin i)$, so
the apparent friction for shear \emph{across} the ridges is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:patton}
\muapp=\frac{T}{N}=\tan(\phi_b+i)=\frac{\tan i+\mu}{1-\mu\tan i},
\end{equation}
with dilation rate $\dot u_n/\dot u_t=\tan i$ (the block climbs the flank). For shear \emph{along} the ridges
(anti-plane) the slip is parallel to the ridge, the friction $\mu t_N$ acts on a facet inflated by $1/\cos i$
with no climb, and
\begin{equation}\label{eq:patton-along}
\muapp=\frac{\mu}{\cos i},\qquad \text{dilation}=0 .
\end{equation}
A representation that smooths the slope $i\mapsto i'<i$ thus under-predicts the across-ridge strength by
$\Delta\muapp\approx\sec^2(\phi_b+i)\,(i-i')$.
\end{remark}

Remark~\ref{prop:patton} ties strength directly to the resolved slope $i$, the quantity on which the chart
and the level set disagree. The law is not imposed: plain Coulomb friction and the resolved tilt are supplied
to the solver, and on a mating sawtooth the framework reproduces \eqref{eq:patton} to $0.00\%$, with the
dilation rate equal to $\tan i$ to within $1\%$; the anti-plane response \eqref{eq:patton-along} is the
zero-dilation mode observed when the deformable joint is sheared along the asperity fabric
(Section~\ref{sec:cv7-p1}). The rock-mechanics literature reads $i$ as a joint-roughness coefficient
\citep{barton1977shear,barton1990review}; here $i$ is not fitted but the slope distribution the chart resolves
directly. Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-surfaces} renders the two mating Inada faces the solver shears, coloured by the
local gap, the geometry from which the apparent friction and dilation emerge.

\subsubsection{Chart versus level set on the real Inada profile}\label{sec:cv7-headline}

We fit two representations to the same Inada profile and then shear them. The first is the height chart $h_\theta$. The second is the comparison baseline: an ambient 2-D neural SDF $\phi(x,z)$, a plain coordinate MLP that learns the signed distance to the surface as a field in the plane, from whose zero level set we extract a reconstructed surface $h_{\rm SDF}(x)$. We compare the two on three things in turn: how well each reconstructs the surface, how well each captures the asperity slope, and what shear strength each then predicts.

The reconstruction gap is large and one-signed. The height chart reproduced the measured surface to $2.3~\mu$m with 66k parameters; the ambient SDF reached only $107~\mu$m, $47\times$ worse, and did so with \emph{more} parameters (83k). The level set spends its capacity on a 2-D distance field and still resolves the 1-D surface far less well. The mechanism is the spectral bias of a fixed-capacity coordinate MLP \citep{rahaman2019spectral}: such a network learns smooth, low-frequency content first and starves the sharp high-frequency detail. The Fourier-feature encoding of the chart defeats that bias \citep{tancik2020fourier}, whereas the plain SDF carries it intrinsically.

This mechanism is measured, not only argued. Figure~\ref{fig:spectral-bias} compares the radially-averaged power spectral density (Welch periodogram) of each trained reconstruction of the real Inada profile against the ground-truth profile. In the friction-relevant asperity band $[1,14.2]$~cyc/mm the Fourier chart never rolls off---it retains $100\%$ of the ground-truth band power and stays within $0.0$~dB of the truth across the band---whereas the plain MLP and the ambient SDF roll off at $0.33$ and $0.25$~cyc/mm respectively and retain only $0.027\%$ and $0.019\%$ of the band power ($-35.7$ and $-37.2$~dB). The spectral cutoff sits two decades below the asperity band: the level set's smoothing is a frequency-domain truncation of exactly the slopes that set the strength, and the chart's win is that it does not truncate them.

Height reconstruction error is not the relevant metric for contact. Friction and dilation depend on the asperity \emph{angle} $i=\arctan\abs{h'}$, the local slope of the surface, not on the height itself. On slope the two representations separate. The chart reproduced the mean asperity angle, $19.8^\circ$ against $19.4^\circ$ for the true surface, while the SDF's spectral bias smoothed it to $12.5^\circ$. Through the Patton law \eqref{eq:patton} the smoothed slope yields a lower strength: shearing the two representations, the level set under-predicted the peak shear strength by $61\%$. The total dilation, by contrast, was barely affected ($0.5\%$). Dilation is set by the large-scale waviness the SDF captures, while strength is controlled by the asperity slope the SDF smooths; the strongly affected strength beside the barely affected dilation is consistent with this mechanism. Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-capstone} collects the results.

The claim is \emph{not} that the chart has a lower height RMSE than the SDF on a well-sampled single-valued profile, since dense interpolation of heights is easy for both. It is that the SDF smooths the asperity \emph{slopes}, and that the smoothing under-predicts strength through \eqref{eq:patton}, together with the storage scaling: the chart is $O(N_{\rm surface})$ with $O(1)$ queries and, with Fourier features, no spectral-bias ceiling, whereas the ambient SDF must represent a 2-D field whose zero set is the surface. This is the same scaling argument as the Koch ceiling, here on a real surface spanning roughly $3.5$ decades of scale, from $23.4~\mu$m asperities over a $73$~mm joint. Single profiles are single-asperity noisy in their peak, so the reported strength uses the steady or ensemble response. The surfaces are single-valued, so the argument rests on the spectral-bias ceiling and storage, not on overhangs.

\subsubsection{Phase 1: two mutually deformable decoder blocks}\label{sec:cv7-p1}

The Inada result treats the blocks as rigid. Phase 1 makes both faces deformable. Two elastic blocks, each carried by a boundary-fitted Fourier chart decoder $D_X:\boldsymbol\xi\mapsto\xbf$ that maps a reference coordinate $\boldsymbol\xi$ to the deformed position $\xbf$ (Section~\ref{sec:fourier}), meet at a fully rough mating interface and exchange node-to-surface penalty and regularised Coulomb forces through the chart-FEM solver. Both faces are rough; neither is a flat platen; the contact is mutual. The roughness lives in the decoder geometry, not in an interface constitutive law, so dilation and strength again emerge from the resolved bumps rather than from a calibrated rule.

The geometry is verified before any mechanical claim. The Fourier decoders reconstruct the rough faces without element foldover ($\det J\in[0.97,1.03]$ on every element, so the chart Jacobian stays positive and the mesh never inverts), and a method-of-manufactured-solutions study on the curved chart of \emph{both} blocks recovered the optimal $O(h^2)$ convergence of the P1 discretization (measured rates $2.39$ and $1.95$). The chart-FEM provably solves on the rough geometry it resolves.

The mechanical response is emergently \emph{anisotropic}: the joint resists differently depending on which way it is sheared relative to the asperity fabric. Shearing across, along, and obliquely to that fabric gave distinct peak apparent friction, with $\muapp$ of $0.42$ (out-of-plane), $0.54$ (in-plane), and $0.74$ (mixed), and with dilation ranging from $0.038$ to $0.090$~mm across the three modes. No direction-dependent friction is imposed; the anisotropy is the asperity geometry expressed through plain Coulomb. Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-twoblock} shows the three modes.

The emergent response is mesh-converged. Refining the two-block shear across four meshes ($n_{\rm cells}=6,8,10,12$; $2.6$k to $20.7$k tetrahedra), the peak $\muapp$ settled to $\approx0.53$ within $2\%$ ($0.510\to0.528\to0.535\to0.526$) and the total dilation converged to $\approx0.063$~mm ($0.094\to0.078\to0.066\to0.063$~mm; the coarse mesh over-predicts), with $\det J_{\min}$ steady near $0.96$. Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-p1vm} shows the von Mises field on both blocks across refinement. Two numerical features qualify this convergence. First, the two-deformable, moving-master, node-to-surface Coulomb friction residual stays at $3.8$ to $4.8\%$ across all meshes. Because it is mesh-\emph{independent}, it reflects the non-smoothness of the moving-master contact, not discretization error, and the frictionless and one-block cases converge to $10^{-9}$. An augmented-Lagrangian or mortar formulation \citep{alart1991mixed,simo1992augmented,puso2004mortar} is the route to tight convergence and is left as future work. Second, the rigid-platen prescribed-displacement boundary concentrates the imposed shear into the top element layer and produces a spurious von Mises spike there. The joint-interface stress and the contact-derived $\muapp$ and dilation are unaffected, since they come from the contact forces, and the platen layer is trimmed for display.

dilatancy gap reported in Section~\ref{sec:cv7-payoff}.

\subsubsection{Phase 2: the dilatancy-versus-roughness law}\label{sec:cv7-p2}

Phase 2 asks how strength depends on roughness, and builds the dilatancy law from the geometry rather than calibrating one. Holding the friction coefficient and the loading protocol fixed and varying only the root-mean-square roughness of the decoder surface, the peak apparent friction rose monotonically from $0.61$ to $1.20$ as the surface RMS rose from $0.022$ to $0.066$~mm, with the emergent dilation tracking it ($0.018\to0.059$~mm). A rougher joint mobilises a higher apparent friction angle and dilates more, here recovered as an output of resolved geometry under plain Coulomb, in the direction of the empirical Barton trend \citep{barton1977shear,barton1990review}. The same monotone law appears when the spectral cutoff of the decoder is swept instead of the amplitude ($0.68\to1.21$), which isolates the resolved frequency content as the control. Across the entire sweep the decoder reconstruction stayed under $8\%$ of RMS, so the strength variation is geometry the chart resolves, not a reconstruction artifact. Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-roughness} plots the law.

\subsubsection{Atlas versus level set on identical shear}\label{sec:cv7-payoff}

Having established that strength and dilatancy are emergent properties of the resolved geometry, we now isolate the representation by running the \emph{identical} shear twice: once on the geometry from the atlas (the true rough surface) and once on the geometry from the ambient signed-distance function's smoothed zero level set, and read off the difference in the predicted mechanics. A band-limited rough surface is carried by a trained three-dimensional chart decoder, verified first: the Fourier decoder reconstructs the surface to $2.2\%$ of RMS, against $48\%$ for a vanilla $\tanh$ decoder and $\sim16\%$ for the ambient neural signed-distance function, with no element foldover and $O(h^2)$ chart-FEM convergence (Section~\ref{sec:fourier}). The same band-limited surface is then sheared under chart-FEM Coulomb friction.

The emergent dilatancy is a geometric consequence of the resolved normal, made precise by the following
remark.

\begin{remark}[Emergent dilatancy from resolved geometry]\label{prop:dilatancy}
Let a single-valued rough face $z=h(x)$ be carried by a height chart with resolved normal
$\nbf=(-h',1)/\sqrt{1+h'^2}$ (eq.~\eqref{eq:cv7-gap}), and let plain Coulomb friction act with no constitutive
dilation law. The contact force $t_N\nbf$ tilts with $h'$, and the friction-projected slip rides the surface
tangent, whose vertical component is $\abs{h'}/\sqrt{1+h'^2}$; integrating the climbing motion over the
bearing asperities gives a non-zero apparent dilation angle wherever the slopes are resolved,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:emergent-dilatancy}
\tan\psi_{\mathrm{dil}}=\big\langle\,\abs{h'(x)}\,\big\rangle_{\text{bearing asperities}}=\langle\tan i\rangle,
\end{equation}
the slope-weighted asperity angle over the contacting set. Smoothing the surface drives $h'\to0$ and hence
$\psi_{\mathrm{dil}}\to0$, so a representation that low-passes the slopes under-predicts the dilatancy.
\end{remark}

On the atlas geometry the dilatancy emerges from the asperities with no effective angle imposed. With friction switched off, the apparent friction rises from $0$ to $0.171$, a pure geometric dilatancy with dilation angle $\approx9.7^\circ$ produced by interlocking alone, and with $\mu=0.3$ it rises from an initial $0.24$ to a peak of $0.467$, the dilatant strengthening of \eqref{eq:patton} read off the real geometry. On the SDF-smoothed geometry the same shear under-predicts the emergent dilatancy by $98\%$ (frictionless: $0.004$ against $0.171$, because the smoothed surface has almost no asperity slope and hence almost no dilatancy) and the shear strength by $35\%$ (with friction: $0.306$ against $0.467$). Table~\ref{tab:cv7-atlas-sdf} collects the comparison and Figure~\ref{fig:cv7-atlas-sdf} shows it. No dilation angle is supplied to either run; the geometry produces it, and the level set, by smoothing the geometry, fails to.

The result holds on real data, not only the synthetic band-limited surface. On an actual Inada-granite topography patch resolved at the mesh scale, the atlas decoder reconstructs the surface to $3.2\%$ of RMS against the SDF's $15.8\%$, and the level set under-predicts the frictionless geometric dilatancy by $\sim60\%$ and the friction strength by $\sim17\%$. The two geometries must not be conflated. The $61\%$ strength figure is the real Inada 1-D profile (Section~\ref{sec:cv7-headline}), and the $98\%/35\%$ pair is the synthetic 3-D decoder run. The sign is nonetheless uniform across both, and across five independent roughness realizations the level set under-predicts the emergent dilatancy by $58\%\pm35\%$. The magnitude depends on how much asperity slope each realization's smoothing destroys, but the under-prediction itself is a statistical fact, not a cherry-picked surface.

Three further studies carry the program beyond this headline and are reported in the supplementary appendix (Appendix~\ref{app:supp}): the transition-map height-chart detector routed as the FEM $(\text{gap},\text{normal})$ contract (active-set agreement $98.9\%$ for the chart against $95.8\%$ for the ambient SDF on $10^5$ query points, at $1.47\times$ the per-query cost), a cyclic stick/slip energy ledger that closes per cycle, and an explicit ChartMPM cross-check that reproduces the Coulomb friction floor.

This validation has a deliberately narrow scope. The blocks here are one deformable face on a rigid mating surface, or two single-realization deformable blocks. The surfaces are single-valued, so the level-set failure rests on the spectral-bias ceiling and storage, not on overhangs; overhangs would require the micro-CT voxel volumes and the full transition-map atlas with decoder inversion at chart overlaps. The roughness is band-limited to the wavelengths the mesh resolves, with a modest amplitude to keep $\det J>0$. The two-block friction residual and the cyclic energy bookkeeping remain open numerical items addressed by augmented-Lagrangian/mortar contact. Within that scope, the apparent friction $\muapp=\tan(\phi_b+i)$ and the dilation are emergent from resolved asperity geometry, the boundary-fitted Fourier chart resolves the asperity slopes the ambient SDF smooths, and the level set therefore under-predicts strength ($61\%$ Inada, $35\%$ synthetic) and dilatancy ($98\%$ synthetic frictionless).

\section{Discussion, limitations, and conclusions}\label{sec:disc}

Two statements organise the results. First, the transition map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$ is the
optimal-transport coupling of the two boundary measures (Section~\ref{sec:tmap:ot}): a two-sided,
mass-conserving operator that transmits a constant pressure exactly across a non-matching interface and solves a
genuine two-body deformable--deformable problem. Second, as the empirical consequence of that coupling on a conforming interface,
the geometric representation, not the constraint-enforcement scheme, controls contact fidelity on
geometrically hard contact. The closest-point projection that the node-to-segment tradition rejects as fragile is
not the opposite of the transition map but its convex-cost / partial-contact limit, while the arclength-monotone
rearrangement is the conforming limit; the two regimes are one framework, which resolves the apparent tension
between ``a composition, not a projection'' and the projection's documented utility. The benchmarks of
Sections~\ref{sec:verif} and~\ref{sec:cv7} put the
transition-map chart and the ambient level set on the same problems, meshes, and tolerances, and the measured
difference between them is therefore attributable to the geometry alone. This section reads those results as the
two statements above, states the preconditions and limitations that bound the method, and concludes.

\subsection{The transition map as a measure coupling}\label{sec:disc:ot}

The coupling has three measured consequences. \emph{The two limits} (Figure~\ref{fig:ot-coupling}). For a
localised convex indenter under
partial support the admissible optimal-transport coupling restricts to the closest-point projection,
$\gn=\norm{\bm p-\bm c}-R$; for two mated rough surfaces it is the global arclength-monotone (Brenier)
rearrangement $\tmap=\hat F_B^{-1}\circ\hat F_A$ \citep{brenier1991polar,mccann1995existence}
(Remark~\ref{rem:ot-limits}). The closest-point limit is required for partial contact, where the arclength map
transports all slave mass onto all master mass and smears the load across the interface (Appendix~\ref{app:ot}). \emph{Mortar-consistent assembly.} The discrete force
$\fbf_I=\sum_q w_q N_I(p_N\nbf+\tbf_T)$ carries the consistent interface mass $M=\tfrac{L}{6}[[2,1],[1,2]]$, and
the master interpolation is a partition of unity along the correspondence, $\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)=1$ (distinct
from, and downstream of, the optimal-transport mass marginal $\chi_\#\hat\mu_A=\hat\mu_B$ that places the foot;
see Appendix~\ref{app:ot:discrete}), so a constant pressure transmits across a non-matching mesh to
$1.4\times10^{-16}$, $67\times$ tighter than the node-lumped scheme, which fails the test. \emph{The two-body
unlock.} Because the coupling is two-sided and transpose-coupled, with a symmetric positive-semidefinite
four-block tangent $\bm K_{ms}=\bm K_{sm}^{\top}$ that is consistent at frozen geometry (Proposition~\ref{prop:spsd};
finite-difference verified to $3.45\times10^{-11}$ on the frozen-geometry residual, force balance
$4.4\times10^{-16}$), it solves the deformable Hertz contact of two elastic blocks and the
$3\times3$ N-body disc array of Section~\ref{sec:ex_twobody}, the master-coupling blocks the rigid-master suite
never assembles.

\subsection{When the chart improves on the level set}\label{sec:disc:regime}

\subsection{Preconditions and trade-offs}\label{sec:disc:precond}

Two conditions bound where the transition-map detector applies, and one property bounds what it returns; each is
a geometric fact about the parametrisation, checked once, not a solver tolerance. \emph{Star-shapedness and
single-valuedness.} A radial chart represents a body as a single-valued radius along every direction, valid only
when each ray from the centre meets the boundary once; a plane or half-space is not star-shaped about any finite
centre, so floors and rigid platens remain on the signed-distance path while the radial detector handles
star-shaped obstacles. A height chart relaxes the need for a centre but represents the surface as a graph, so it
admits no overhangs and no re-entrant detail; a genuinely re-entrant surface requires the full decoder atlas with
Newton inversion at chart overlaps \eqref{eq:newton-invert}, the generalisation outlined below.
\emph{Conservative-large, sign-exact, not eikonal.} By Remark~\ref{prop:active-set} the radial gap is
sign-exact, so the chart and the level-set active sets coincide on star-shaped bodies, but its magnitude is
enlarged by $1/\cos\alpha$ and its gradient is not of unit length: $\norm{\grad g}$ can reach $\sim10^{4}$ in deep
concave valleys. The two gaps stand in one identity: the single-body radial gap is
$g_{\mathrm{rad}}=g_\perp/\cos\alpha$ with $\cos\alpha=\dhat\cdot\nbf$ (biased on the flanks), while the
two-body measure-coupling gap $\gn=(\xbf_s-\tmap(\xbf_s))\!\cdot\!\nbf_s=g_\perp$ of Appendix~\ref{app:ot} is the
exact normal-projected distance. The $1/\cos\alpha$ enlargement is therefore a property of the \emph{single-body}
inverse-radial chart that the measure coupling removes: the OT gap is the unbiased refinement the radial chart
only approximates. A perpendicular-foot refinement recovers the normal-projected distance but is reserved for
verification, since that foot jumps across the medial axis and would violate Remark~\ref{cor:second-law}.
\emph{Storage versus query cost.} The recursive chart of the Koch benchmark stores the generating rule in $O(1)$ and answers a
query in $O(\text{depth})$ work, whereas a uniform level set must discretise the ambient field at its finest scale,
$O(9^{n})$ storage; the level set answers each query more cheaply, by an $O(1)$ lookup, but its fixed capacity
caps the geometry it can represent, and that ceiling is what the Koch benchmark measures.

One trade-off is worth isolating, because it is the most carefully measured. Routing the height-chart detector
into the contact loop on the real rough joint, the chart's active set agrees with the analytic reference $98.9\%$
of the time against $95.8\%$ for the ambient field, and the contact-driving gap RMSE is $4.2\%$ against $44.7\%$,
a $10.5\times$ sharper quantity, at $1.47\times$ the per-query cost. The chart does not win everywhere: its
random-Fourier height gradient gives a noisier tip normal, $10.3^{\circ}$ of median deviation against the level
set's $0.8^{\circ}$ at the sharpest asperity peaks. This local loss does not weaken the strength prediction,
because the penalty force and the friction cone read the gap and the active set, not the pointwise normal at an
isolated tip, so the smoothed \emph{gap} of the level set propagates into the response where the chart's noisier
tip normal does not.

\subsection{Limitations}\label{sec:disc:limits}

Four limitations follow directly from the measurements, each stated with what it does and does not put at risk.
\emph{The two-deformable friction residual.} The frictionless and one-block solves converge to a contact Newton
residual of $\sim10^{-9}$ with a consistent tangent; two mutually deformable blocks under Coulomb friction do not,
the residual holding at $3.8$--$4.8\%$ and not shrinking under refinement. Its mesh-independence identifies it as
the non-smoothness of moving-master node-to-surface contact, not a discretisation error, to be cured by a
semismooth-Newton, augmented-Lagrangian, or mortar treatment of the tangential complementarity
\citep{alart1991mixed,simo1992augmented,puso2004mortar,chouly2013nitsche}---a constraint-enforcement upgrade
orthogonal to \emph{both} the geometric representation and the measure-coupling formulation argued for here, and
one that plugs into the two-sided optimal-transport mortar tangent already assembled in
Section~\ref{sec:ex_twobody}. It does not threaten the main result, because the
emergent quantities are mesh-converged ($\muapp\to0.53\pm2\%$, dilation $\to0.063$~mm).
\emph{The two-body contact-edge floor.} The deformable-Hertz half-width and peak pressure of
Section~\ref{sec:ex_twobody} carry a $\sim3$--$6\%$ contact-edge residual that does not shrink under refinement;
it is the infinite Hertz edge-pressure slope resolved over one element, so $p_0$ plateaus near $5.8\%$, a genuine
floor rather than a vanishing discretisation error. Both CV-8 and CV-9a are single-realization, plane-strain CST
(small-strain) solves, frictionless, with rigid outer walls in CV-9a. The geometric tangent term
$d\nbf/d\chi$ is dropped under the standard small-rotation approximation, which leaves the converged solution
tangent-independent (identical $a_{\mathrm{fem}}$ at $40$ and $120$ iterations); it is a convergence-rate lever,
not an accuracy lever.
\emph{A single band-limited realisation in small strain.} The rough-geometry validation rests on one band-limited
surface realisation in small-strain elasticity; a single realisation is statistically spiky and the roughness sweep
of Section~\ref{sec:cv7} reports the trend, not any absolute value. Finite strain, asperity-tip plasticity, and
asperity damage lie outside the present model.
\emph{The contact gap is a small-slope proxy.} On the height-chart joint the gap is the vertical offset
\eqref{eq:height-gap-proj}, exact in sign and accurate to $O(\text{slope}^2)$ in magnitude, which is why the rock-joint fidelity
story is told in slopes and normals rather than gap magnitude; the Inada head-to-head shows that this is exactly
where the level set fails.
\emph{The dynamic path is opt-in.} The production material-point oracle still computes the gap from the
signed-distance gradient, and the chart detector drives the superformula analytically and the rock joint numerically as an opt-in path
rather than the default everywhere. The explicit cross-check reproduces the Coulomb floor
($\muapp\approx0.34$ against base $\mu=0.4$); what makes the eventual replacement conservative is the
energy-consistency of Remark~\ref{prop:matched-normal}, confirmed by the cyclic ledger of
Appendix~\ref{app:supp}, whose per-cycle balance closes from $1.09$ to $1.01$ on the deformable joint.

\emph{A bounded trait with a definite resolution path.} The slope proxy and the moving-master residual are not
open-ended failures but bounded numerical traits, each with a matched cure, and the three cures compose into one
roadmap. First, the vertical proxy $g_{\mathrm{vert}}$ is replaced by the exact normal-projected gap, the
perpendicular foot $\bar{\bm\xi}=\arg\min_{\bm\xi}\norm{\xbf-\map{B}(\bm\xi)}$ on the master chart, which removes
the $O(\text{slope}^2)$ magnitude error while preserving the sign-exact active set of
Remark~\ref{prop:active-set}; on a height chart this is a single scalar Newton solve per query, and the radial
chart already carries it (the perpendicular-foot refinement of Section~\ref{sec:disc:precond}). Second, the
consistent tangent of Appendix~\ref{app:linearization}, with the chart-intrinsic curvature
\eqref{eq:weingarten-chart} and friction-direction \eqref{eq:friction-dir-deriv} blocks restored, recovers the
asymptotic quadratic Newton rate; we measured this directly, $8$ iterations against $40$ on a smooth curved
master surface frictionless and $11$ against $44$ under friction, with the converged solution unchanged. Third,
what survives the first two upgrades is the genuine non-smoothness of the moving contact point under Coulomb
stick--slip: completing the tangent leaves the apparent friction invariant to within $0.3\%$ and the residual
floor unmoved on the rough joint, isolating the plateau as a tangential complementarity problem to be closed by
a semismooth (primal--dual active-set) Newton or an augmented-Lagrangian or mortar treatment of the tangential
condition \citep{alart1991mixed,simo1992augmented,puso2004mortar,chouly2013nitsche}. The $3.8$--$4.8\%$ plateau
is thus a diagnosed property of constraint enforcement, orthogonal to the chart-versus-level-set representation
result the rest of the paper establishes.

\subsection{Conclusions and outlook}\label{sec:disc:concl}

Computational contact is an optimal-transport coupling of two boundary measures: the transition map
$\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$ is the Brenier map of the quadratic-cost problem, of which the closest-point
projection is the convex-cost / partial-contact limit and the arclength-monotone rearrangement the conforming /
rough limit. Read this way the gap and the traction are continuous, mass-preserving fields whose discrete master
interpolation is a partition of unity along the correspondence, $\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)=1$ (the discrete
reflection of mass conservation), so a constant pressure
transmits exactly across a non-matching interface (patch test $1.4\times10^{-16}$, $67\times$ tighter than the
node-lumped scheme), and the coupling is two-sided: its symmetric positive-semidefinite four-block tangent
$\bm K_{ms}=\bm K_{sm}^{\top}$, consistent at frozen geometry, solves a genuine two-body deformable--deformable
problem (deformable Hertz $a$ to $2.75\%$, $p_0$ to $5.82\%$; a $3\times3$ array to $0.58\%$).

The geometric representation of the boundary, not the constraint-enforcement scheme, then controls contact fidelity on
geometrically hard contact, the empirical consequence of the coupling on a conforming interface. A fixed-capacity ambient signed-distance field learns low spatial frequencies first
and high frequencies slowly or never (Remark~\ref{prop:ntk}), so it smooths cusps, asperities, and fine
relief and carries a non-smooth medial axis in any concavity; a boundary chart stores the same body as a function
one dimension lower, with no interior and no medial axis, and a random-Fourier-feature input removes the spectral
ceiling (Remarks~\ref{prop:ntk} and~\ref{cor:capacity}), so the chart resolves the boundary where the
ambient field cannot. Posed as a transition map on a learned atlas, the detector returns a conservative matched
normal (Remark~\ref{prop:matched-normal}) and a sign-exact active set (Remark~\ref{prop:active-set}),
and it inherits the variational-inequality and frictional-dissipation structure of Section~\ref{sec:govern}
unchanged. The closed-form ladder confirms the chart and the level set are equivalent on convex and superposable
geometry (Hertz, Cattaneo--Mindlin, Brazilian, nine-disc) and separate measurably on cusps (superformula) and
self-similar refinement (Koch); the real Inada-granite joint shows that the apparent friction $\tan(\phi_b+i)$ and the normal dilation emerge from
the resolved asperity geometry under plain Coulomb friction (Remarks~\ref{prop:patton}
and~\ref{prop:dilatancy}), and that the level set, by smoothing the slopes, under-predicts strength by $61\%$ on
the granite profile and dilatancy by $98\%$ on the synthetic block.

The two-body deformable--deformable problem is solved, not deferred: the symmetric four-block tangent of
Appendix~\ref{app:ot} drives deformable Hertz between two elastic blocks (CV-8) and a $3\times3$ N-body disc
array (CV-9a) to the closed forms (Section~\ref{sec:ex_twobody}), so what remains is dimensional, not
two-body. Three directions remain, each removing a stated restriction. The star-shapedness and single-valuedness
preconditions are lifted by a multi-chart decoder atlas, in which several boundary-fitted decoder charts cover a
body and their overlaps reconcile through Newton-inverted transition maps \eqref{eq:newton-invert}, so that
overhangs and re-entrant lobes no single chart can hold are handled chart by chart. Extending the \emph{two-body}
optimal-transport coupling to full three-dimensional deformable Hertz, with local mesh refinement at the contact
patch, would close the resolution gap that limited the two-dimensional line-contact verifications and place the
chart-FEM contact pressure against the closed-form $p_0$ and $a(F)$ in three dimensions---the dimensional step,
not the two-body step, which is already taken. Finally, embedding the transition-map detector inside a
mortar or augmented-Lagrangian dynamic solver, with a finite-strain hyperelastic interior, would extend the
representation-controls-fidelity result from quasi-static rock joints to large-sliding, finite-deformation
contact. Across all three the conclusion is constant: fidelity on hard contact follows the geometry one chooses
to resolve, and the transition map resolves what the level set smooths.

Two directions reach further. The first is topo-geometric. A contact pair tracked across a sliding increment can
leave the chart $i$ that first held it, and the inter-body map $\tmap$ then composes with the intra-body
transition map $\psi_{ij}$ of \eqref{eq:intra-tmap} to re-express the migrating foot in the neighbouring chart
$j$ before re-pairing, $\tmap\circ\psi_{ij}$, the active set handed off across the chart overlap by the
partition-of-unity blend of the multiplicative-Schwarz coupling (Remark~\ref{rem:schwarz})---the same partition
of unity that makes the inter-body optimal-transport marginal $\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)=1$ exact---with the
Newton inversion \eqref{eq:newton-invert} kept local to each chart. Genus, branching, and overhang then reduce to
bookkeeping on the overlap graph rather than a global re-parametrisation, so the detector inherits the atlas's
topological generality at the cost of a chart-membership test per active node. The second couples the geometry
to material degradation. Because the boundary is itself a trainable field---a height chart $h_\theta$ or a
volumetric decoder $D_\theta$---it can carry an evolving state: an Archard-type wear law or a surface
phase-field damage posed on the chart parameter domain evolves $h_\theta\mapsto h_\theta(t)$, so that asperity
crushing, tip plasticity, and micro-cracking feed back into the very geometry the detector reads. The resolved
contact tractions then drive the degradation that reshapes the asperities, closing a loop between the geometry
and the response it produces, and carrying the representation-controls-fidelity thesis from elastic rock joints
to wear, third-body debris, and gouge formation.

Across both reaches the transition map---read as an optimal-transport coupling of the two boundary measures---is
the mass-conserving, two-sided operator that resolves what the level set smooths and couples two deformable
bodies the level set cannot.

\section{Closed-form benchmark solutions}\label{app:closedforms}

This appendix collects the closed forms that serve as acceptance targets for the
code-verification benchmarks (Hertz, Cattaneo--Mindlin, Brazilian, nine-disc). Each solution is small-strain, linear-elastic,
and quasi-static. Each is also the limit a finite-deformation contact solve must reproduce
once the loading and material parameters enter the appropriate regime, so a numerical
result that misses these targets signals a coding error rather than a modelling choice. Each
solution is derived symbolically and verified against an independent vectorised evaluator.
Throughout the paper we use one sign convention: a normal gap $\gn<0$ denotes penetration, and
compressive stress is negative. The Hertz and nine-disc forms below are also the acceptance targets for the
two-body optimal-transport solves of Appendix~\ref{app:ot}: the combined reduced modulus $1/E^{*}$ of
\eqref{eq:hertz-effective} sets the deformable--deformable Hertz half-width, and the equibiaxial centre stress
\eqref{eq:ninedisc-center} is the target the $3\times3$ N-body coupling must reach with the per-pole load $N$ as
an emergent output.

The three-dimensional axisymmetric forms below are the canonical reference, and the
numerical study restricts itself to their two-dimensional reductions. The verification in
Section~\ref{sec:verif} exercises the line-contact limit of the Hertz benchmark, namely the contact
half-width, and the planar reduction of Cattaneo--Mindlin, whose stick-radius exponent is $1/2$ rather
than the axisymmetric $1/3$. The plane-strain solver reaches these regimes; we leave full
three-dimensional Hertz to future work.

\subsection{Hertz normal contact}\label{app:hertz}

Two elastic bodies of radii $R_1,R_2$ and moduli $(E_1,\nu_1),(E_2,\nu_2)$ are pressed
together by a normal force $F$. Hertz theory replaces this two-body problem with a single
contact between an effective elastic medium and an effective curved surface. We therefore
collapse the four elastic constants and two radii into one modulus and one radius:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hertz-effective}
  \frac{1}{E^\ast}=\frac{1-\nu_1^2}{E_1}+\frac{1-\nu_2^2}{E_2},
  \qquad
  \frac{1}{R}=\frac{1}{R_1}+\frac{1}{R_2},
\end{equation}
where $E^\ast$ is the effective contact modulus and $R$ the effective radius of the gap.
With $R$ fixed, the undeformed gap near the apex is the paraboloid $\gn(r)=r^2/2R$, with
$r$ the in-plane distance from the contact axis. The axisymmetric Hertz solution
\citep{johnson1985contact} then gives the contact radius $a$, the pressure $p(r)$, its
peak value $p_0$, the approach $\delta$ (the relative rigid-body motion of the two bodies),
and the force--approach law:
\begin{align}
  a &= \left(\frac{3FR}{4E^\ast}\right)^{1/3}, \label{eq:hertz-a}\\
  p(r) &= p_0\sqrt{1-r^2/a^2}, \qquad
  p_0 = \frac{2E^\ast a}{\pi R} = \frac{3F}{2\pi a^2}, \label{eq:hertz-p}\\
  \delta &= \frac{a^2}{R}, \qquad
  F = \frac{4}{3}\,E^\ast\sqrt{R}\,\delta^{3/2}. \label{eq:hertz-force}
\end{align}
The contact radius grows as the cube root of the load, and the pressure traces a
hemispherical cap whose peak $p_0$ is $3/2$ the mean pressure $F/\pi a^2$. Integrating
this pressure over the contact recovers the applied load, $\int_0^a p(r)\,2\pi
r\,\mathrm{d}r = F$. The harness uses this identity as an independent consistency check on
the numerical pressure field.

Beneath the contact, the load redistributes into the bulk, and the stresses on the
symmetry axis follow \citep[Eq.~3.45]{johnson1985contact}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hertz-subsurface}
  \sigma_z = -\frac{p_0}{1+\zeta^2}, \qquad
  \sigma_r = \sigma_\theta
  = -p_0\!\left[(1+\nu)\!\left(1-\zeta\arctan\tfrac{1}{\zeta}\right)-\tfrac12\,\frac{1}{1+\zeta^2}\right],
  \qquad \zeta=\frac{z}{a}.
\end{equation}
Here $\sigma_z$ is the axial stress, $\sigma_r=\sigma_\theta$ the in-plane normal
stress, and $\zeta=z/a$ the depth measured in contact radii. The difference between these
components sets the shear that drives yield, which is why the maximum shear sits below the
surface rather than at it. For $\nu=0.3$ the maximum shear
$\tfrac12|\sigma_z-\sigma_r|$ reaches $\tau_{\max}=0.31\,p_0$ at depth $z/a=0.481$, the
von Mises stress peaks at $\sigma_{vM}^{\max}=0.62\,p_0$, and first yield occurs at
$p_0\approx 1.61\,\sigma_Y$. We accept the numerical solution when $a$, $p_0$, $\delta$,
and $F(\delta)$ agree to $<1\%$, the pressure integral to $<0.5\%$, and the subsurface
maximum shear lies at $z/a=0.48\pm0.02$ with magnitude $0.31\,p_0\pm2\%$.

The plane-strain solver reaches the line-contact limit rather than the full axisymmetric
case, so we use that limit as the indenter benchmark for the chart-FEM verification. For a
load per unit thickness $P'$, the half-width and the peak pressure are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:line-contact}
  a = 2\sqrt{\frac{P'R}{\pi E^\ast}}, \qquad
  p_0 = \frac{2P'}{\pi a} = \sqrt{\frac{P'E^\ast}{\pi R}},
\end{equation}
with $E^\ast = E/2(1-\nu^2)$ for two identical bodies and $a$ now the contact half-width
of the strip. The half-width grows as the square root of the load here, in contrast to the
cube-root growth of the axisymmetric radius in \eqref{eq:hertz-a}. The subsurface field of
a Hertzian half-plane pressure over $[-a,a]$ is the McEwen solution
\citep[Eq.~4.49]{johnson1985contact}, evaluated for the stress-bulb figures.

\subsection{Cattaneo--Mindlin frictional partial slip}\label{app:cattaneo}

A tangential load $Q<\mu P$ is applied to a pre-formed Hertz contact of radius $a$ and
peak pressure $p_0$ under normal load $P$. Because the tangential load stays below the
sliding limit $\mu P$, the contact does not slide as a whole. Instead it splits into an
inner stick zone $r\le c$ and an outer slip annulus $c\le r\le a$. The stick radius $c$
shrinks as $Q$ approaches $\mu P$ \citep{mindlin1949compliance,johnson1985contact}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cattaneo-c}
  \frac{c}{a} = \left(1-\frac{Q}{\mu P}\right)^{1/3}.
\end{equation}
The plane-strain solver of Section~\ref{sec:verif} verifies the two-dimensional line-contact
reduction, for which the stick radius follows the larger exponent
$c/a = (1-Q/\mu P)^{1/2}$; the axisymmetric $1/3$ form above is the spherical-contact reference.
The stick zone vanishes ($c\to0$) exactly when $Q$ reaches $\mu P$ and full sliding
begins. The tangential traction $q(r)$ superposes the full-sliding distribution on an
inner corrective term that cancels the slip displacement over the stick zone:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cattaneo-q}
  q(r) =
  \begin{cases}
    \mu p_0\sqrt{1-r^2/a^2}, & c\le r\le a,\\[4pt]
    \mu p_0\!\left[\sqrt{1-r^2/a^2}-\dfrac{c}{a}\sqrt{1-r^2/c^2}\,\right], & r\le c.
  \end{cases}
\end{equation}
In the slip annulus the traction saturates at the Coulomb limit $\mu p(r)$, while inside
the stick zone the corrective term keeps it below that limit. Integrating $q$ over the
contact and relating it to the tangential motion gives the compliance $\delta_x$, the
tangential displacement under load:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:cattaneo-delta}
  \delta_x = \frac{3\mu P}{16\,a\,G^\ast}\!\left[1-\left(1-\frac{Q}{\mu P}\right)^{2/3}\right],
  \qquad
  \frac{1}{G^\ast} = \frac{2-\nu_1}{4G_1}+\frac{2-\nu_2}{4G_2}.
\end{equation}
Here $G^\ast$ is the effective shear modulus, built from the shear moduli $G_1,G_2$ the
same way $E^\ast$ is built from the Young's moduli. The compliance is the frictional
counterpart of the normal force--approach law. It softens as $Q$ rises and the stick zone
gives way. We accept the numerical solution when $c/a$ agrees to $<1\%$, the integral
$\int q\,\mathrm{d}A = Q$ to $<1\%$, and $q(r)$ in \eqref{eq:cattaneo-q} agrees pointwise.

\subsection{Brazilian disc}\label{app:brazilian}

A disc of diameter $D=2R$ and thickness $t$ is compressed diametrally by a force $P$. The
solution superposes two Flamant load-point charts at the poles $(0,\pm R)$ on a uniform
biaxial tension $T=2P/\pi Dt$ that cancels the spurious traction the point loads leave on
the rim \citep{timoshenko1970elasticity}. At the disc center the two charts combine into a
tensile horizontal stress and a compressive vertical stress:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:brazilian-center}
  \sigma_{xx} = +\frac{2P}{\pi D t} \;\;(\text{tensile}),
  \qquad
  \sigma_{yy} = -\frac{6P}{\pi D t} \;\;(\text{compressive}),
\end{equation}
so the horizontal tension at the center is one third the vertical compression, and this
tension splits the disc. The splitting tensile strength inferred from a failure load $P_f$
is therefore $\sigma_t = 2P_f/\pi D t$ (ASTM~D3967). Vertical equilibrium across the
diametral section gives an independent balance the field must satisfy:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:brazilian-balance}
  \int_{-R}^{R}\sigma_{yy}(x,0)\,\mathrm{d}x = -\frac{P}{t},
\end{equation}
that is, the vertical stress integrated across the diameter carries the full applied load
per unit thickness. The field must also satisfy the admissibility conditions
$\grad\!\cdot\bm{\sigma}=\bm{0}$ and $\grad^2(\mathrm{tr}\,\bm{\sigma})=0$ away from the
poles. Along the loaded diameter, a finite load patch replaces the point load, and the
distributed-pressure (Hondros) form \citep{hondros1959evaluation} gives, for a contact
half-angle $2\alpha$ subtended by the patch at the center,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:hondros}
  \sigma_{xx}(0,y) = \frac{2P}{\pi D t\,\alpha}\!\left[\,
    \frac{(1-r^2/R^2)\sin 2\alpha}{1-2r^2\cos2\alpha/R^2+r^4/R^4}
    -\arctan\!\Big(\frac{1+r^2/R^2}{1-r^2/R^2}\tan\alpha\Big)\right],
\end{equation}
with $r=|y|$ the distance from the center along the loaded diameter. As the patch shrinks
($\alpha\to0$), \eqref{eq:hondros} reduces to the center value \eqref{eq:brazilian-center}
and removes the point-load singularity. We require the center stresses
\eqref{eq:brazilian-center} to $<1\%$ and the global balance \eqref{eq:brazilian-balance}
to $<0.5\%$. The diametral compliance under a true point load diverges logarithmically. A
finite contact half-width $b$ keeps it finite:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:brazilian-compliance}
  \Delta_D = \frac{4P}{\pi E t}\!\left[\ln\frac{2R}{b}-\frac{1-\nu}{2}\right],
\end{equation}
where $\Delta_D$ is the change in diameter along the load line. The prefactor and the
$-(1-\nu)/2$ term are robust, while the logarithmic argument carries the calibration of the
contact width $b$.

\paragraph{Chart-FEM verification (no contact detector).} The Brazilian disc is a pure stress boundary-value
problem with no contact detector engaged, so it certifies the chart-FEM stress recovery on a learned disc
geometry in isolation from the contact formulation of Section~\ref{sec:tmap}. The chart-FEM solve reproduces the
center stresses \eqref{eq:brazilian-center} to $1.62\%$ in $\sigma_{xx}$ and $0.58\%$ in $\sigma_{yy}$ against
the exact closed form. The constant-strain triangulation leaves a $\sim 1.6\%$ center floor that plateaus under
mesh refinement, a discretization error rather than a modeling error, and we exclude the pole singularity from
the comparison. With no detector engaged the chart contributes only the domain map, so the chart and the level
set are interchangeable here; the example certifies the solver, not the detector
(Figure~\ref{fig:brazilian-verif}).

\subsection{Nine-disc equibiaxial unit cell}\label{app:ninedisc}

A $3\times3$ square array of identical discs of radius $R$ is confined isotropically. No
diagonal contacts form, because the diagonal spacing $2\sqrt2\,R$ exceeds the contact
spacing $2R$. The $D_4$ symmetry of the lattice makes every contact carry the same normal
force $N$, while reflection parity keeps friction inactive. Each disc then sees four equal
inward loads $N$ at its north, south, east, and west poles, so the unit cell is the
superposition of two perpendicular Brazilian solutions, that is, four Flamant charts and
two uniform-tension charts. Adding the two perpendicular Brazilian fields makes the center
state an equibiaxial in-plane compression:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ninedisc-center}
  \sigma_{xx} = \sigma_{yy} = -\frac{2N}{\pi R t}, \qquad \sigma_{xy}=0,
\end{equation}
that is, equal compression in both directions and no shear. In the rigid-plate limit, the
numerical solution must reproduce this to $<1\%$ with an interior field identical across
the nine discs. The force--compression relation then closes the system through the
per-contact closing displacement $\Delta_x(N)$, the amount each contact shortens under
force $N$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ninedisc-force}
  3\,\Delta_x(N) = 2d, \qquad
  \Delta_x(N) = \frac{4N}{\pi E t}\ln\frac{2R}{b}
  + \frac{N}{\pi E t}\big[(2-\pi)\nu + \pi - 6\big],
  \qquad
  b = 2\sqrt{\frac{N R_{\rm rel}}{\pi E^\ast}},
\end{equation}
where $d$ is the boundary compression and $b$ the Hertz half-width of a disc--disc
contact; the factor of three counts the three contacts a load crosses along one row. For
$R=1.7$~mm, $E=100$~GPa, $\nu=0.3$, and $t=1$~mm, the relation gives
$N(d{=}10^{-4}\,\text{mm}) \approx 0.887$~N/mm. Equation~\eqref{eq:ninedisc-center} holds
exactly only for rigid confining plates. Deformable plates widen and soften the disc--plate
contacts by $\sqrt2$, so the interior discs differ by a few percent near their boundary
contacts, and any initial gaps make the contacts engage progressively. We record these
caveats with each numerical comparison. The two-body measure coupling of Appendix~\ref{app:ot} reaches the
equibiaxial state \eqref{eq:ninedisc-center} as a genuine $3\times3$ N-body optimal-transport contact in which
the per-pole load $N$ is an emergent output of twelve mutual couplings rather than an imposed pole load, matching
the centre mean stress to $0.58\%$ with a net force balance of $3.71\times10^{-15}$, in the rigid-outer-wall limit.

\section{Chart training and verification protocol}\label{app:training}

Every coordinate chart in this work is a small multilayer perceptron with a Fourier-encoded input. We train each one by the same regression recipe and admit it to a contact solve only after it passes a fixed set of acceptance checks. This appendix records the four chart families and their Fourier banks (Sec.~\ref{app:training:families}), the training recipe shared by all of them (Sec.~\ref{app:training:recipe}), and the verify-first acceptance thresholds that a chart must clear before it enters a solve (Sec.~\ref{app:training:verify}). The charts differ only in their domain and in which Fourier bank they carry. The optimizer, the network, and the schedule are the same for all four; the loss is mean-squared error for the height and decoder charts and mean-absolute error for the radial chart.

\subsection{Four chart families, three Fourier banks}\label{app:training:families}

A chart maps a low-dimensional parameter on the body's boundary to a coordinate in physical space; the volumetric decoder maps a parameter on the reference cube instead. Three of the four charts are surface charts. The fourth is a boundary-fitted decoder $\xbf=D(\bm\xi)$ that carries a rough relief on one face of a deformable block, which the chart-FEM of Sec.~\ref{app:training:verify} then deforms. Each chart prepends a \emph{fixed}, non-trainable bank of sinusoidal features to its input before the MLP. We first normalize the input to $\tilde x\in[-1,1]^d$, where $d$ is the input dimension, so that a frequency means ``cycles across the domain'' rather than per physical unit. With that normalization the encoding reads

\begin{equation}\label{eq:appB-fourier}
\gamma(\tilde x)=\big[\cos(2\pi\,\tilde x B^{\!\top}),\ \sin(2\pi\,\tilde x B^{\!\top})\big]\in\R^{2K},
\qquad
h_\theta(x)=\mathrm{base}+\mathrm{MLP}_\theta\!\big(\gamma(\tilde x)\big),
\end{equation}

where $B\in\R^{K\times d}$ is the frequency bank, $K$ is the number of features, and $\mathrm{base}$ is a single learnable scalar. The scalar $\mathrm{base}$ carries the mean of the target, so the network regresses only the deviation about that offset. The bank $B$ is a registered buffer: the optimizer never updates it. Only the MLP weights $\theta$ and the scalar $\mathrm{base}$ are trained. This fixed encoding carries the method, as Sec.~\ref{sec:fourier} establishes. A chart of the raw coordinate inherits the same spectral bias as an ambient neural SDF and smooths the geometry in the same way. The bank supplies the high frequencies that the raw network suppresses.

The three banks differ in how they tile frequency space, each in the manner appropriate to its chart's domain.

\begin{itemize}
\item \emph{Geometric} (deterministic), for the 1-D height chart on the open interval. The frequencies are spaced geometrically, $B=\operatorname{geomspace}(0.5,f_{\max},K)$, which gives equal coverage per octave of scale; the scalar $\mathrm{base}$ carries the DC component. A self-affine rock profile sampled at spacing $\Delta x$ over a length $L$ contains on the order of $L/\Delta x\approx 3000$ resolvable wavelengths, so we set $f_{\max}$ to that count of cycles across the normalized domain.
\item \emph{Gaussian random}, for the 2-D height field and the 3-D rough decoder. The rows of $B$ are drawn $B_i\sim\mathcal N(0,\sigma^2 I)$, which induces an isotropic, stationary kernel that stays flat out to a bandwidth set by $\sigma$ (Sec.~\ref{sec:fourier}). The decoder draws its relief bank from the same generator at a capped amplitude (see Sec.~\ref{app:training:verify}).
\item \emph{Harmonic}, for the 2-D radial chart on $\Sph^1$. The bank is the integer harmonics $[\cos(k\psi),\sin(k\psi)]_{k=1,\dots,K}$, which is \emph{periodic by construction}. Periodicity is mandatory here: the radius $\rho(\psi)$ must close up at $\psi$ and $\psi+2\pi$, so a non-periodic bank is inadmissible on the circle. The radial chart also wraps its output in a softplus, $\rho=\mathrm{softplus}(\mathrm{base}+\Delta)$, to keep $\rho>0$.
\end{itemize}

Table~\ref{tab:appB-charts} collects the four families, their domains and outputs, and the bank parameters used in production. The 1-D and 2-D height charts share the width-128, depth-4 MLP of Sec.~\ref{app:training:recipe}. \subsection{Common training recipe}\label{app:training:recipe}

We fit all four charts by minibatch regression onto sampled surface points. The training data are the measured or analytic target $(x_i,z_i)$: the topography map for a height chart, the analytic radius $\rho(\psi_i)$ for the radial chart, and the relief target on the rough face for the decoder. For the two height charts and the decoder the objective is the mean-squared error between the chart's prediction and that target,

\begin{equation}\label{eq:appB-mse}
\mathcal L(\theta,\mathrm{base})=\frac{1}{\abs{\mathcal B}}\sum_{i\in\mathcal B}
\big(h_\theta(x_i)-z_i\big)^2,
\end{equation}

evaluated on a random minibatch $\mathcal B$. The superformula radial chart instead minimizes the mean-absolute radius error $\frac{1}{\abs{\mathcal B}}\sum_i\abs{\rho_\theta(\psi_i)-\rho(\psi_i)}$, which is less sensitive to the cusped lobe tips; the remaining choices below are identical for all four families.

\begin{enumerate}
\item \emph{Input normalization.} We map the raw coordinate affinely to $\tilde x=2(x-x_{\mathrm{lo}})/(x_{\mathrm{hi}}-x_{\mathrm{lo}})-1\in[-1,1]^d$, so that the bank's frequencies mean ``cycles across the domain'' independent of physical units.
\item \emph{Fixed Fourier bank.} We build the bank $B$ of Eq.~\eqref{eq:appB-fourier} once at construction and register it as a buffer; the optimizer never sees it.
\item \emph{Network.} A $\tanh$ MLP with Xavier initialization: width 128, depth 4 for the two height charts; width 96, depth 3 for the radial chart; width 64, depth 3 for the volumetric decoder. The input dimension is $2K$.
\item \emph{Learnable base.} We initialize the scalar $\mathrm{base}$ to the mean of the target and train it jointly with $\theta$, so the network regresses the deviation about a learned offset.
\item \emph{Optimizer and schedule.} Adam at learning rate $2\times10^{-3}$, with a cosine-annealing schedule that decays the rate to zero over the iteration budget. The minibatch sizes are 4096 (height), 8192 (surface), and 2048 (decoder).
\item \emph{Precision and seed.} Double precision with a fixed random seed. The networks are small, and double precision yields better-conditioned chart Jacobians and inverses for the downstream pushforward and gap evaluation.
\end{enumerate}

A plain regression with the fixed encoding suffices: there is no loss curriculum, no adversarial term, and no schedule of weights. The four trainers are structurally identical and differ only in the bank they construct.

\subsection{Verify-first acceptance thresholds}\label{app:training:verify}

A trained chart is geometry, and a defective chart silently corrupts every downstream solve. We therefore require each chart to pass acceptance checks before we admit it to a contact computation. The surface charts run the reconstruction check. The volumetric decoder runs all three, in the order below. If any check fails, we reject the chart rather than patch it.

\paragraph{(1) Reconstruction fidelity.} The reconstruction RMSE measures how closely the chart reproduces the sampled surface; we report it as a fraction of the surface RMS and require it below a few percent. On the rough decoder target the Fourier decoder reaches $2.2\%$ of RMS, the same network with the bank disabled plateaus at $48\%$, and an ambient three-dimensional neural signed-distance function of the same surface reaches $15.7\%$. This measured separation is the quantity that justifies the chart over the level set.

\paragraph{(2) No element foldover, $\det J>0$.} A folded chart has no admissible pushforward, so for the volumetric decoder the chart-Jacobian determinant must be strictly positive on every element of the reference mesh before any contact is attempted. The relief amplitude is capped so that the third column of the Jacobian, $\nbf+\tfrac12\,(\mathrm{relief})\,\hat\nbf$, keeps a positive determinant, and the geometric acceptance check verifies a positive $\det J_{\min}$ over the mesh.

\paragraph{(3) Discretization convergence (MMS $O(h^2)$).} The trained geometry must not degrade the solver's accuracy, so on the curved decoder geometry a manufactured-solution study must recover the second-order spatial rate of the underlying P1 chart-FEM. The measured rates on the rough geometry are $2.07$ and $1.83$, consistent with the $O(h^2)$ target of the P1 element.

These thresholds make the geometric representation falsifiable. A chart whose cutoff is too low under-resolves the asperities: spectral smoothing returns, and check (1) catches it. A chart whose relief amplitude is too high folds an element, and check (2) catches that. Only a chart that passes all applicable checks enters a contact solve.

\section{The optimal-transport measure coupling and the two-body contact tangent}\label{app:ot}

This appendix supplies the optimal-transport backbone of the transition-map contact model of
Section~\ref{sec:tmap}: the boundary measures, the Brenier map and its two limits, the discrete measure coupling
and its identity with a mortar assembly, and the symmetric four-block consistent tangent that solves the
two-body deformable--deformable problem.

\subsection{The measure-coupling gap and the Brenier map}\label{app:ot:gap}

Each boundary chart carries a measure. For a single-valued face $z=h_X(x)$ the pushforward of Lebesgue measure
on the parameter line is the arclength measure
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-measure}
d\mu_X=(\map{X})_\#\,dx=\sqrt{1+\abs{h_X'(x)}^2}\,dx ,
\end{equation}
the object the contact pairing couples between two bodies. The coupling matches the \emph{normalised} cumulative
measures $\hat F_X=F_X/F_X(\theta_{\max})$, so the two marginals carry equal total mass even when the two faces
have unequal arclength; this is the discrete renormalisation the code applies
(\code{coupling.py}, $\hat F=F/F[-1]$) and the condition under which $\chi_\#\hat\mu_A=\hat\mu_B$ is admissible,
where $\hat\mu_X=\mu_X/\mu_X(\Gamma_c^X)$ are the normalised (probability) marginals. (The unnormalised arclength
measures of two faces of unequal length carry unequal total mass and admit no measure-preserving map; the
renormalisation is what makes the pushforward identity well posed, which is why \citet{brenier1991polar} and
\citet{mccann1995existence} state existence for probability measures.) A correspondence
$\chi:\Gamma_c^A\to\Gamma_c^B$ is \emph{admissible} when it transports the normalised slave measure onto the
normalised master measure, $\chi_\#\hat\mu_A=\hat\mu_B$. Among admissible maps the Monge problem selects the one
minimising a transport cost,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-monge}
\tmap=\arg\min_{\chi_\#\hat\mu_A=\hat\mu_B}\int_{\Gamma_c^A} c\big(\xbf,\chi(\xbf)\big)\,d\hat\mu_A(\xbf),\qquad
c(\xbf,\ybf)=\tfrac12\norm{\xbf-\ybf}^2 .
\end{equation}
The Monge problem \eqref{eq:ot-monge} can be infeasible---no map need carry an atom of $\hat\mu_A$ to a spread
of $\hat\mu_B$---so existence is established through its convex Kantorovich relaxation, which minimises over
transport plans $\pi\in\Pi(\hat\mu_A,\hat\mu_B)$ (couplings on $\Gamma_c^A\times\Gamma_c^B$ with the prescribed
marginals) rather than over maps,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-kantorovich}
\inf_{\pi\in\Pi(\hat\mu_A,\hat\mu_B)}\ \int_{\Gamma_c^A\times\Gamma_c^B}\!\! c(\xbf,\ybf)\,d\pi(\xbf,\ybf),\qquad
\Pi(\hat\mu_A,\hat\mu_B)=\big\{\pi\ge0:\ (P_A)_\#\pi=\hat\mu_A,\ (P_B)_\#\pi=\hat\mu_B\big\},
\end{equation}
with $P_A,P_B$ the coordinate projections \citep{villani2009optimal,santambrogio2015optimal}. The optimiser is
characterised geometrically: the support of an optimal plan is $c$-cyclically monotone, meaning that for every
finite cycle of matched pairs $\{(\xbf_i,\ybf_i)\}_{i=1}^{N}\subset\operatorname{supp}\pi$ no rematching lowers
the total cost,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-cyclical}
\sum_{i=1}^{N} c(\xbf_i,\ybf_i)\ \le\ \sum_{i=1}^{N} c\big(\xbf_i,\ybf_{\sigma(i)}\big)
\qquad\text{for every permutation }\sigma ,
\end{equation}
the transport analogue of the rearrangement inequality \citep{villani2009optimal,santambrogio2015optimal}. For
the quadratic cost and an absolutely continuous slave marginal the relaxation \eqref{eq:ot-kantorovich} is tight:
the optimal plan is supported on the graph of a map, $\pi=(\mathrm{id}\times\tmap)_\#\hat\mu_A$, so the Monge and
Kantorovich values coincide and \eqref{eq:ot-cyclical} reduces to monotonicity of the correspondence,
$\big(\tmap(\xbf_1)-\tmap(\xbf_2)\big)\cdot(\xbf_1-\xbf_2)\ge0$. On the one-dimensional contact parameter this is
exactly the order-preserving (no-crossing) property of the conforming arclength rearrangement
\eqref{eq:ot-brenier-1d}; it is the structure that the partial-support restriction below \emph{forfeits} when it
drops to a sub-arc, not the cause of the smearing it avoids. For the quadratic cost \citet{brenier1991polar}
proves the minimiser is unique and is the gradient of a convex
potential $\varphi$, $\tmap=\grad\varphi$, provided $\hat\mu_A$ is absolutely continuous with respect to Lebesgue
measure on the parameter line (here guaranteed by the strictly positive arclength density
$\sqrt{1+\abs{h_A'}^2}\ge1>0$, so $\hat F_A$ is a strictly increasing homeomorphism and $\hat F_A^{-1}$ exists,
which is exactly the absolute-continuity hypothesis the theorem requires)
\citep{villani2009optimal,santambrogio2015optimal,peyre2019computational}. In one parameter dimension the explicit
monotone-rearrangement form $\tmap=\hat F_B^{-1}\circ\hat F_A$ of the quadratic-cost Brenier map is classical
\citep[Thm.~2.5]{santambrogio2015optimal}\citep[Thm.~2.18]{villani2003topics}\citep{mccann1995existence}, writing
$F_X$ for the cumulative arclength and $\hat F_X$ for its normalisation by the total mass,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-brenier-1d}
\tmap=\hat F_B^{-1}\circ\hat F_A,\qquad \hat F_X(\theta)=\frac{1}{F_X(\theta_{\max})}\int_{\theta_{\min}}^{\theta}d\mu_X,\qquad
\hat F_B\big(\tmap(\theta_A)\big)=\hat F_A(\theta_A),
\end{equation}
so the transported point carries the same \emph{normalised} cumulative-arclength quantile. The renormalisation by
the in general unequal total masses $F_A(\theta_{\max})\neq F_B(\theta_{\max})$ is exactly what makes the
pushforward $\chi_\#\hat\mu_A=\hat\mu_B$ admissible (Section~\ref{sec:tmap:ot}): without it the composition would
query $F_B^{-1}$ at a quantile up to $F_A(\theta_{\max})$ outside $F_B$'s range. This is the map
$\code{MonotoneCoupling1D}$ evaluates ($\hat F=F/F[-1]$).\footnote{The quantile/marginal identity
$\hat F_B(\tmap(x))=\hat F_A(x)$ characterising the one-dimensional monotone map $\tmap=\hat F_B^{-1}\!\circ\hat F_A$ (computed to
machine precision in \code{MonotoneCoupling1D}) is machine-checked in Lean~4
(\code{lean/OTContact/BrenierProposed.lean}, \code{quantile\_identity}, axiom-free). The measure-theoretic Brenier
existence/uniqueness and the pushforward $(\tmap)_\#\mu_A=\mu_B$ are stated formally with a documented \code{sorry}
and a prose proof (\code{brenier\_existence\_uniqueness\_proposed}, \code{monotone\_map\_pushes\_forward\_proposed});
these require mathlib measure theory and are labelled proposed, not machine-checked.} The signed gap is the normal
projection of the displacement to the transported point,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-gap}
\gn(\xbf)=\big(\xbf-\tmap(\xbf)\big)\!\cdot\!\nbf ,
\end{equation}
with $\nbf$ the true surface normal of \eqref{eq:jacobian-normal}; \eqref{eq:ot-gap} is the primary definition,
valid in both regimes. In the conforming regime the correspondence itself is a gradient map \emph{on the
parameter coordinate}: $\tmap=\varphi'$ with $\varphi$ the convex Brenier potential on the one-dimensional slave
parameter line $\Gamma_c^A$ (the Kantorovich--Brenier link for the quadratic cost,
\citet[Prop.~1.21]{santambrogio2015optimal}), so the parameter-to-parameter rearrangement
$\theta_A\mapsto\psi_B=\varphi'(\theta_A)$ is monotone and conservative and the contact pairing carries no
circulation. The signed gap \eqref{eq:ot-gap}, by contrast, is read in physical space: $\xbf\in\R^2$ is the slave
point, $\tmap(\xbf)=\map{B}(\psi_B)\in\R^2$ its transported foot, and $\xbf-\tmap(\xbf)$ a physical displacement
whose normal projection is $\gn$. The energy consistency of the \emph{traction} is the matched-normal property
$\grad_{\xbf}\gn=\nbf$ of Remark~\ref{prop:matched-normal} (the chart normal is the unit gap gradient), not a
claim that the physical displacement $\xbf-\tmap(\xbf)\in\R^2$ is itself the gradient of a scalar field on
$\R^2$. In the partial/closest-point regime $\tmap=\pi_B$ is a coupling rather than a gradient map---$\pi_B$ is
many-to-one across the medial axis, hence not even on the parameter line almost everywhere a single-valued
monotone rearrangement---and \eqref{eq:ot-gap} remains the definition. Either way the measure-coupling gap is
unbiased, unlike the single-body radial gap of Remark~\ref{prop:active-set} whose magnitude is enlarged by
$1/\cos\alpha$.

\subsection{The two limits: closest-point projection and arclength rearrangement}\label{app:ot:limits}

The same coupling has two admissible regimes selected by the support of the contact, and the closest-point
projection is not the opposite of the transition map but the coupling admissible under partial support.

\begin{remark}[Two admissible regimes]\label{rem:ot-limits}
Let $\Gamma_c^A$ be a slave arc and $\Gamma_c^B$ a master arc.
\emph{(i) Conforming limit.} When the two arcs are mated and fully in contact (matching supports), the unique
quadratic-cost Brenier map is the arclength-monotone rearrangement $\tmap=\hat F_B^{-1}\circ\hat F_A$ of
\eqref{eq:ot-brenier-1d}.
\emph{(ii) Partial-support limit.} When only a sub-arc of the slave touches a localised convex master, the global
rearrangement is no longer admissible: it forces every slave point onto a master partner and smears the load
across the open part of the interface, mislocating a central slave node onto the indenter edge. The remedy is to
relax the hard marginal constraint of the Kantorovich problem~\eqref{eq:ot-kantorovich}, which demands that
\emph{all} of $\hat\mu_A$ be transported, to the unbalanced/partial-transport problem
\citep{chizat2018unbalanced} that penalises rather than enforces the marginals,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-unbalanced}
\inf_{\pi\ge0}\ \int_{\Gamma_c^A\times\Gamma_c^B}\!\! c(\xbf,\ybf)\,d\pi
+\lambda\,\mathrm{KL}\!\big((P_A)_\#\pi\,\|\,\hat\mu_A\big)
+\lambda\,\mathrm{KL}\!\big((P_B)_\#\pi\,\|\,\hat\mu_B\big),
\end{equation}
with $\mathrm{KL}$ the Kullback--Leibler divergence and $\lambda>0$ the marginal-relaxation weight; as $\lambda\to\infty$
the penalties recover the hard marginals of~\eqref{eq:ot-kantorovich}, while a finite $\lambda$ lets the optimal
plan create or destroy mass and so leave the untouched part of the open interface unmatched. The minimiser of
\eqref{eq:ot-unbalanced} therefore concentrates its support on the touching part of the interface, and for a
convex master its restriction is the closest-point projection $\pi_B=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$ with gap
$\gn=\norm{\bm p-\bm c}-R$, $\bm p$ the slave point, $\bm c$ the indenter centre, $R$ its radius. The two are the
conforming and partial-support limits of one measure coupling, not opposites. The restriction is well posed
under two hypotheses: the master is convex, so its metric projection $\pi_B$ is single-valued for every slave
point off the master's medial axis \citep[\S1.3]{santambrogio2015optimal}, and the slave face is a
single-valued graph $z=h_A(x)$, so the slave parameter $x$ indexes the contact arc without overhang; the
restricted quadratic-cost minimiser is then unique and coincides with $\pi_B$ on the touching support. The
code realises this support screen discretely: $\code{ClosestPointCoupling1D}$ zeroes the coupling mass outside the
contact band (the finite-$\lambda$ analogue), so a central slave node carries no reaction
(Figure~\ref{fig:ot-unbalanced}).
\end{remark}

\begin{figure}[t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fig_ot_section_loop6}
  \caption{The partial-support limit as an \emph{unbalanced} optimal-transport problem
  \eqref{eq:ot-unbalanced}. (a)~Under a hard marginal $\chi_{\#}\hat\mu_A=\hat\mu_B$ the balanced map is
  inadmissible for a localised convex indenter: it forces every slave point onto a master partner and smears the
  untouched flanks onto the indenter edge (red). (b)~Relaxing the marginals with the Kullback--Leibler penalties
  $\lambda\,\mathrm{KL}((P_A)_{\#}\pi\,\|\,\hat\mu_A)+\lambda\,\mathrm{KL}((P_B)_{\#}\pi\,\|\,\hat\mu_B)$ lets the
  optimal plan create or destroy mass; at finite $\lambda$ its support collapses onto the touching band, the open
  interface is left unmatched, and the restriction is the closest-point projection $\pi_B$ with gap
  $\gn=\norm{\bm p-\bm c}-R$. (c)~The restriction is well posed off the medial axis: for a convex master the metric
  projection is single-valued at every slave point (lower), whereas at a re-entrant feature the medial axis
  (purple) carries query points with two equidistant feet, where $\pi_B$ is multivalued (upper). The chart
  inversion $\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$ never forms that axis. Schematic; no benchmark quantity is plotted.}

Compactly,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-twolimits}
\tmap(\xbf)=
\begin{cases}
\big(\hat F_B^{-1}\circ\hat F_A\big)(\xbf)\ (\text{arclength-monotone}), & \text{conforming / rough interface},\\[3pt]
\pi_B(\xbf)=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}\ (\text{closest point}), & \text{partial-support / convex master},
\end{cases}
\qquad \gn=\norm{\bm p-\bm c}-R\ (\text{partial limit}).
\end{equation}
The partial-support restriction is realised in the code by the contact-band mass screen of
\code{ClosestPointCoupling1D} (mass $=0$ outside the touching support; \code{coupling.py}). The arclength-monotone
limit is the correct map for two mated rough profiles, where every slave arc has a conforming master partner; it
is the map used for the conforming patch test and the rock-joint interface, and the closest-point map drives the
localised seat, slide, and convergence of the deformable-Hertz solve.

\subsection{The discrete measure coupling is a mortar assembly}\label{app:ot:discrete}

Discretising \eqref{eq:ot-gap} on $P_1$ surface elements with Gauss quadrature $\{(\xi_q,w_q)\}$, the consistent
nodal contact force is the traction field tested against the slave shape functions and scattered to the master
through the partition-of-unity host weights $(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-force}
\fbf_I^+=\sum_q w_q\,J\,N_I^+(\xi_q)\,\tbf(\xi_q),\qquad
\fbf_K^-=-\sum_q w_q\,J\,(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)(\xi_q)\,\tbf(\xi_q),\qquad
\tbf=t_N\nbf+\tbf_T,
\end{equation}
with $J$ the surface Jacobian. This is a mortar assembly: writing the slave force as $\fbf_I=\sum_J M_{IJ}\tbf_J$
recovers the consistent (non-lumped) interface mass on a $P_1$ segment of length $L$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-mortar-mass}
M_{IJ}=\int N_I N_J\,ds,\qquad
M=\frac{L}{6}\begin{bmatrix}2&1\\[1pt]1&2\end{bmatrix},
\end{equation}
whose lumped diagonal $M_{IJ}\to A_I\delta_{IJ}$ is the node-collocated tributary penalty of
\eqref{eq:contact-block}. The host weights along the correspondence form a partition of unity,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-marginal}
\sum_K\big(N_K^-\!\circ\chi\big)(\xi_q)\equiv1 ,
\end{equation}
because they are the $P_1$ shape functions of the master segment that hosts the foot $\chi(\xi_q)$. The
correspondence $\chi$ assigns each slave point a single master host, so that no master node is double-counted or
starved, and the host weights then distribute the reaction without leakage. The two roles are distinct: measure
preservation of $\chi$, the quantile identity~\eqref{eq:ot-brenier-1d}, controls \emph{where} the foot lands,
while the partition of unity~\eqref{eq:ot-marginal} controls \emph{how} the reaction is split once it lands, and
it is this latter property---not the optimal-transport mass marginal---that the patch test exercises.

\begin{proposition}[Exact transmission of a constant pressure]\label{prop:patch}
Let the slave traction be a constant normal pressure $\tbf=p\,\nbf$ and let each Gauss foot $\chi(\xi_q)$ lie in a
single master segment with $P_1$ host weights satisfying $\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)(\xi_q)=1$, $N_K^-\ge0$. Then
the assembled master reaction $\fbf_K^-$ of~\eqref{eq:ot-force} integrates the same traction field as the slave
force $\fbf_I^+$, and the net resultant vanishes, $\sum_I\fbf_I^+ + \sum_K\fbf_K^-=\bm0$; consequently a uniform
pressure transmits to a uniform stress on the receiving body independent of the two interface meshes.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
With $\tbf=p\,\nbf$ constant, the master sum of~\eqref{eq:ot-force} factors the pressure out of the Gauss sum,
$\sum_K\fbf_K^-=-\sum_q w_q J\big[\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)(\xi_q)\big]p\,\nbf=-\sum_q w_q J\,p\,\nbf$ by the
partition of unity~\eqref{eq:ot-marginal}, which is exactly $-\sum_I\fbf_I^+$ since the $P_1$ slave shape functions
also sum to one and the quadrature is Gauss-exact on $P_1$. Hence $\sum_I\fbf_I^++\sum_K\fbf_K^-=\bm0$. The
precondition is the partition of unity plus Gauss exactness, not the optimal-transport marginal.\qed
\end{proof}

Consequently the reaction force on the master integrates the same traction field the slave applies, so a constant
pressure transmits as a uniform stress across a non-matching interface to machine precision: the contact patch
test, measured at $1.4\times10^{-16}$, $67\times$ tighter than the node-lumped scheme, independent of the
interface mesh because \eqref{eq:ot-marginal} is mesh-independent.\footnote{The algebraic precondition of
Proposition~\ref{prop:patch}---that the $P_1$ weights $N_0=1-t,\,N_1=t$ satisfy $N_0+N_1=1$ with $N_0,N_1\in[0,1]$
for $t\in[0,1]$, and that the constant-pressure resultant is then $\sum_K\fbf_K^-=-\sum_I\fbf_I^+$---is
machine-checked in Lean~4 (\code{lean/OTContact/PartitionOfUnity.lean}, theorems \code{p1\_host\_weights},
\code{marginal\_two\_host}, and \code{patch\_test\_resultant}; sorry-free, depending only on
\code{propext}/\code{Quot.sound}).}

\subsection{The symmetric four-block consistent tangent}\label{app:ot:tangent}

When both surfaces are deformable the master point $\xbf_m(\chi)=\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)\,\xbf_K^-$ moves with
the master dofs, so the gap $\gn=(\xbf_s-\xbf_m)\!\cdot\!\nbf$ depends on slave and master dofs alike. The
variations are taken at \emph{frozen geometry}---the surface normal $\nbf$ and the correspondence $\chi$ (hence
the host segment and its weights $N_K^-\!\circ\chi$) are held at the current configuration, dropping both the
geometric $\partial\nbf$ term and the correspondence-drift term $\partial(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)/\partial\ubf$, exactly
as the one-body assembly of Appendix~\ref{app:linearization} freezes the projection foot:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ot-gapvar}
\frac{\partial\gn}{\partial\ubf_I^+}=N_I^+\,\nbf,\qquad
\frac{\partial\gn}{\partial\ubf_K^-}=-(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)\,\nbf .
\end{equation}
With the penalty traction $\tbf=\varepsilon_n\pos{-\gn}\nbf$, active where $\gn<0$, the consistent tangent is the
symmetric positive-semidefinite four-block operator
\begin{equation}\label{eq:twobody-tangent}
\begin{aligned}
\bm K_{ss}[I,J]&=+\varepsilon_n\!\sum_q w_q J\,N_I^+ N_J^+\,(\nbf\otimes\nbf),
&\bm K_{sm}[I,K]&=-\varepsilon_n\!\sum_q w_q J\,N_I^+(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)(\nbf\otimes\nbf),\\
\bm K_{ms}[K,J]&=\bm K_{sm}^{\top}=-\varepsilon_n\!\sum_q w_q J\,(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)N_J^+(\nbf\otimes\nbf),
&\bm K_{mm}[K,L]&=+\varepsilon_n\!\sum_q w_q J\,(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)(N_L^-\!\circ\chi)(\nbf\otimes\nbf),
\end{aligned}
\end{equation}
in which $\bm K_{ss}$ is the one-body block of Appendix~\ref{app:linearization}, $\bm K_{sm}=\bm K_{ms}^{\top}$ is
the slave--master coupling block the rigid-master suite never assembles, and $\bm K_{mm}$ the master--master
block.

\begin{proposition}[Symmetric positive-semidefinite two-body tangent]\label{prop:spsd}
At frozen geometry \eqref{eq:ot-gapvar} the active-set penalty tangent \eqref{eq:twobody-tangent} is symmetric,
$\bm K_{ms}=\bm K_{sm}^{\top}$, and positive semidefinite.
\end{proposition}
\begin{proof}
At each active Gauss point stack the four interpolation weights with their signs into
$\bm w=(N_I^+,\,-(N_K^-\!\circ\chi))$. The four-block contribution is then
$\bm K=\varepsilon_n\sum_q w_q J\,(\bm w\otimes\bm w)\otimes(\nbf\otimes\nbf)$, a non-negative-weighted sum of
rank-one outer products, hence symmetric and positive semidefinite; off the active set the block is zero. The
transpose coupling $\bm K_{ms}=\bm K_{sm}^{\top}$ is the off-diagonal of this outer product.
\end{proof}

This is the algebraic kernel that the assembly forms entry by entry (the signed double loop in
\code{two\_body.py}). The four blocks are checked against the analytic Jacobian $dR/du$ entry by entry by symbolic
differentiation of the frozen-geometry residual---the residual the Newton iteration linearises; $\nbf$ and $\chi$
are recomputed at each Newton step and frozen within the linear solve, so the analytic four-block tangent equals
the finite-difference Jacobian of that residual to $3.45\times10^{-11}$ (Table~\ref{tab:ot:gates}). The dropped
drift and curvature terms cost asymptotic quadratic convergence on curved, large-rotation contact but not
consistency, since the residual \eqref{eq:newton} carries the full geometry. Under a uniform translation
$\ubf_I^+=\ubf_K^-=\bm\delta$ the combined slave--master gap variation vanishes, because the slave shape
functions and the master host weights are \emph{each} a partition of unity,
$\sum_I N_I^+=\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)=1$, so the signed contraction
$\varepsilon\big(\sum_I N_I^+-\sum_K N_K^-\!\circ\chi\big)=0$ and the net contact force on a rigid-body
translation is null.\footnote{The algebraic core of Proposition~\ref{prop:spsd}---that the single-active block
$\bm K=\varepsilon(\bm a\otimes\bm a)$ is symmetric and positive semidefinite,
$\bm x^{\top}\bm K\bm x=\varepsilon(\bm a\cdot\bm x)^2\ge0$, and that the assembled coefficient
$\varepsilon\,(s_aw_a)(s_bw_b)$ is symmetric in $(a,b)$ so $\bm K_{ms}=\bm K_{sm}^{\top}$---is machine-checked in
Lean~4 (\code{lean/OTContact/TangentPSD.lean}, theorems \code{rank1\_psd}, \code{rank1\_form},
\code{block\_coeff\_symm}; sorry-free, depending only on \code{propext}/\code{Quot.sound}). The rigid-translation
invariance itself---that the signed slave-minus-master partition-of-unity coefficient is zero,
$\varepsilon d+(-(\varepsilon((d{-}k){+}k)))=0$, so a uniform translation leaves the gap and hence the contact
force unchanged---together with the patch resultant summed over the \emph{full} Gauss-foot list (not one foot)
is machine-checked in Lean~4 (\code{lean/OTContact/TranslationInvariance.lean}, theorems
\code{tangent\_translation\_null}, \code{rigid\_translation\_gap\_invariant}, \code{patch\_resultant\_list};
sorry-free, depending only on \code{propext}/\code{Quot.sound}).}

\subsection{Algorithm summary}

Algorithm~\ref{alg:detector} states the two procedures that distinguish this work from a level-set contact
solver: the transition-map detector, which returns the matched pair $(\gn,\nbf)$ of Section~\ref{sec:tmap}, and
the verify-first protocol, which admits a trained chart only after it passes the reconstruction, foldover, and
convergence checks of Appendix~\ref{app:training}. Everything downstream of the detector---the broad phase, the
force laws, the consistent tangent, and the discretisations of Section~\ref{sec:discr}---is shared with the
signed-distance path through the dispatch \eqref{eq:detector}.

\begin{algorithm}
\caption{Transition-map contact detection and the verify-first chart protocol.}
\label{alg:detector}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Procedure{Detect}{query point $\xbf$; chart; frame $(c_B,Q_B)$}
  \If{radial chart}
    \State $\bm d \gets Q_B^\top(\xbf-c_B)$;\quad $r\gets\norm{\bm d}$;\quad $\dhat\gets\bm d/r$
    \State $\gn \gets r-\rho_B(\dhat)$ \Comment{inverse-chart gap, Eq.~\eqref{eq:radial-gap}}
    \State $\grad_S\rho \gets (I-\dhat\dhat^\top)\,\grad_{\hat d}\rho_B$
    \State $\nbf\gets\widehat{Q_B(\dhat-\grad_S\rho/r)}$ \Comment{matched normal, Prop.~\ref{prop:matched-normal}}
  \Else \Comment{height chart}
    \State $\gn\gets z-h_\theta(x)$;\quad $\nbf\gets(-h_\theta'(x),1)/\sqrt{1+h_\theta'^2}$ \Comment{Eq.~\eqref{eq:height-gap}}
  \EndIf
  \State \Return $(\gn,\nbf)$ \Comment{$\gn<0$ flags penetration; consumed by Eq.~\eqref{eq:detector}}
\EndProcedure
\Statex
\Procedure{VerifyFirst}{trained chart}
  \State \textbf{reject} unless reconstruction RMSE $<$ a few percent of surface RMS
  \State \textbf{reject} unless $\det J>0$ on every element \Comment{no foldover}
  \State \textbf{reject} unless a manufactured-solution study recovers $O(h^2)$
  \State \Return admitted chart
\EndProcedure
\Statex
\Procedure{CoupleTwoBody}{slave surface $\partial\Omega_A$, master surface $\partial\Omega_B$}
  \State build the correspondence $\chi=\pi_B\circ\map{A}$ \Comment{closest-point or arclength-monotone OT limit}
  \For{each Gauss point $\xi_q$ on $\Gamma_c^A$}
    \State $\gn(\xi_q)\gets(\xbf_s(\xi_q)-\xbf_m(\chi(\xi_q)))\!\cdot\!\nbf$;\quad host weights $(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)(\xi_q)$, $\sum_K=1$
    \State traction $\tbf(\xi_q)\gets t_N\nbf+\tbf_T$ on the active set $\{\gn<0\}$
  \EndFor
  \State scatter $\fbf_I^+\!\gets+\!\sum_q w_q N_I^+\tbf$, $\fbf_K^-\!\gets-\!\sum_q w_q (N_K^-\!\circ\chi)\tbf$ \Comment{transpose-coupled via the OT marginal}
  \State assemble the symmetric four-block tangent $\bm K_{ss},\bm K_{sm}{=}\bm K_{ms}^{\top},\bm K_{mm}$, Eq.~\eqref{eq:twobody-tangent}
  \State \Return $(\fbf,\bm K_c)$ on the shared slave--master dof vector
\EndProcedure
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

Two points govern exact reproduction. The deformable rock-joint study reports stresses in a consistent unit
system with an uncalibrated modulus, so stresses scale linearly with it while the dimensionless apparent
friction and the geometry-set dilation do not. The two-block node-to-surface friction residual settles near
$4\%$ and does not shrink with mesh refinement, measuring the Coulomb non-smoothness of moving-master contact
rather than discretisation error, and it is the one quantity whose last digits depend on the contact-solver
tolerances. A third point governs the two-body solves of Appendix~\ref{app:ot}: the deformable-Hertz half-width
and peak pressure carry a $\sim3$--$6\%$ contact-edge floor that does not shrink under refinement (the infinite
Hertz edge-pressure slope resolved over one element), whereas the non-matching patch test transmits a uniform
pressure to $1.4\times10^{-16}$ irrespective of the interface mesh, because the partition-of-unity marginal
$\sum_K(N_K^-\!\circ\chi)=1$ holds by construction.

\section{Supplementary material}\label{app:supp}

This appendix records three deferred studies of the rough rock joint of Section~\ref{sec:cv7}. Each
study isolates one question that the main text raises but does not develop. The first (S.1) asks whether
the transition-map detector survives being wired into the contact loop of an unsteady solve, rather than
only queried on a fixed point cloud. The second (S.2) asks whether the dilatant interface obeys the first
law over a full load cycle, with the energy ledger closed term by term. The third (S.3) asks whether an
independent explicit solver reproduces the same frictional response. We report each study with its
measured numbers and with the limit it exposes. The level set (a neural signed-distance function, or SDF)
runs throughout as the comparison baseline, on the same geometry and the same query set as the chart. All
three deferred studies are single-body solves against a rigid counterface; the two-body
deformable--deformable measure coupling, with its symmetric four-block tangent, is developed and verified
separately in Appendix~\ref{app:ot}, so this supplementary material is not the full optimal-transport
contribution.

\subsection{Transition-map detection inside the contact loop}\label{app:supp:p3}

The main text reports the chart and the level set as static geometry estimators, each queried on a fixed
grid. Here we instead let the transition-map detector supply the gap and the normal that drive a
quasi-static direct-shear solve, so the detector is evaluated inside the Newton loop. The chart reads
contact as the boundary-to-boundary map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\!\circ\map{A}$, which sends a point on one
face to the mating point on the other: for a query point on face $A$, the height chart returns the mating
surface height on face $B$ directly, and the gap then follows as the signed normal separation between the
two. The level set returns the same gap as the value of the ambient neural SDF $\phi$, with normal
$\nbf=\grad\phi/\norm{\grad\phi}$.

Throughout, $\gn$ denotes the signed normal gap, with the convention $\gn<0$ in penetration. The detector
declares a query point in contact when its gap is negative, so the active set is the index set of those
points,
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:supp_activeset}
\mathcal{A}=\{\,p : \gn(\xbf_p)<0\,\},
\end{equation}
%
where $p$ indexes the query points and $\xbf_p$ is the position of point $p$. To score the active set, we
compare it against the analytic active set $\mathcal{A}^{\star}$, which we obtain from the closed-form
mating surface. The score is the symmetric agreement
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:supp_agreement}
\eta=1-\frac{\abs{\mathcal{A}\,\triangle\,\mathcal{A}^{\star}}}{N},
\end{equation}
%
in which $\triangle$ is the symmetric difference (the points the two sets disagree on) and $N$ is the
number of query points; $\eta=1$ means the detector and the closed form agree on every point. We measure
gap accuracy separately, as the relative root-mean-square error of the predicted gap $\gn$ against the
analytic gap $\gn^{\star}$,
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:supp_gaprmse}
e_g=\frac{\norm{\gn-\gn^{\star}}_{2}}{\norm{\gn^{\star}}_{2}}.
\end{equation}
%
The gap error matters because the gap, not the active-set membership, sets the contact pressure.

On $N=10^{5}$ query points sampled over the joint footprint, the chart detector reproduced the analytic
active set to $\eta=98.9\%$, against $95.8\%$ for the level set. On the gap, the chart was sharper by a
wider margin: $e_g=4.2\%$ for the chart against $44.7\%$ for the level set, a factor of $10.5$ on the
quantity that sets the contact pressure. Wired into the FEM shear, the chart-driven solve tracked the
analytic-surface solve closely, reaching a peak apparent friction $\muapp=0.566$ against the analytic
$0.513$, with the shear traction agreeing to within $9.5\%$ over the loading history. Table~\ref{tab:supp_p3}
collects these results.

One quantity runs the other way. At the asperity tips the level set carries the sharper
normal, $0.8^{\circ}$ of deviation against $10.3^{\circ}$ for the chart, because the random-Fourier height
field has a noisier gradient at the tips than the smooth ambient field does. The chart advantage is on the
gap and the active set, the two quantities that decide where contact occurs and how hard, not on the
pointwise normal direction at the sharpest features.

The chart detector costs $1.47\times$ the level set per query. A chart query is a single forward
evaluation of $\map{A}$ followed by the chart read of $\map{B}^{-1}$, with no level-set root find or ray
march, because the mating height is the chart output. The factor below two is the overhead of evaluating
two Fourier-feature charts in place of one ambient SDF, and it stays constant as the number of query
points grows. The shear traction and the emergent normal dilation that this detector produces over the
loading history appear in Figure~\ref{fig:supp_traction}.

\subsection{Cyclic loading and the energy ledger}\label{app:supp:p4}

The cyclic study drives the joint through a triangle-wave tangential schedule under constant normal
displacement. It accounts for every term of the first law. The friction model uses a stateful
return-map update, so stick and slip alternate and the shear-traction response forms hysteresis loops.
The Plesha asperity-degradation parameter decays across cycles, from $0.3405$ to $0.3377$ over four
cycles, and the contact Newton residual averages $7\times10^{-5}$. The peak apparent friction is
$\abs{\muapp}\approx0.33$.

The first law states that the external work fed into the joint must reappear as stored energy plus
dissipation. With $\gn<0$ in penetration and slip taken as plastic, the ledger reads
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:supp_ledger}
W_{\mathrm{ext}}
= \Delta U_{\mathrm{el}}
+ W_{\mathrm{pen}}
+ W_{\mathrm{stick}}
+ W_{\mathrm{fric}},
\end{equation}
%
where each term has a physical meaning. The left side, $W_{\mathrm{ext}}=\sum \fbf_{\mathrm{contact}}\cdot
\mathrm{d}\ubf_{\mathrm{platen}}$, is the machine work the contact tractions do on the moving platen. On
the right, $\Delta U_{\mathrm{el}}=\tfrac12\ubf^{\!\top}\!K\ubf$ is the recoverable strain energy of the
blocks, $W_{\mathrm{pen}}=\tfrac12\sum\varepsilon_n A\,\gn^{2}$ is the recoverable normal penalty energy,
$W_{\mathrm{stick}}$ is the recoverable tangential spring energy of the stuck nodes, and
$W_{\mathrm{fric}}=\sum\abs{\tbf_T}\,\abs{\mathrm{d}\bm{s}_p}\ge0$ is the frictional dissipation
accumulated over the plastic slip increments; the tangential traction $\tbf_T$ entering it is the discrete
realisation of the optimal-transport traction field of Appendix~\ref{app:ot}, carried across the
correspondence under the same cone bound $\abs{\tbf_T}\le\mu\,t_N$, and the active set on which slip is
accumulated is the support of the transport plan, the zero-mass screen of unbalanced optimal transport
\citep{chizat2018unbalanced}. The three recoverable terms make up the moving part of the
balance over a cycle: each one returns to its start value once the load closes,
$\Delta U_{\mathrm{el}}\to0$, $W_{\mathrm{pen}}\to0$, $W_{\mathrm{stick}}\to0$. The per-cycle closure
therefore reduces to a single ratio that should equal one,
%
\begin{equation}\label{eq:supp_closure}
\Pi_{\mathrm{cyc}}
= \frac{W_{\mathrm{ext}}}
{\,W_{\mathrm{fric}}+\Delta U_{\mathrm{el}}+W_{\mathrm{pen}}+W_{\mathrm{stick}}\,}
\;\longrightarrow\; 1 .
\end{equation}
%
A value of $\Pi_{\mathrm{cyc}}=1$ means the work in matches the energy out, term for term, over the cycle.

Measured per cycle, $\Pi_{\mathrm{cyc}}=1.09$ on the first cycle and $1.01$ on the second, the latter
inside the acceptance band $[0.98,1.02]$. The first-cycle excess is the recoverable terms not yet having
returned to zero after a single traversal; by the second cycle they do return, and the ledger closes to
one percent. Figure~\ref{fig:supp_cyclic} shows the hysteresis loops and the per-cycle ledger.

The \emph{cumulative} ratio over the whole history is a different number, $0.17$, far from one. The
shear-only constant-normal machine work $W_{\mathrm{ext}}$ does not count the dissipation spent seating
the asperities under the initial normal load. The run begins with
$W_{\mathrm{fric}}[0]=0.91$ already accumulated while $W_{\mathrm{ext}}[0]=0$, because the normal-load
seating happens before the tangential schedule starts. That offset enters the denominator of the
cumulative ratio but never the numerator, so it depresses the ratio permanently. The offset is a
bookkeeping artifact, the unmeasured seating dissipation, not an energy violation. The per-cycle balance
of Eq.~\eqref{eq:supp_closure} differences the accumulated work across one full cycle and so cancels the
constant offset; that per-cycle balance is the correct first-law statement, and it closes.

\subsection{Explicit ChartMPM cross-check}\label{app:supp:p5}

The last study checks the frictional response with an independent solver. We discretize a deformable block
with the explicit finite-deformation ChartMPM (linear B-spline transfers, Neo-Hookean response) and shear
it against the \emph{same} band-limited rough surface that the chart-FEM uses, sliding the surface beneath
the block at a constant rate to impose direct-shear kinematics. The contact runs through the existing
per-particle channel: the gap comes from the geometric oracle, the normal force from the penalty model,
and the tangential force from regularised Coulomb friction, all scattered as physical-space per-particle
forces through the same particle-to-grid channel as gravity. The solver sees the floor as the analytic
SDF $\phi(\xbf)=z-h(x-u_x,y)$, where $h$ is the rough height field and $u_x$ is the imposed slide.

The explicit solve reproduced the Coulomb friction floor. A flat-bottomed block sliding on the rough floor
reached an emergent apparent friction $\muapp\approx0.34$ against the prescribed base $\mu=0.4$, within
about $14\%$, and converged stably under dynamic relaxation. This is an independent dynamic confirmation
of the frictional channel that the quasi-static FEM uses.

The explicit solve does not, however, confirm the dilatant interlock. The chart-FEM produces emergent
dilation and an apparent-friction enhancement above the base value because two rough faces ride over one
another. A \emph{mated} rough-on-rough block in the explicit solver, with the full surface in conformal
contact, is unstable in explicit penalty dynamics and diverges without an implicit or mortar contact
treatment. The cross-check therefore validates friction but not dilatancy. The explicit MPM is a consistent check of the Coulomb response, and the emergent dilatancy
of Section~\ref{sec:cv7} rests instead on the quasi-static implicit chart-FEM, whose consistent tangent
drives the contact Newton residual to $\sim10^{-9}$. An implicit or mortar contact treatment for the
mated MPM case is the documented path to a full dynamic cross-check of dilatancy.

\section{Linearisation of the contact contribution}\label{app:linearization}

The consistent tangent derived here is the slave--slave block $\bm K_{ss}$ of the general two-body
measure-coupling tangent of Appendix~\ref{app:ot}: it linearises the contact virtual work \eqref{eq:contact-vw}
against the slave displacement alone, the restriction $\partial\xbf_m=0$ that holds when the counterface is rigid.
When the counterface is itself deformable the same correspondence adds the master-coupling blocks
$\bm K_{sm}=\bm K_{ms}^{\top}$ and $\bm K_{mm}$ of \eqref{eq:twobody-tangent}; this appendix derives the
slave--slave block in full and Appendix~\ref{app:ot} supplies the master blocks.
The consistent tangent of the contact virtual work \eqref{eq:contact-vw} follows from linearising the contact
pressure and the gap variation. The penalty pressure and the gap variation linearise as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lin-pressure}
\Delta t_N=\varepsilon_n\,\Delta\gn,\qquad
\Delta\gn=\nbf\cdot(\Delta\ubf^B-\Delta\ubf^A),
\end{equation}
and the variation of the gap carries the rotation of the normal,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lin-gapvar}
\Delta(\delta\gn)=(\delta\ubf^B-\delta\ubf^A)\cdot\Delta\nbf
=(\delta\ubf^B-\delta\ubf^A)\cdot\frac{\partial\nbf}{\partial\ubf}\,(\Delta\ubf^B-\Delta\ubf^A).
\end{equation}
The normal contribution $\Delta t_N\,\delta\gn+t_N\,\Delta(\delta\gn)$ yields a penalty normal stiffness and a
curvature term,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lin-normal}
\bm K_N=\varepsilon_n A\,(\nbf\otimes\nbf)+t_N A\,\frac{\partial\nbf}{\partial\ubf},
\end{equation}
in which $\partial\nbf/\partial\ubf$ is the shape operator of \eqref{eq:shape-operator}. Written intrinsically on
the master chart---the second factor $\map{B}$ of the transition map $\tmap=\map{B}^{-1}\circ\map{A}$---the shape
operator is the pushforward of the surface's second fundamental form,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:weingarten-chart}
\frac{\partial\nbf}{\partial\ubf}=-\,\bm J_B^{t}\,\bm a^{-1}\bm b\,\bm a^{-1}\,\bm J_B^{t\top},\qquad
a_{\alpha\beta}=\tbf_\alpha\!\cdot\!\tbf_\beta,\quad
b_{\alpha\beta}=\nbf\!\cdot\!\frac{\partial^2\map{B}}{\partial\xi^\alpha\partial\xi^\beta},\quad
\tbf_\alpha=\frac{\partial\map{B}}{\partial\xi^\alpha},
\end{equation}
with $\bm J_B^{t}=[\tbf_1\,\tbf_2]$ the surface tangents (the master-chart Jacobian columns), $\bm a$ the first
fundamental form (metric) and $\bm b$ the second fundamental form (curvature) at the foot $\map{B}(\psi_B)$, and
$\bm a^{-1}\bm b$ the Weingarten map; the signed-distance Hessian of \eqref{eq:shape-operator} is its level-set
specialisation. For a three-dimensional height chart $z=h_\theta(x,y)$, $\bm a=\bm I_2+\grad h\otimes\grad h$ and
$\bm b=\mathrm{Hess}\,h/s$ with $s=\sqrt{1+\norm{\grad h}^2}$, generalising the two-dimensional graph curvature
already given in \eqref{eq:shape-operator}. Linearising the
regularised friction traction \eqref{eq:friction-reg} at the cone bound gives a friction--normal coupling and a
friction--tangent term,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:lin-friction}
\bm K_T=\mu\,\varepsilon_n A\,(\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}\otimes\nbf)
+\mu\,t_N A\,\frac{\partial\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}}{\partial\ubf},
\end{equation}
the first block non-symmetric, the structural signature of the non-associative friction law. With
$\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}=\bm P_T\vbf/\norm{\bm P_T\vbf}$ the projected slip direction and
$\bm P_T=\bm I-\nbf\otimes\nbf$ the tangential projector, so that $\bm P_T\vbf=\vbf_T$ of
\eqref{eq:friction-reg}, the friction--tangent factor follows by differentiating the projection,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:friction-dir-deriv}
\frac{\partial\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}}{\partial\ubf}
=-\frac{\bm I-\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}\otimes\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}}{\norm{\bm P_T\vbf}}
\Big[(\nbf\!\cdot\!\vbf)\,\frac{\partial\nbf}{\partial\ubf}
+\nbf\otimes\Big(\vbf^{\top}\frac{\partial\nbf}{\partial\ubf}\Big)\Big],
\end{equation}
so the friction--tangent block is governed by the same chart curvature \eqref{eq:weingarten-chart} as the normal
term. By default the
implementation assembles the penalty normal stiffness and the friction--normal coupling,
$\bm K^a_c=\varepsilon_n A(\nbf\otimes\nbf+\mu\,\tbf_{\mathrm{proj}}\otimes\nbf)$ of \eqref{eq:contact-block},
the smoother two-block tangent. The curvature \eqref{eq:weingarten-chart} and friction--tangent
\eqref{eq:friction-dir-deriv} blocks are available as an opt-in \emph{consistent} tangent, assembled per active
node as the exact Jacobian of the resolved contact force; this equals $\bm K_N+\bm K_T$ to leading order, needs
no third derivative of the boundary, and stays consistent with the $\varepsilon$-smoothed resolved normal. We
verified the closed form against that Jacobian---the curvature term \eqref{eq:weingarten-chart} matches the
finite-difference normal derivative to $5.6\times10^{-4}$---and measured its effect. On a smooth curved master
surface the consistent tangent restores the asymptotic quadratic Newton rate, converging in $8$ iterations
against $40$ for the two-block tangent frictionless and $11$ against $44$ under Coulomb friction, with the
converged solution unchanged. On the rough Coulomb joint, by contrast, completing the tangent does \emph{not}
remove the residual plateau: both tangents stall at the same few-percent floor and the emergent apparent
friction is invariant to within $0.3\%$. The plateau reported in Section~\ref{sec:discr} is therefore
the slip non-smoothness of the moving contact point, not the dropped tangent terms, and is cured by the
semismooth or augmented-Lagrangian treatments discussed there.

The two-body counterpart of this slave--slave block is the symmetric positive-semidefinite four-block
tangent of Appendix~\ref{app:ot}, in which the master blocks $\bm K_{sm}=\bm K_{ms}^{\top}$ and $\bm K_{mm}$
are added through the correspondence $\chi$. Its assembly is verified against the analytic Jacobian to all
$64$ entries by symbolic differentiation, against a finite-difference perturbation of the residual to
$3.45\times10^{-11}$, and against Newton's third law by a net force balance of $4.4\times10^{-16}$.
